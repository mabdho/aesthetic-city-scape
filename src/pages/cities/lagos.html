15 Best Things to Do in Lagos, Nigeria (2025 Guide)

Welcome to Lagos, the pulsating heart of Nigeria and Africa's most populous city. A sprawling metropolis of relentless energy, Lagos is a captivating blend of chaos and charm, where vibrant culture, booming commerce, and creative expression collide. From the historic avenues of Lagos Island to the trendy shores of Victoria Island, the city is a sensory overload in the best way possible. It's the birthplace of Afrobeats, the engine of Nollywood, and a hub for fashion and art. Navigating its infamous traffic is part of the adventure, leading you to serene nature reserves, bustling markets, and world-class art galleries. Whether you're here to dance the night away or delve into rich history, exploring the best things to do in Lagos promises an unforgettable journey into the spirit of modern Africa.

Complete Guide to Lagos Attractions

Lekki Conservation Centre

Escape the city's hustle at the Lekki Conservation Centre, a 78-hectare urban nature park. This sanctuary is a jewel of ecotourism and a testament to preserving Nigeria's rich biodiversity. The main attraction is the 401-metre long canopy walkway, the longest in Africa. Suspended high above the forest floor, it offers breathtaking panoramic views of the reserve and a thrilling perspective on the lush vegetation and wildlife below. Down on the ground, elevated wooden boardwalks meander through mangrove forests and swamp habitats. Keep an eye out for monkeys, crocodiles, and a spectacular variety of birdlife. The centre also features a family park with picnic areas, a fish pond, and a floor-level 'tree house', making it a perfect outing for nature lovers and adventure seekers alike.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the Lekki-Epe Expressway in the Lekki Peninsula. Best reached by a ride-sharing service like Uber or Bolt, or a private car.

Opening Hours: 8:30 AM - 5:00 PM daily

Cost: Entrance fee is approx. ₦2,000. Access to the canopy walk costs an additional ₦1,000.

Website: https://www.ncfnigeria.org/projects/lekki-conservation-centre

Nike Art Gallery

Prepare to be mesmerized by the Nike Art Gallery, a towering five-story building that is a treasure trove of Nigerian art. Owned and curated by the renowned artist and designer Nike Davies-Okundaye, the gallery showcases an astonishing collection of over 8,000 diverse works from a wide range of Nigerian artists. From contemporary paintings and sculptures to traditional textiles and beadwork, every corner of this space is filled with color, creativity, and storytelling. The gallery is more than just a place to view art; it's an immersive cultural experience. Visitors are free to wander through its packed halls, and you might even be lucky enough to meet Mama Nike herself, who is often on-site to share stories and insights. It's an essential stop for anyone interested in understanding the depth and vibrancy of Nigeria's art scene.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at 2 Elegushi Road, in Lekki Phase 1. Easily accessible via ride-sharing apps.

Opening Hours: 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM daily

Cost: Free entry, but purchasing art is encouraged.

Website: https://www.nikeartgallery.com/

Freedom Park

Freedom Park is a poignant memorial and leisure park built on the grounds of the former Her Majesty's Broad Street Prison. This historical site, where political activists who fought for Nigeria's independence were once imprisoned, has been transformed into a vibrant cultural hub. The park masterfully preserves elements of the old prison, with cell blocks and gallows stands now serving as artistic reminders of the nation's past. Today, it's a serene space for relaxation, live music, and cultural events. The park features fountains, ponds, an amphitheater, and food courts. It regularly hosts concerts, festivals, and art exhibitions, making it a lively center for Lagos's creative community. It’s a place of reflection, celebration, and a beautiful symbol of transformation from oppression to freedom.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Broad Street, Lagos Island. It's a central location, accessible by car, taxi, or BRT bus.

Opening Hours: 10:00 AM - 10:00 PM daily

Cost: A small entry fee of approx. ₦500 is required.

Website: https://freedomparklagos.com/

Kalakuta Republic Museum

Step into the world of a music legend at the Kalakuta Republic Museum, the last home of Fela Kuti, the pioneer of the Afrobeat genre and a fearless political activist. The museum preserves Fela's personal effects, instruments, and living space exactly as he left them. A guided tour offers intimate stories about his revolutionary life, his music, and his relentless fight against corruption and oppression. The building itself was declared an independent 'Kalakuta Republic' by Fela, a commune and recording studio. Visiting feels like stepping back in time, with his bedroom, clothes, and even his iconic saxophone on display. It's a raw, powerful, and deeply personal tribute to one of Africa's most influential figures.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at 7 Gbemisola Street, Allen, Ikeja. Best reached by ride-sharing services.

Opening Hours: 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM daily (Tues-Sun), Closed Mondays.

Cost: Entrance fee is approx. ₦5,000, which includes a guided tour.

Website: No official website, but widely covered on travel blogs.

The New Afrika Shrine

For an authentic Lagos nightlife experience, nothing beats The New Afrika Shrine. This open-air entertainment venue is the spiritual successor to Fela Kuti's original Afrika Shrine, which burned down in the 1970s. Managed by his children, Femi and Seun Kuti, the Shrine is the epicenter of contemporary Afrobeat music. The atmosphere is electric, charged with pulsating rhythms, energetic dancers, and a diverse crowd of locals and tourists. Catch a performance by Femi Kuti on Thursdays or Seun Kuti on the last Saturday of the month for an unforgettable night. Even on other nights, the house band keeps the energy high. It's more than a music venue; it’s a cultural institution where the spirit of Fela's music and activism lives on. Be prepared for a loud, smoky, and exhilarating experience.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at 1 NERDC Road, Agidingbi, Ikeja. Ride-sharing is the recommended mode of transport, especially at night.

Opening Hours: Opens in the evenings, typically from 6:00 PM until late. Best to check for specific event times.

Cost: Entry is usually free, but there's a cover charge of around ₦1,000 - ₦2,000 for major performances.

Website: No official website, but their schedule is on their social media pages.

Lekki Arts & Crafts Market (Jakande Market)

For souvenir hunting and a dose of local commerce, head to the Lekki Arts & Crafts Market, also known as Jakande Market. This vibrant, semi-open-air market is a labyrinth of stalls selling a vast array of Nigerian crafts. You can find everything from intricate wooden sculptures and bronze statues to colorful paintings, handcrafted leather goods, traditional masks, and beautiful textiles. Bargaining is part of the experience, so come prepared to negotiate prices with a friendly attitude. It’s an excellent place to interact with local artisans and find unique, authentic keepsakes to remember your trip. The sheer variety and craftsmanship on display make it a fascinating cultural visit, even if you don't plan on buying anything.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located off the Lekki-Epe Expressway, near the 5th roundabout. Accessible via ride-sharing apps.

Opening Hours: Approximately 9:00 AM - 6:00 PM daily.

Cost: Free entry. Prices for goods are negotiable.

Website: N/A

National Museum Lagos

Delve into Nigeria's rich history at the National Museum in Onikan. Though modest in size, the museum holds significant archaeological and ethnographic collections. Its galleries showcase artifacts from various Nigerian cultures, including the famous Benin City brasses, Nok terracotta heads, and intricate Igbo Ukwu bronze figures. These pieces provide a fascinating glimpse into the country's ancient civilizations and artistic heritage. The museum also features a sobering exhibit on the Nigerian civil war and a display of the bullet-riddled car in which former Head of State, General Murtala Muhammed, was assassinated in 1976. It’s a crucial stop for anyone looking to understand the historical context of modern Nigeria.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Onikan, Lagos Island, near Tafawa Balewa Square. Centrally located and accessible by car.

Opening Hours: 9:30 AM - 3:30 PM (Mon-Sat), Closed Sundays.

Cost: Entrance fee is approx. ₦500 for adults.

Website: http://www.nigerianmuseums.org/national-museum-lagos/

Tarkwa Bay Beach

Tarkwa Bay offers a more tranquil beach experience compared to its mainland counterparts. As it's only accessible by boat, the beach is sheltered and often less crowded. Its calm waters make it one of the few beaches in Lagos that is safe for swimming. It’s a popular spot for water sports enthusiasts, with opportunities for jet skiing and surfing. The vibe here is rustic and laid-back. You can rent chairs and umbrellas from local vendors, buy fresh coconuts, and enjoy grilled fish. The boat ride from Victoria Island offers fantastic views of the Lagos coastline and the bustling port. It's the perfect day trip to unwind and soak up the sun away from the city's frenzy.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take a boat or water taxi from Mekwe Jetty near Bonny Camp on Victoria Island or from the Fiki Marina on Lagos Island. The ride takes about 15-20 minutes.

Opening Hours: Daylight hours are recommended.

Cost: Boat rides cost between ₦3,000 - ₦7,000 for a round trip. There might be a small entry fee (approx. ₦500) at the beach.

Website: N/A

Balogun Market

Dive headfirst into the chaotic heart of Lagosian commerce at Balogun Market. This sprawling market on Lagos Island is one of the largest in West Africa, a dizzying maze of streets and alleys packed with vendors selling everything imaginable. The main draw is textiles, with an incredible selection of colorful Ankara fabrics, lace, and Aso-oke. You can also find shoes, bags, jewelry, and household goods. Visiting Balogun Market is not for the faint of heart. It is crowded, loud, and intense, but it offers an unparalleled, authentic glimpse into the daily life and economic engine of Lagos. Be prepared for a sensory overload and keep your belongings secure. For the adventurous traveler, it's an experience you won't forget.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Lagos Island. It's best to go with a local guide or be prepared for intense crowds. Ride-sharing can get you close, but you'll have to walk the final stretch.

Opening Hours: Roughly 8:00 AM - 5:00 PM (Mon-Sat). Avoid midday rush if possible.

Cost: Free to enter. Haggling is essential.

Website: N/A

Terra Kulture

Terra Kulture is a leading cultural center in Lagos, dedicated to promoting the richness and diversity of Nigerian languages, arts, and culture. This multi-functional space houses an art gallery with curated exhibitions, a bookstore with a fantastic selection of Nigerian literature, a craft shop, and a renowned restaurant serving authentic Nigerian cuisine from various ethnic groups. It is most famous for its theatre, which hosts world-class stage plays and musicals every weekend. It's the perfect one-stop shop for a comprehensive cultural immersion. Whether you're there to see a play, buy a book by a Nigerian author, or savor a delicious plate of jollof rice, Terra Kulture offers a sophisticated and welcoming atmosphere.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Plot 1376, Tiamiyu Savage Street, Victoria Island. Easily accessible by car or ride-sharing.

Opening Hours: 9:00 AM - 10:00 PM daily. Check their website for theatre showtimes.

Cost: Free to enter the building. Food, books, art, and theatre tickets have separate costs (theatre tickets approx. ₦5,000 - ₦10,000).

Website: https://www.terrakulture.com/

Elegushi Royal Beach

Elegushi Beach is one of the most popular private beaches in Lagos, known for its lively and party-like atmosphere, especially on weekends. It's a hub of activity with numerous waterfront bars and restaurants blasting Afrobeats music, offering grilled fish, suya, and cold drinks. The energy is infectious, making it a great place to socialize and experience the city's vibrant beach culture. The beach is owned by the Elegushi royal family. While the water can be rough and not always suitable for swimming, the main draw is the social scene. It's the place to see and be seen, enjoy horseback riding along the shore, and dance as the sun sets. Go on a weekday for a quieter experience or dive into the weekend chaos for a true Lagos party.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the Lekki-Epe Expressway. Accessible by ride-sharing services.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7, but best visited during the day and early evening.

Cost: Entrance fee is approx. ₦2,000 per person. Parking has an additional fee.

Website: N/A

Hi-Impact Planet Amusement Park

Located just outside the main city hub, Hi-Impact Planet is a world-class amusement park and a fantastic destination for families and thrill-seekers. The park boasts a wide range of rides and attractions, from gentle carousels and a Ferris wheel for younger children to high-octane roller coasters and pendulum rides for adrenaline junkies. It also features an indoor section with arcade games, a 4D cinema, and other interactive experiences. The park is well-maintained and offers a clean, safe environment for a full day of fun. With various food courts and souvenir shops on-site, it provides a complete entertainment package. It's a great way to take a break from the urban intensity of Lagos and enjoy some classic theme park excitement.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the Lagos-Ibadan Expressway, a bit of a drive from the city center. Best reached by private car or a dedicated taxi hire for the day.

Opening Hours: 12:00 PM - 5:00 PM (Tues-Fri), 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM (Sat-Sun), Closed Mondays.

Cost: Various ticket packages are available, typically starting from ₦15,000 for access to a set number of rides.

Website: https://www.hi-impactplanet.com/

Oniru Beach

Oniru Private Beach offers a more upscale and curated beach experience compared to its public counterparts. It's known for its clean shoreline, well-organized setting, and a plethora of trendy beach clubs and lounges. This is the spot for enjoying gourmet food, sipping cocktails, and relaxing on comfortable sunbeds while listening to curated DJ sets. The beach attracts a stylish crowd and is a popular venue for events and concerts. While it still has that energetic Lagos vibe, the atmosphere is generally more relaxed and controlled than at Elegushi. If you're looking for a comfortable and chic beach day with all the amenities, Oniru is an excellent choice.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in Oniru, Victoria Island, near the Landmark Centre. Easily accessible.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7, but individual establishments have their own hours.

Cost: Entrance fee is around ₦1,000 - ₦2,000. Food and drinks at the clubs can be pricey.

Website: N/A

Tafawa Balewa Square (TBS)

Tafawa Balewa Square is a monumental plaza on Lagos Island, built to commemorate Nigeria's independence in 1960. The square's entrance features four massive white horses and seven red eagles, symbols from the national coat of arms representing strength and dignity. The sprawling 14.5-hectare site was once a racecourse and now serves as a venue for major national celebrations, religious gatherings, and commercial events. While often used for large-scale events, visitors can appreciate its grand architecture and historical significance on any given day. The square is surrounded by important buildings, including the former Nigerian Parliament building and the National Museum. A stroll through TBS is a walk through a key chapter of Nigerian history.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Lagos Island, it is very central and a well-known landmark.

Opening Hours: Open to the public, but access might be restricted during events.

Cost: Free to visit the exterior.

Website: N/A

Cathedral Church of Christ, Marina

An architectural masterpiece and a haven of peace, the Cathedral Church of Christ on Lagos Island is the oldest Anglican cathedral in the Church of Nigeria. Construction of this stunning Gothic-style building began in 1925, and its design is a beautiful example of Norman architecture. The cathedral is known for its impressive stained-glass windows, imposing arches, and serene atmosphere. Visitors are welcome to admire the architecture and find a moment of tranquility away from the city's noise. The church also houses a fine pipe organ, and attending a service here can be a moving experience, filled with powerful hymns and music. It stands as a historic and spiritual landmark in the heart of old Lagos.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Marina Road, Lagos Island, easily visible and accessible.

Opening Hours: Open daily for visitors and services. Be respectful of worship times.

Cost: Free entry. Donations are welcome.

Website: http://www.cathedrallagos.org/

Planning Your Visit to Lagos

Getting Around

Navigating Lagos is an adventure. Ride-sharing apps like Uber and Bolt are the most convenient and relatively safe options for tourists. For shorter distances, 'Keke Napep' (motorized tricycles) are common. The iconic yellow 'Danfo' buses are the cheapest way to get around but can be crowded and chaotic, recommended only for the adventurous. The city's BRT (Bus Rapid Transit) system offers a more structured bus service on dedicated lanes.

Where to Stay

Victoria Island (VI) is the commercial hub, bustling with restaurants, bars, and hotels catering to business and leisure travelers. Ikoyi is a more upscale, residential area with luxury hotels and serene streets. The Lekki Peninsula is a modern, rapidly growing area popular with expats and younger crowds, offering a mix of hotels and serviced apartments. For a more historic feel, staying on Lagos Island provides proximity to key cultural sites.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Lagos is during the dry season, which runs from October to March. During this period, rainfall is minimal, making it easier to explore the city. Be aware of the Harmattan season (December to February), which brings dry, dusty winds from the Sahara, sometimes causing hazy skies. The rainy season (April to September) is characterized by heavy downpours and can lead to significant flooding and traffic disruptions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Lagos expensive?
Lagos can be both expensive and affordable, depending on your lifestyle. Accommodation, dining, and nightlife in upscale areas like Victoria Island and Ikoyi can be on par with Western cities. However, local transportation like Danfo buses, street food (suya, puff-puff), and shopping in local markets are very inexpensive. Using ride-sharing apps is moderately priced. A tourist can expect to spend anywhere from $50 to $300+ per day.
How many days do you need in Lagos?
To get a good feel for the city's diverse offerings without feeling too rushed, 4 to 5 days is recommended. This allows enough time to explore key attractions across different areas (the Island and the Mainland) and accounts for the city's notorious traffic, which can significantly impact travel times between sights.
Is Lagos safe?
Lagos is a massive metropolis with safety concerns typical of large cities. While generally safe for tourists, it is crucial to be vigilant. Stick to well-known areas, especially after dark. Avoid displaying expensive items like jewelry and cameras openly. Use trusted transportation like official hotel cars or reputable ride-sharing apps. Be wary of petty crime like pickpocketing in crowded markets. It's advisable to get local advice on which areas to avoid.
What is Lagos famous for?
Lagos is famous for being the vibrant, energetic heart of Nigeria. It's the global epicenter of Afrobeats music, home to legends like Fela Kuti, Wizkid, and Burna Boy. It's also the center of Nollywood, Nigeria's prolific film industry. The city is known for its intense energy ('Eko for show'), legendary traffic jams, bustling markets like Balogun, a dynamic art scene, and a lively beach and nightlife culture.
