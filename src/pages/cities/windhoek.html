15 Best Things to Do in Windhoek, Namibia (2025 Guide)

Windhoek, the capital of Namibia, is a city of surprising contrasts. Nestled in a high-altitude basin, it's a modern, clean city where German colonial-era castles and church spires sit alongside contemporary architecture and bustling African markets. More than just a gateway to the country's epic deserts and wildlife parks, Windhoek offers a unique cultural experience in its own right. It’s the vibrant political, economic, and cultural heart of the nation, offering a gentle introduction to the wonders of Namibia. Whether you're exploring its historical landmarks, savoring its unique culinary scene, or delving into its recent past, you'll find a wide array of fascinating activities. This guide will walk you through the very best things to do in Windhoek, ensuring your visit is both memorable and insightful.

Top 5 Must-Visit Attractions

  • Independence Memorial Museum
  • Katutura Township Tour
  • Namibia Craft Centre

Complete Guide to Windhoek Attractions

Christuskirche (Christ Church)

An enduring symbol of Windhoek, the Christuskirche stands elegantly on a traffic island in the heart of the city. This German Lutheran church, consecrated in 1910, is a remarkable architectural feat, blending Art Nouveau and neo-Gothic styles. Its construction from local sandstone and the use of Italian Carrara marble for the altar and pulpit showcase a fusion of local materials and European design. The stained-glass windows, a gift from Kaiser Wilhelm II, add to its historical and aesthetic appeal. Visitors can admire its picturesque exterior, which provides a stunning photo opportunity against the Namibian sky, or step inside to appreciate the serene atmosphere and historical details. It stands not only as a place of worship but as a monument to peace and a testament to the city's complex history.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Robert Mugabe Avenue, easily walkable from most central hotels.

Opening Hours: Monday-Friday: 9:00 AM - 1:00 PM; Saturday: 9:00 AM - 12:00 PM. Check locally for services and afternoon hours.

Cost: Free entry, donations are welcome.

Website: N/A - Information available on general Windhoek tourism sites.

Independence Memorial Museum

Dominating the skyline next to the Christuskirche, the Independence Memorial Museum is a must-visit for anyone interested in Namibia's journey to freedom. Housed in a striking, North Korean-built golden structure, the museum chronicles the anti-colonial resistance and the national liberation struggle against South African rule. The exhibits are spread across several floors, featuring powerful photographs, artifacts, and interpretive displays. A glass elevator on the outside of the building whisks visitors to the top floor, which houses a restaurant and viewing deck offering unparalleled panoramic views of Windhoek. The museum provides a somber but essential context to understanding modern Namibia and its people's resilience.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Robert Mugabe Avenue, next to the Alte Feste and Christuskirche.

Opening Hours: Monday-Friday: 9:00 AM - 5:00 PM; Saturday-Sunday: 10:00 AM - 5:00 PM.

Cost: Free entry.

Website: https://www.museums.com.na/museums/windhoek/independence-memorial-museum

Katutura Township Tour

For an authentic and eye-opening cultural experience, a guided tour of Katutura is essential. The name 'Katutura' means 'The place where people do not want to live' in Herero, a poignant reminder of its origin as a segregated township during the apartheid era. Today, it is a vibrant and sprawling suburb, home to a majority of Windhoek's population and a melting pot of Namibian cultures. Tours, typically led by residents, take you through different areas, from formal housing to informal settlements. You'll visit local markets like the Soweto Market, interact with community members, and perhaps try local delicacies like kapana (street-side grilled meat). It's a powerful way to move beyond the tourist bubble and gain a deeper understanding of Namibian life and history.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Book a tour with a reputable operator (e.g., Katu Tours). They provide transport from your hotel.

Opening Hours: Tours typically run daily, with morning and afternoon slots.

Cost: Approximately N$400 - N$600 per person.

Website: Search for local operators like 'Katu Tours Namibia' or 'Chameleon Safaris'.

Namibia Craft Centre

Located in the Old Breweries Complex, the Namibia Craft Centre is the premier destination for authentic, high-quality Namibian souvenirs and art. This vibrant hub is more than just a shop; it's a social enterprise that supports over 4,000 rural artisans from across the country, representing various ethnic groups. Here, you'll find an incredible array of products, including Himba jewelry, San ostrich eggshell crafts, Caprivian baskets, textiles, and contemporary art. The centre also features the Omba Gallery, showcasing exhibitions by local artists, and a lovely courtyard café that is a popular spot for lunch. It's the perfect place to find a meaningful memento of your trip while directly contributing to local communities.

Practical Information

How to Get There: 40 Tal Street, a short walk or taxi ride from the city center.

Opening Hours: Monday-Friday: 9:00 AM - 5:30 PM; Saturday: 9:00 AM - 2:30 PM.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: https://www.namibiacraftcentre.com/

Daan Viljoen Game Park

Just a 20-minute drive west of Windhoek, Daan Viljoen Game Park offers a quick and accessible dose of Namibian wildlife. Nestled in the hilly Khomas Hochland, the park is a popular weekend retreat for locals and a great introduction to the country's fauna for visitors short on time. As there are no large predators, it's one of the few game parks where you can safely hike or mountain bike among the animals. Keep your eyes peeled for giraffes, zebras, wildebeest, kudu, springbok, and a rich variety of birdlife. The park has well-marked hiking trails of varying lengths, a dam that attracts animals, and a restaurant and pool area. It’s a perfect half-day trip to escape the city and immerse yourself in nature.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located 24 km west of Windhoek. A taxi or rental car is required.

Opening Hours: Daily from sunrise to sunset.

Cost: Entrance fee is approximately N$50 for adults (international) plus a small vehicle fee.

Website: http://www.nwr.com.na/resorts/daan-viljoen-resort

Alte Feste (Old Fort)

The Alte Feste is Windhoek's oldest surviving building, a stark white fort that was established in 1890 to serve as the headquarters for the imperial German Schutztruppe. Its construction marks the founding of modern Windhoek. For many years, it housed the historical collection of the National Museum of Namibia, though the exhibits have since been relocated to the adjacent Independence Memorial Museum. While the interior is currently closed to the public pending renovations, the exterior remains a significant historical landmark. Outside, you can see the iconic Reiterdenkmal (Equestrian Monument), a controversial statue that has been moved several times due to its colonial connotations. It's a key site for understanding the city's colonial origins.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Centrally located on Robert Mugabe Avenue, next to the Independence Memorial Museum.

Opening Hours: Exterior can be viewed at any time. Interior is currently closed.

Cost: Free to view from the outside.

Website: https://www.museums.com.na/museums/windhoek/alte-feste

Parliament Gardens and Tintenpalast

The Tintenpalast, or 'Ink Palace', is the seat of the National Assembly (the lower house) and the National Council (the upper house) of Namibia's Parliament. Built between 1912 and 1913, its name humorously refers to the large amount of ink used by the bureaucrats who worked there. While access to the building itself is usually restricted, it can be viewed from the beautiful Parliament Gardens that surround it. The gardens are a meticulously maintained public park, offering a shady and tranquil escape from the city buzz. With lush lawns, rose beds, and statues of Namibian leaders, it's a popular spot for locals to relax. It's a perfect place for a quiet stroll and to appreciate one of Windhoek's most important government buildings.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Just east of Robert Mugabe Avenue, behind the Christuskirche.

Opening Hours: Gardens are open to the public daily during daylight hours.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Gibeon Meteorites on Post Street Mall

Strolling down Post Street Mall, a pedestrian-only shopping street, you'll encounter an unusual open-air exhibit: the Gibeon Meteorite Fountain. In 1836, a massive shower of meteorites fell in the Gibeon area of southern Namibia. The fragments, composed of a unique iron-nickel alloy, were collected over the years by the Nama people and later by colonists. 33 of the original meteorites are now mounted on steel columns as a public display in the heart of Windhoek's commercial district. It’s a fascinating and accessible piece of natural history, allowing you to see—and even touch—rocks that have traveled through space. The surrounding mall area is also a lively place to shop and people-watch.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Post Street Mall, in the central business district.

Opening Hours: Accessible 24/7.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Joe's Beerhouse

Joe's Beerhouse is more than just a restaurant; it's a Windhoek institution and a cultural experience. This legendary eatery is famous for its eclectic, maze-like decor, which includes everything from old car parts and animal skulls to empty Jägermeister bottles and quirky artifacts. The atmosphere is vibrant, rustic, and utterly unique, making it a favorite for both tourists and locals. The menu is equally adventurous, specializing in Namibian game meat. Here you can try dishes featuring kudu, oryx, zebra, and crocodile, alongside German classics like eisbein (pork knuckle). With a huge selection of local and imported beers, Joe's is the perfect place for a lively and memorable dinner.

Practical Information

How to Get There: 160 Nelson Mandela Ave. Best to take a taxi as it's a bit outside the main city center.

Opening Hours: Open daily for dinner, usually from 4:00 PM until late. Lunch on some days.

Cost: Mains range from N$150 - N$300. Reservations are highly recommended.

Website: https://www.joesbeerhouse.com/

National Art Gallery of Namibia (NAGN)

For a deep dive into the country's creative soul, visit the National Art Gallery of Namibia. The gallery is dedicated to preserving and promoting Namibian visual arts, from traditional to contemporary. The permanent collection provides a visual history of Namibia, with artworks that reflect the country's diverse cultures, landscapes, and sociopolitical journey. The NAGN also hosts a dynamic program of temporary exhibitions, showcasing the vibrant work of emerging and established Namibian artists. It's a quiet, reflective space that offers a different perspective on the nation's identity and ongoing conversations about its past and future.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Corner of Robert Mugabe Ave and John Meinert St, near the city center.

Opening Hours: Tuesday-Friday: 8:00 AM - 5:00 PM; Saturday: 9:00 AM - 2:00 PM. Closed Sunday and Monday.

Cost: Free entry.

Website: https://www.nagn.org.na/

Trans-Namib Railroad Museum

Housed in the historic Windhoek Railway Station, the Trans-Namib Museum offers a fascinating look at the history of transportation in Namibia, from the German colonial era to the present day. The railway was instrumental in the country's development and played a significant role in its history. The museum's collection includes a range of railway artifacts, from old telecommunication equipment and tools to maps and timetables. You can also view historical photographs and learn about the ambitious engineering projects required to build railways across Namibia's harsh terrain. The beautifully maintained station building itself is a point of interest.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at the Windhoek Railway Station on Bahnhof Street.

Opening Hours: Monday-Friday: 8:00 AM - 1:00 PM and 2:00 PM - 5:00 PM.

Cost: Small entrance fee of around N$20.

Website: N/A

Okapuka Ranch

Located just 30 kilometers north of Windhoek, Okapuka Ranch provides an excellent and convenient safari experience for those with limited time. The ranch is set on a large private game reserve and offers guided game drives where you are almost guaranteed to see a wide variety of wildlife, including white rhinos, giraffes, wildebeest, and numerous antelope species. One of the highlights at Okapuka is the lion feeding, an impressive spectacle where you can observe these magnificent predators up close from a safe vantage point. The ranch offers morning and afternoon activities, making it an ideal day trip from the capital. It's a perfect teaser for the larger national parks like Etosha.

Practical Information

How to Get There: 30km north of Windhoek on the B1 highway. A rental car or pre-booked transfer is needed.

Opening Hours: Activities are scheduled for morning and afternoon slots. Booking is essential.

Cost: Game drives and lion feeding activities have separate costs, typically around N$500-N$800 per activity.

Website: https://okapuka-ranch.com/

Soweto Market

For a truly local and sensory experience, head to the Soweto Market in Katutura. This isn't a tourist market; it's a bustling, energetic hub where residents shop for daily necessities. It offers a fascinating glimpse into everyday life in Windhoek. The market is a hive of activity, with vendors selling everything from fresh produce and meat to clothing and household goods. The main attraction for adventurous foodies is the 'kapana' section. Here, vendors expertly grill beef on open flames, chop it into bite-sized pieces, and serve it with a choice of chili powder, salt, and spices. You eat it standing up, sharing the experience with locals. It's loud, smoky, and an unforgettable taste of Namibian street food culture.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in Katutura. It's best visited as part of a guided township tour for context and safety.

Opening Hours: Most active during the day, seven days a week.

Cost: Free to enter. Kapana is very affordable, costing around N$20-N$50 for a good portion.

Website: N/A

Namibia Breweries Limited Tour

Namibia is famous for its beer, brewed according to the ancient German Purity Law (Reinheitsgebot) of 1516. Namibia Breweries Limited (NBL) is the home of iconic brands like Windhoek Lager and Tafel Lager. A tour of their state-of-the-art brewery on the outskirts of the city provides a fascinating insight into this history and the brewing process. The guided tour takes you through the entire production line, from the raw ingredients to the bottling and packaging. You'll learn about the company's commitment to quality and sustainability. The tour culminates, of course, in a beer tasting session, where you can sample the crisp, clean lagers that are a source of national pride.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Northern Industrial area. A taxi is required.

Opening Hours: Tours are by appointment only and must be booked in advance.

Cost: Varies, check upon booking.

Website: https://nambrew.com/contact-us/ (for booking inquiries)

Feline Conservation at Naankuse

While a bit further out (about 45km from Windhoek), the Naankuse Foundation Wildlife Sanctuary is a world-renowned conservation organization and a phenomenal experience. Founded by Marlice van Vuuren, the sanctuary focuses on rehabilitating injured and orphaned animals, with a special focus on large carnivores. Naankuse offers various activities, but the 'Carnivore Feeding Tour' is a highlight. You'll get to see and learn about leopards, cheetahs, African wild dogs, and caracals in large, semi-wild enclosures. This is not a zoo; it's a conservation project where you learn about the human-wildlife conflict and the importance of protecting these incredible predators. It's an inspiring and educational excursion for any animal lover.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located 45km east of Windhoek, off the road to the international airport. Self-drive or book a tour with transfer.

Opening Hours: Activities are at set times; booking is essential.

Cost: Carnivore feeding tours are around N$700 per person.

Website: https://naankuse.com/

Planning Your Visit to Windhoek

Getting Around

Windhoek's city center is compact and walkable. For longer distances, taxis are plentiful and affordable. Always use registered taxis or ride-hailing apps like LEFA. Do not hail unmarked cabs. Agree on the fare before starting your journey, as meters are not always used. For trips outside the city like to Daan Viljoen or Naankuse, renting a car provides the most flexibility.

Where to Stay

The City Centre (CBD) is ideal for first-time visitors, with easy access to museums and landmarks. Klein Windhoek, an upscale suburb, offers charming guesthouses and excellent restaurants. For budget travelers, backpackers and guesthouses can be found throughout the city. Recommended options include The Hilton Windhoek (luxury), The Olive Exclusive All-Suite Hotel (boutique), and Chameleon Backpackers & Guesthouse (budget).

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Windhoek is during the dry winter season, from May to October. The weather is pleasant with warm, sunny days and cool nights, and it's the prime time for wildlife viewing in the rest of Namibia. The summer months (November to April) are hotter and bring the rainy season, with potential for dramatic afternoon thunderstorms.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: Historical Heart. Explore the city center on foot. Visit Christuskirche, climb the Independence Memorial Museum for city views, and see the exterior of the Alte Feste and Tintenpalast. Stroll through Parliament Gardens and see the Gibeon Meteorites on Post Street Mall. Day 2: Culture & Crafts. Take a morning guided tour of Katutura Township. In the afternoon, shop for authentic souvenirs at the Namibia Craft Centre. Have a memorable farewell dinner at Joe's Beerhouse. Day 3: Wildlife Encounter. Choose a half-day trip to either Daan Viljoen Game Park for hiking among wildlife or to Naankuse or Okapuka Ranch for a guided carnivore feeding tour and game drive.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Windhoek expensive?
Windhoek can be considered moderately priced. Compared to major European or North American cities, it's quite affordable. Local food (like at markets), transport (taxis), and some activities are inexpensive. However, accommodation in mid-range to luxury hotels, organized tours, and dining at upscale restaurants can be comparable to Western prices. Overall, a savvy traveler can enjoy Windhoek on a reasonable budget.
How many days do you need in Windhoek?
Two to three full days are sufficient to experience the best of Windhoek. This allows enough time to cover the main historical and cultural sites, take a township tour, and enjoy a half-day wildlife activity nearby. Most travelers use Windhoek as a logistical hub to start or end a longer self-drive or guided tour of Namibia, so 2-3 days is a perfect introduction to the country.
Is Windhoek safe?
Windhoek is generally considered safe for tourists, especially during the day in the central areas. However, like any capital city, it has issues with crime. Petty crime, such as pickpocketing and bag snatching, can occur. It is advisable to avoid walking alone at night, especially in unlit areas. Keep valuables out of sight and be aware of your surroundings. Using reputable taxis or ride-hailing apps for evening travel is recommended.
What is Windhoek famous for?
Windhoek is famous for its unique blend of African and German influences, visible in its architecture, food, and culture. It's known for being one of Africa's cleanest and safest capitals. Key highlights include the iconic Christuskirche, its poignant Independence Memorial Museum, and its role as the primary starting point for adventures into Namibia's vast landscapes, such as the Namib Desert, Sossusvlei, and Etosha National Park. It is also famous for its high-quality beer, brewed according to German purity laws.
