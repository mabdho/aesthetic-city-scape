Complete Guide to Kampala Attractions

Gaddafi National Mosque

Officially the Uganda National Mosque, this colossal structure is one of Kampala's most iconic landmarks. Funded by Libya's former leader, Colonel Muammar Gaddafi, it was completed in 2006. The mosque's impressive architecture, blending modern and traditional Islamic styles, can hold up to 15,000 worshippers inside and another 10,000 in the courtyard, making it one of the largest in sub-Saharan Africa. The absolute highlight is climbing the minaret. After ascending over 300 steps, you are rewarded with a breathtaking 360-degree panoramic view of Kampala's seven hills, the bustling downtown, and the surrounding landscape. It's the single best vantage point in the city and provides a fantastic orientation to Kampala's layout.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Kampala Hill in Old Kampala. Easily accessible by boda-boda or special hire taxi from anywhere in the city.

Opening Hours: Daily from 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM for tourists. Closed to tourists during Friday prayer times.

Cost: Approx. 20,000 UGX for foreign non-residents, which includes a guided tour and covering for women.

Website: No official website, information available through local tour operators.

Ndere Cultural Centre

For a vibrant and energetic immersion into Ugandan culture, a visit to the Ndere Cultural Centre is an absolute must. 'Ndere' means 'flute,' a symbol of universal peace and communication. The centre is home to the Ndere Troupe, a celebrated group that performs traditional dances and music from across Uganda's diverse ethnic groups. The main event is the dinner-theatre style performance, a high-energy spectacle featuring hypnotic drumming, intricate choreography, and colorful traditional attire. The show is not just entertainment but a living museum of Ugandan heritage, with explanations provided for each performance. It's a joyful, educational, and thoroughly engaging experience for all ages.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in Kisaasi, a suburb of Kampala. Best reached by boda-boda, Uber, or special hire taxi.

Opening Hours: Performances are typically held on Wednesday, Friday, and Sunday evenings, starting around 7:00 PM. It's best to check their schedule.

Cost: Around 50,000 UGX for adults. A buffet dinner is available for an additional cost.

Website: https://ndere.com/

Kasubi Tombs

A UNESCO World Heritage Site, the Kasubi Tombs are the sacred burial grounds for four Kabakas (Kings) of the Buganda Kingdom. The main tomb building, Muzibu Azaala Mpanga, is a masterpiece of traditional Ganda architecture, constructed entirely from natural materials like wood, reeds, and thatch, with a massive dome-like structure. Despite a tragic fire in 2010 that destroyed the main building, extensive restoration efforts have been underway to preserve this vital piece of Buganda heritage. A visit here offers a profound insight into the history, beliefs, and spiritual traditions of the Buganda people, who form the largest ethnic group in Uganda. Guides provide detailed narratives about the kings and the kingdom's customs.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Kasubi Hill. A short boda-boda or taxi ride from the city center.

Opening Hours: Daily from 8:00 AM to 6:00 PM. It's wise to call ahead as restoration work may affect access.

Cost: Approximately 25,000 UGX for foreign visitors.

Website: https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/1022

Kabaka's Palace (Lubiri) and Idi Amin's Torture Chambers

The Kabaka's Palace, also known as the Lubiri, is the historic seat of the King of the Buganda Kingdom. While you can't enter the main palace building (Twekobe), the tour of the grounds provides a fascinating look into the history of the kingdom and its turbulent relationship with the central government, particularly during the 1966 crisis when it was attacked by Milton Obote's forces. A haunting and essential part of the tour is a visit to the underground prison and torture chambers built by Idi Amin. This dark, eerie network of cells, filled with water and electrified, serves as a chilling memorial to the thousands who were tortured and killed here during his brutal regime. The visit is somber but crucial for understanding a dark chapter in Uganda's history.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Mengo Hill, a short distance from the city center. It's often visited along with the nearby Buganda Parliament.

Opening Hours: Daily from 8:00 AM to 5:00 PM.

Cost: Entrance is around 30,000 UGX for a guided tour.

Website: Information is available through the Buganda Kingdom's tourism office.

Baha'i Temple

Perched atop Kikaya Hill, the Baha'i House of Worship is a serene oasis of tranquility and architectural beauty. As the Mother Temple of Africa for the Baha'i Faith, it is the only one of its kind on the continent. The nine-sided structure, topped with a distinctive green dome, is surrounded by expansive, beautifully manicured gardens that are perfect for a peaceful stroll or quiet reflection. The temple is open to people of all faiths for prayer and meditation. Its unique design allows light to filter in, creating a serene and contemplative atmosphere inside. The lush grounds offer stunning views of Kampala and are a popular spot for birdwatching and escaping the city's hustle.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Kikaya Hill, off the Gayaza Road. Best reached by boda-boda or special hire taxi.

Opening Hours: Grounds are open daily from 9:00 AM to 5:30 PM. The temple interior is open for prayer and viewing.

Cost: Free admission.

Website: https://www.bahai.ug/

Uganda Museum

Established in 1908, the Uganda Museum is the oldest in East Africa. It offers a comprehensive journey through Uganda's rich cultural and natural history. The exhibits are well-curated, covering ethnography, archaeology, musicology, and science. You can see traditional musical instruments (which you can sometimes play), ancient hunting equipment, and displays on traditional Ugandan homes and lifestyles. Outside, the museum features a cultural village with reconstructions of traditional huts from various Ugandan tribes, providing a tangible sense of the country's diverse architectural styles and ways of life. Itâ€™s an excellent starting point for any visitor looking to understand the deep cultural tapestry of the 'Pearl of Africa'.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Kira Road in Kamwokya, near the city center. Easily accessible by all forms of transport.

Opening Hours: Daily from 10:00 AM to 6:00 PM.

Cost: 10,000 UGX for foreign non-residents.

Website: https://www.ugandamuseums.or.ug/

Owino Market (St. Balikuddembe Market)

Prepare for a sensory overload at Owino Market, Kampala's largest and most chaotic bazaar. This sprawling labyrinth of stalls and alleyways is where thousands of locals come to buy and sell everything imaginable, but it's most famous for its vast selection of second-hand clothing ('mivumba'). A visit here is not for the faint of heart, but it's an authentic, unfiltered Kampala experience. You can find incredible bargains on clothes, shoes, electronics, and textiles. Even if you don't plan to shop, navigating the crowded passages, observing the vibrant commerce, and interacting with the resourceful vendors is an adventure in itself. It's advisable to go with a local guide for the first time.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in downtown Kampala, near the main taxi park. It's very central but can be overwhelming to reach.

Opening Hours: Daily from early morning (approx. 7:00 AM) until early evening (approx. 7:00 PM).

Cost: Free to enter, but keep belongings secure.

Website: No official website.

Uganda Martyrs Shrine, Namugongo

This is one of the most significant Christian pilgrimage sites in Africa. The shrine commemorates the 32 young Ugandan men (both Catholic and Anglican) who were burnt to death in 1886 on the orders of Kabaka Mwanga II for refusing to renounce their faith. The site features a stunning modern basilica, built in the shape of a traditional African hut over the spot where the Catholic martyrs were killed. The lake on the grounds is believed by pilgrims to have healing powers. While the site is busiest on Martyrs' Day (June 3rd), when millions of pilgrims converge here, it's a moving and peaceful place to visit year-round, offering a powerful lesson in faith and sacrifice.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in Namugongo, about 15km east of Kampala city center. Accessible by matatu or special hire taxi.

Opening Hours: Daily from 6:00 AM to 7:00 PM.

Cost: Free admission, but donations are welcome.

Website: https://www.ugandamartyrsshrine.org.ug/

Namirembe Cathedral

St. Paul's Cathedral Namirembe, perched atop Namirembe Hill, is the oldest cathedral in Uganda and the provincial cathedral of the Anglican Church of Uganda. The current structure, a magnificent red-brick building, dates back to 1919 and offers spectacular views across Kampala. Its interior is notable for its impressive organ and beautiful stained-glass windows. A visit here offers a peaceful retreat and a glimpse into the history of Christianity in Uganda. The church's graveyard contains the remains of Bishop James Hannington, an early missionary, and the cook who discovered the source of the Nile, adding another layer of historical significance.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Namirembe Hill, easily reached by boda-boda from the city center.

Opening Hours: Open daily for visitors, though access may be restricted during services.

Cost: Free admission, donations appreciated.

Website: https://www.namirembediocese.org/

Rubaga Cathedral

Saint Mary's Cathedral Rubaga, commonly known as Rubaga Cathedral, is the parent cathedral of the Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Kampala. Standing majestically on Rubaga Hill, it offers panoramic views of the city. The cathedral's twin-towered Romanesque architecture is a prominent feature of Kampala's skyline. It was consecrated in 1925 and stands on land donated by the Kabaka. Inside, the cathedral houses the remains of the first African Catholic Bishop, Joseph Kiwanuka. It is a site of great historical and religious importance for Uganda's Catholic community and a tranquil place to visit and admire the architecture.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Rubaga Hill, a short ride from the city center. Often visited in conjunction with Namirembe Cathedral.

Opening Hours: Open daily for visitors, except during mass times.

Cost: Free admission, donations are welcome.

Website: https://klarchdiocese.org.ug/parishes/rubaga-cathedral-parish/

Exposure Africa Craft Market

For a more relaxed souvenir shopping experience than the bustling city markets, head to Exposure Africa Craft Market. This open-air market on Buganda Road is one of the largest and most well-known craft markets in the city, offering a vast array of handmade goods from Uganda and neighboring countries. You can find everything from wood carvings, leather goods (especially sandals), vibrant textiles (kitenge), drums, jewelry, and paintings. The vendors are generally friendly, and while bargaining is expected, the atmosphere is less intense than at Owino. It's the perfect place to find unique, authentic souvenirs to take back home.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Buganda Road, just off Kampala Road in the city center. It's within walking distance of many central hotels.

Opening Hours: Daily from approximately 8:30 AM to 7:00 PM.

Cost: Free to browse. Prices are negotiable.

Website: No official website.

Lake Victoria at Munyonyo or Ggaba

No trip to Kampala is complete without seeing the mighty Lake Victoria, the largest lake in Africa and the world's second-largest freshwater lake. While Kampala isn't directly on a sandy beach, the lakeside suburbs of Munyonyo and Ggaba offer great access. Munyonyo is more upscale, home to the Speke Resort, where you can enjoy boat cruises, horseback riding, and fine dining with lake views. Ggaba is more of a local's spot, famous for its bustling fish market right on the shore. Here you can see fishermen bringing in their daily catch of tilapia and Nile perch. You can buy fresh fish and have it fried or grilled on the spot, served with cassava or chipsâ€”a truly authentic and delicious Ugandan experience.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Both Munyonyo and Ggaba are located south of the city center and are best reached by boda-boda or special hire taxi.

Opening Hours: Ggaba market is busiest in the morning and late afternoon. Munyonyo resorts are open all day.

Cost: Access to Ggaba is free. Activities and dining at Munyonyo resorts have associated costs.

Website: https://www.spekeresort.com/ for Munyonyo options.

Independence Monument

Located in the heart of Kampala, the Independence Monument is a powerful and historically significant sculpture. Unveiled just before Uganda's first independence celebrations on October 9, 1962, it symbolizes the nation's birth. The statue depicts a man unwrapping a child and raising it to the sky, representing a newly born nation being freed from the bonds of colonialism. While a quick stop, it's a poignant photo opportunity and a moment to reflect on Uganda's journey. Its central location, surrounded by high-end hotels and government buildings, makes it an easy and important landmark to visit while exploring the city center.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Centrally located on Speke Road, across from the Sheraton Kampala Hotel.

Opening Hours: 24/7, publicly accessible.

Cost: Free.

Website: No official website.

Makerere University

Often referred to as the 'Harvard of Africa,' Makerere University is one of the oldest and most prestigious universities on the continent. Established in 1922 as a technical school, it has been a center of intellectual and political life in East Africa for decades, with alumni including numerous post-independence African leaders. A walk through its sprawling, leafy campus is a pleasant way to spend an afternoon. You can admire the colonial-era architecture of the main building, visit the university art gallery which showcases works by Ugandan artists, and soak in the academic atmosphere. It offers a different, more serene perspective on Kampala life.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Makerere Hill, north of the city center. Easily accessible by boda-boda or matatu.

Opening Hours: Campus is generally open during daylight hours. The gallery has specific opening times.

Cost: Free to walk around the campus.

Website: https://www.mak.ac.ug/

Kisementi and Acacia Mall

To experience modern, cosmopolitan Kampala, head to the Kisementi area and the adjacent Acacia Mall in the upscale Kololo neighborhood. This area is the hub of expatriate and affluent local life, brimming with trendy cafes, international restaurants, cocktail bars, and boutiques. Acacia Mall offers a familiar shopping mall experience with a cinema, supermarkets, and various stores. The Kisementi square, just across the road, comes alive at night and is one of Kampala's premier nightlife spots. It's the perfect place to enjoy a great meal, sip on a coffee, or experience the city's vibrant social scene.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in Kololo. Easily accessible by Uber, SafeBoda, or special hire taxi.

Opening Hours: Shops and restaurants have individual hours, generally 9:00 AM until late.

Cost: Free to walk around. Dining and shopping costs vary.

Website: https://acaciamall.co.ug/

Planning Your Visit to Kampala

Getting Around

The most common way to get around Kampala is by 'boda-boda' (motorcycle taxi), which is fast and navigates traffic easily. For safety, use ride-hailing apps like SafeBoda or Uber, which provide helmets and fixed pricing. 'Matatus' (minibus taxis) are the cheapest option and run on fixed routes, but can be crowded and confusing for new visitors. Special hire taxis are also available but are more expensive; always agree on a price beforehand.

Where to Stay

For upscale accommodation and proximity to fine dining and nightlife, stay in Kololo or Nakasero. These areas host hotels like the Kampala Serena Hotel and the Sheraton. For beautiful views and a quieter suburban feel, consider Muyenga Hill. Budget and mid-range travelers can find excellent guesthouses and boutique hotels in areas like Bukoto and Naguru. For a central, bustling experience, areas around the city center offer numerous options.

Best Time to Visit

Kampala has a tropical climate, with pleasant temperatures year-round. The best times to visit are during the two dry seasons: from June to August and from December to February. During these months, there is less rain, making it easier to explore the city. The two rainy seasons are from March to May (the heaviest) and from September to November. Even during the rainy seasons, it rarely rains all day, typically featuring short, heavy downpours.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: History & Culture. Start at the Uganda Museum. Then take a boda-boda to the Kasubi Tombs. In the afternoon, visit the Kabaka's Palace and Idi Amin's Torture Chambers. Day 2: Faith & Markets. Climb the minaret at the Gaddafi National Mosque for morning views. Then visit the twin cathedrals: Namirembe (Anglican) and Rubaga (Catholic). In the afternoon, brave the chaos of Owino Market and finish with souvenir shopping at the Exposure Africa Craft Market. Day 3: Serenity & Modern Life. Spend a peaceful morning at the Baha'i Temple and its gardens. Head to the Kisementi/Acacia Mall area for lunch. In the evening, enjoy a spectacular cultural performance and dinner at the Ndere Cultural Centre.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Kampala expensive?
Kampala can be a very affordable city for travelers. Local transport like boda-bodas and matatus are very cheap, and a delicious local meal (like a 'rolex' or food from a local buffet) can cost just a few dollars. However, it can also be expensive if you choose to stay in luxury hotels, dine at high-end international restaurants in areas like Kololo, or use private taxis for all transport. Overall, it offers a wide range of options to suit any budget, from backpacker to luxury traveler.
How many days do you need in Kampala?
To get a good feel for the city and see the main highlights without rushing, 3 to 4 days is an ideal amount of time. This allows you to explore the historical and cultural sites, experience the markets, enjoy the nightlife, and even relax by Lake Victoria. If Kampala is your entry point to a longer Ugandan safari, 2 full days can cover the absolute must-sees.
Is Kampala safe?
Kampala is generally considered safe for tourists, and Ugandans are known for being incredibly warm and welcoming. However, like any large city, it's important to take common-sense precautions. Petty crime like pickpocketing and bag-snatching can occur, especially in crowded areas like markets and taxi parks. Avoid walking alone at night in unlit areas and keep your valuables secure. Traffic is a more significant safety concern; when using a boda-boda, always insist on a helmet and use a trusted service like SafeBoda.
What is Kampala famous for?
Kampala is famous for several things: its 'City of Seven Hills' geography, providing stunning views from various points; its vibrant and chaotic energy, best exemplified by the ubiquitous boda-boda motorcycle taxis; its incredibly friendly and welcoming people; and its dynamic nightlife. It's also known as a major cultural and historical hub for Uganda, home to the seat of the Buganda Kingdom and significant religious landmarks.
