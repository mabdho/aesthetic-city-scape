15 Best Things to Do in Maputo, Mozambique (2025 Guide)

Welcome to Maputo, the 'City of Acacias'. This vibrant coastal capital of Mozambique is a captivating blend of African energy and Portuguese colonial charm. With its wide, tree-lined avenues, stunning seaside setting, and a cultural scene pulsating with the rhythms of Marrabenta music, Maputo offers a unique travel experience. The city's architecture tells a story of its past, from grand colonial buildings and Art Deco gems to imposing socialist-era structures. Beyond the history, you'll find bustling markets, a world-class culinary scene famed for its fresh seafood, and the warm, welcoming spirit of its people. Whether you're exploring historical forts, admiring architectural wonders, or soaking up the sun on a nearby island, you'll quickly discover the many incredible and best things to do in Maputo. This guide will walk you through the must-see attractions and hidden gems of this dynamic African metropolis.

Complete Guide to Maputo Attractions

Maputo Central Train Station (CFM)

Often cited as one of the world's most beautiful train stations, the Maputo Central Railway Station is an architectural masterpiece. Designed by associates of Gustave Eiffel, the famed architect of the Eiffel Tower, its striking mint-green facade and magnificent wrought-iron lattice work are a sight to behold. The grand dome, completed in 1910, crowns a building that feels more like a palace than a transportation hub. While still a functioning station, its grandeur far exceeds its current utility. Visitors can wander through the main hall, admire the vintage steam locomotives on display, and soak in the historic atmosphere. The station is not just a transit point but a cultural landmark, home to the Kulungwana Art Gallery and a jazz club, making it a living piece of Maputo's history and a testament to its golden age of rail travel.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Praça dos Trabalhadores in the Baixa (downtown) district. Easily accessible on foot from most central locations.

Opening Hours: Station is generally open daily from early morning until evening. Gallery and club have specific hours.

Cost: Free to enter the main station hall. A small fee may apply for specific exhibits.

Maputo Fortress (Fortaleza de Maputo)

The Fortress of Maputo is a stark, star-shaped reminder of the city's colonial origins. Originally built by the Portuguese in the late 18th century, the current stone structure dates back to the mid-19th century. It stands guard over the harbor, offering a tangible link to a past filled with maritime trade, conflict, and colonial administration. The fort's thick walls and cannons are impressively preserved, providing a powerful sense of history. Inside, you'll find a small military history museum and the Gilded Coffin of Ngungunhane, the last ruler of the Gaza Empire who resisted Portuguese rule. The centerpiece is a striking equestrian statue of Mouzinho de Albuquerque, a former colonial governor, which was relocated here from its original city-center location after independence. It's a fascinating and contemplative place to explore.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Situated at Praça 25 de Junho, near the port and the Baixa district.

Opening Hours: Typically 9:00 AM - 5:00 PM, closed on Mondays.

Cost: A small entrance fee of around 50-100 MZN.

Website: N/A

Mercado Central (Central Market)

Dive headfirst into the daily life of Maputo at the Mercado Central. This bustling, covered market is the city's commercial and sensory heart. Housed in a historic building dating back to 1901, the market is a vibrant explosion of colors, sounds, and smells. The aisles are packed with vendors selling everything from fresh fruits, vegetables, and spices to an incredible array of fresh fish and seafood, including the city's famous prawns. Beyond produce, you'll find stalls selling cashews, local crafts, fabrics (capulanas), and household goods. It's an excellent place for photography and to get a genuine feel for the local culture. Be prepared to politely haggle if you wish to buy something, and always keep an eye on your belongings in the crowded space.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Avenida 25 de Setembro in the Baixa district.

Opening Hours: Monday - Saturday, from early morning until around 5:00 PM. Closed on Sundays.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: N/A

The Iron House (Casa de Ferro)

Another architectural curiosity linked to Gustave Eiffel, the Casa de Ferro is a prefabricated, all-metal house designed in Belgium in 1892. It was shipped to Maputo to serve as the governor's residence, but its design proved completely impractical for Mozambique's hot, humid climate, quickly turning into a sweltering oven. The governor never lived in it, and it was moved to its current location. Today, this unique and elegant building stands as a historical folly and a popular tourist attraction. Its intricate metalwork and verandas are fascinating to examine up close. The building now houses a small office for the National Directorate of Cultural Heritage, but visitors are free to walk around its exterior and admire its unusual design.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Avenida Samora Machel, next to the Tunduru Botanical Gardens.

Opening Hours: Accessible to view from the outside 24/7. Interior access may be limited.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

FEIMA - Feira de Artesanato, Flores e Gastronomia

For the best souvenir shopping and a relaxed afternoon, head to FEIMA. This sprawling, permanent arts and crafts market is a treasure trove of Mozambican creativity. Set in a pleasant park, dozens of stalls showcase a vast array of goods, including intricate wooden sculptures, colorful paintings, handmade jewelry, leather goods, and traditional textiles. Unlike the more chaotic central markets, FEIMA has a calm atmosphere, and vendors are generally less pushy. It's a great place to find high-quality, authentic souvenirs. The market also features a number of small restaurants and bars serving delicious local food and drinks, making it an ideal spot to have lunch and watch local artisans at work.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Avenida dos Mártires da Machava, in the Polana Cimento 'A' neighborhood.

Opening Hours: Daily, approximately 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: N/A

National Art Museum (Museu Nacional de Arte)

The National Art Museum offers a comprehensive overview of Mozambican art, with a particular focus on the 20th century. The collection showcases the country's best painters and sculptors, including world-renowned artists like Malangatana Ngwenya and Alberto Chissano. Their powerful works often explore themes of colonialism, the war for independence, civil war, and national identity. The museum provides profound insight into the nation's turbulent history and vibrant culture through the eyes of its most creative minds. It's a must-visit for anyone interested in art and the story of modern Mozambique. The collection is housed in a modest but well-lit building, allowing the striking artwork to take center stage.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at 1233 Avenida Ho Chi Minh, near the city center.

Opening Hours: Tuesday - Sunday, 2:00 PM - 6:00 PM. Closed on Mondays.

Cost: A small entrance fee is charged, around 100-200 MZN.

Website: N/A

Maputo Cathedral (Catedral de Nossa Senhora da Imaculada Conceição)

An imposing and beautiful landmark, the Maputo Cathedral stands tall in Praça da Independência. Designed by Portuguese architect Marcial Simões de Freitas e Costa, this striking white Art Deco cathedral was completed in 1944. Its clean lines, soaring bell tower, and minimalist yet grand interior make it a peaceful sanctuary from the city's hustle and bustle. The cathedral's simple elegance is its main draw. Inside, stained-glass windows cast colorful light across the expansive nave. It's an active place of worship but welcomes respectful visitors to admire its unique architecture and serene atmosphere. It forms a key part of the architectural ensemble around Independence Square, alongside City Hall and the Iron House.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Praça da Independência in the heart of the city.

Opening Hours: Open daily, but be respectful of service times.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Jardim Tunduru (Tunduru Botanical Gardens)

A green oasis in the center of Maputo, the Tunduru Botanical Gardens offer a tranquil escape. Designed in 1885 by Thomas Honney, who also designed gardens for sultans in Turkey and kings in Greece, this park is a beautiful example of late 19th-century landscape design. It's filled with towering trees, exotic plants, and shady pathways perfect for a leisurely stroll. While it has seen better days, its historic charm remains. The gardens are home to a statue of Mozambique's first president, Samora Machel, and the whimsical Iron House is located right at its entrance. It's a popular spot for locals to relax and a great place to cool down on a hot afternoon.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Rua Henrique de Sousa, with entrances on Avenida Samora Machel.

Opening Hours: Open daily during daylight hours.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Museum of Natural History (Museu de História Natural)

Housed in a beautiful neo-Manueline style building, the Museum of Natural History is as interesting for its architecture as its exhibits. The collection, though somewhat dated, offers a fascinating glimpse into the natural world of Mozambique. Its most famous and compelling exhibits are the dioramas of Southern African wildlife and a unique collection of elephant fetuses showing their development in the womb. The museum also has sections on ethnography and geology. It’s a quirky and educational experience, particularly for families with children, providing context to the country's incredible biodiversity. The building itself, with its ornate details, is worth the visit alone.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Praça Travessia do Zambeze, in the Polana neighborhood.

Opening Hours: Tuesday - Sunday, 9:00 AM - 4:00 PM. Closed on Mondays.

Cost: A small entrance fee applies, around 50-100 MZN.

Website: N/A

The Money Museum (Museu da Moeda)

Located in a beautiful historic building known as the 'Yellow House' (Casa Amarela), the Money Museum is one of Maputo's best-curated attractions. It traces the history of money in Mozambique and the wider region, from ancient bartering systems and trade beads to colonial currencies and the modern Metical. The exhibits are well-presented and informative, offering a unique lens through which to view the country's social and political history. The building itself, constructed in the 1760s, is believed to be the oldest colonial building in Maputo, adding another layer of historical significance to your visit. It provides a fascinating and often overlooked perspective on the development of the nation.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Praça 25 de Junho, right next to the fortress.

Opening Hours: Tuesday - Sunday, 10:00 AM - 5:00 PM. Closed on Mondays.

Cost: Small entrance fee.

Website: N/A

Mafalala Walking Tour

For a truly authentic and insightful experience, take a walking tour of Mafalala. This historic bairro (neighborhood) is considered the cradle of Mozambican nationalism and culture. It was the birthplace or home of many of the country's most famous figures, including poet José Craveirinha, former president Samora Machel, and football legend Eusébio. Guided tours, often led by local residents, take you through the vibrant streets, sharing stories of resistance, culture, and daily life. You'll learn about the origins of Marrabenta music, see significant historical sites, and get a feel for a community that has shaped the nation's identity. It's a powerful way to connect with the real Maputo beyond the main tourist trail.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Tours are organized by local associations. Book in advance. They usually arrange a meeting point.

Opening Hours: Tours run on specific schedules, typically in the morning. Booking is essential.

Cost: Varies by tour operator, but expect around $20-30 USD per person.

Website: Search for 'Iverca Mafalala Walking Tour' to find booking options.

Núcleo de Arte

A cornerstone of Maputo's contemporary art scene, Núcleo de Arte is a dynamic artists' cooperative and gallery. Established in the 1930s, it has long been a hub for creative minds to gather, work, and exhibit. The space is vibrant and ever-changing, showcasing paintings, sculptures, and mixed-media works from both established and emerging Mozambican artists. Beyond the gallery, the cooperative has a lively backyard bar that becomes a social hotspot, especially on weekends and during its famous Sunday afternoon live music sessions. It’s an incredible place to see cutting-edge art, meet local artists, and enjoy a cold beer while listening to some of the best live music in the city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at 194 Rua da Argélia, not far from the city center.

Opening Hours: Gallery is open daily. The bar and events have evening and weekend hours.

Cost: Free to enter the gallery. Cover charge may apply for music events.

Website: N/A

Maputo-Katembe Bridge

Dominating the city's skyline, the Maputo-Katembe Bridge is Africa's longest suspension bridge and a marvel of modern engineering. Completed in 2018, it connects the capital with the previously isolated district of Katembe, opening up access to the beautiful beaches to the south, including Ponta do Ouro. The bridge itself is a stunning landmark, especially when lit up at night. While you can drive across it, a great way to experience it is to take a taxi to the Katembe side and look back at the spectacular panoramic views of the Maputo skyline and bay. The bridge symbolizes a new era of development for Mozambique and offers a breathtaking perspective of the city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: The bridge spans the Maputo Bay, accessible by car or taxi from the city.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7.

Cost: A toll is charged for vehicles to cross.

Website: N/A

Fish Market (Mercado de Peixe)

For a quintessential Maputo culinary experience, visit the new Mercado de Peixe on the city's waterfront. This is more than just a market; it's a food destination. You start by walking through the clean and modern market section, where you can buy an incredible variety of fresh-off-the-boat seafood—prawns, lobster, squid, and a wide range of fish. After making your selection, you take your purchase to one of the adjacent open-air restaurants. For a small fee, they will grill or fry your seafood to perfection, serving it with traditional sides like fries, salad, or xima (maize porridge). It's a lively, delicious, and incredibly fresh meal with a fantastic sea view.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the Avenida da Marginal, in the Costa do Sol area. A taxi is the easiest way to get there.

Opening Hours: Daily from late morning until evening.

Cost: You pay for the raw seafood (by weight) plus a cooking fee at the restaurant.

Website: N/A

Inhaca Island

For a perfect day trip or weekend escape from the city, head to Inhaca Island. A short ferry or boat ride across the bay, Inhaca feels like a world away from the capital's bustle. The island boasts pristine beaches, clear turquoise waters, and important coral reefs, making it a great spot for snorkeling and diving. You can also visit the Marine Biology Museum or hike to the historic lighthouse for spectacular views. The island offers a taste of Mozambique's idyllic coastline without having to travel far. Relax on the sand at Santa Maria, enjoy a fresh seafood lunch at a beachside restaurant, and experience the laid-back island lifestyle. It's the perfect complement to a city-based holiday.

Practical Information

How to Get There: A public ferry departs from the port in Maputo several times a week (check schedule locally). Faster, private speedboats can also be hired.

Opening Hours: Ferries have set departure times. Day trips are popular.

Cost: Ferry cost is low, but speedboats are significantly more expensive. There's also a small island conservation fee upon arrival.

Website: N/A

Planning Your Visit to Maputo

Getting Around

Walking is feasible in the central Baixa district. For longer distances, official taxis are available but agree on a price beforehand or ensure the meter is used. Ride-sharing apps like Bolt are popular and often cheaper. For a local experience, you can use 'chapas' (minibus taxis), which run on set routes and are very cheap, but can be crowded.

Where to Stay

Polana is an upscale neighborhood with luxury hotels like the iconic Polana Serena Hotel and the modern Radisson Blu Hotel & Residence. The Baixa (downtown) area offers mid-range options and easy access to historical sites. For a beachside vibe, look for guesthouses in the Costa do Sol area. Hotel Cardoso offers stunning bay views from its clifftop location between Polana and Baixa.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Maputo is during the dry season, from May to November. During these months, the weather is pleasant, with cooler temperatures and lower humidity, making it ideal for exploring the city. The wet season, from December to April, is hot, humid, and can experience heavy rainfall.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: Explore the historic Baixa. Visit the CFM Train Station, Maputo Fortress, Money Museum, and the Iron House. Wander through Jardim Tunduru and admire the Maputo Cathedral. Day 2: Immerse yourself in culture and markets. Start at the Mercado Central, then head to FEIMA for craft shopping and lunch. In the afternoon, visit the National Art Museum and end the day with live music at Núcleo de Arte. Day 3: Experience the coast. Take a taxi to the Mercado de Peixe (Fish Market) for an unforgettable seafood lunch. Afterwards, cross the Maputo-Katembe Bridge for incredible city views, or relax on the Costa do Sol beach.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Maputo expensive?
Maputo can have a reputation for being expensive, but it depends on your travel style. Accommodation in high-end hotels, imported goods, and dining at tourist-focused restaurants can be costly, comparable to European prices. However, the city can be very affordable if you live like a local. Eating at small restaurants, using chapas for transport, and buying produce from markets are all very cheap. The local currency is the Mozambican Metical (MZN).
How many days do you need in Maputo?
To see the main highlights of Maputo city itself, 3 to 4 days is an ideal amount of time. This allows you to explore the historical sights, markets, and cultural institutions at a relaxed pace. If you want to include a day trip or an overnight stay on Inhaca Island, plan for 5 days.
Is Maputo safe?
Maputo is generally considered safe for tourists, but like any large city, it requires common sense and awareness. Petty crime, such as pickpocketing and bag snatching, can occur, especially in crowded areas like markets. It's advisable to avoid walking alone at night, especially in poorly lit areas. Use reputable taxis or ride-sharing apps after dark. Do not display expensive items like jewelry, cameras, or large amounts of cash openly.
What is Maputo famous for?
Maputo is famous for several things: its wide, acacia-lined avenues and unique blend of Portuguese colonial, Art Deco, and Brutalist architecture; its vibrant cultural scene, especially the homegrown Marrabenta music; its incredible culinary offerings, particularly fresh prawns and seafood; and its strategic importance as a major port city on the Indian Ocean.
