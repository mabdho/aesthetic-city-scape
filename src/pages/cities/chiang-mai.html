15 Best Things to Do in Chiang Mai, Thailand (2025 Guide)

Nestled in the mountainous region of Northern Thailand, Chiang Mai is a captivating blend of ancient culture and modern creativity. Known as the 'Rose of the North,' it offers a more relaxed, verdant alternative to the bustling southern cities. Here, golden temples sit beside trendy cafes, and lush national parks are just a short drive from vibrant night markets. Whether you're seeking spiritual enlightenment, culinary adventures, or close encounters with gentle giants, you'll find it here. This city is a sanctuary for travelers, artists, and digital nomads alike, drawn by its rich Lanna heritage and laid-back atmosphere. It’s a place where every winding soi (alley) reveals a new surprise, making it easy to see why exploring the best things to do in Chiang Mai is an unforgettable experience. Prepare to be enchanted by its unique charm and warm hospitality.

Complete Guide to Chiang Mai Attractions

Wat Phra That Doi Suthep

Perched atop Doi Suthep mountain, this temple is Chiang Mai's most sacred and iconic landmark. The final climb involves a 306-step staircase flanked by magnificent Naga serpents, or you can opt for a cable car. At the summit, you'll find a stunning golden chedi (stupa) that glitters in the sun, said to contain a relic of the Buddha. The panoramic views of Chiang Mai city from the temple terrace are breathtaking, especially at sunrise or sunset when the city lights begin to twinkle. The entire complex is a work of art, with intricate carvings, murals depicting the life of the Buddha, and serene statues. The ringing of bells and the scent of incense create a deeply spiritual atmosphere. It's an essential pilgrimage for Thais and a must-see for any visitor, offering both cultural insight and incredible photo opportunities. Remember to dress respectfully, covering your shoulders and knees.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Hire a 'songthaew' (red shared taxi) from the Old City or near the Chiang Mai Zoo. The scenic drive takes about 45 minutes.

Opening Hours: 6:00 AM - 6:00 PM daily

Cost: 30 THB for foreigners (plus 20 THB for optional cable car)

Website: https://www.doisuthep.org/

Visit an Ethical Elephant Sanctuary

An encounter with elephants is a quintessential Northern Thailand experience, and Chiang Mai is a leader in ethical elephant tourism. Sanctuaries like Elephant Nature Park and Elephant Jungle Sanctuary have pioneered a 'no riding' model, focusing on rescue, rehabilitation, and conservation. Here, you can spend a day or half-day with these gentle giants in a natural setting. Activities typically include preparing their food, feeding them bananas and sugarcane, and walking with them through the forest to a river where you can help bathe them. It's a joyful and deeply moving experience to see these majestic animals happy and well-cared for. Booking in advance is crucial as these popular centers fill up quickly. It's a responsible way to interact with Thailand's national animal and support their welfare.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Most sanctuaries include round-trip transportation from your hotel in their tour packages.

Opening Hours: Varies by sanctuary; typically full-day or half-day tours.

Cost: 2,500 - 6,000 THB depending on the sanctuary and program length.

Website: https://www.elephantnaturepark.org/

Explore the Chiang Mai Old City Temples

Chiang Mai's Old City is a treasure trove of history, enclosed by a moat and remnants of an ancient defensive wall. Within this one-square-mile area, you can discover over 30 beautiful temples (wats) dating back to the Lanna Kingdom. The best way to explore is on foot or by bicycle, allowing you to wander down quiet lanes and stumble upon hidden gems. Key temples to visit include Wat Chedi Luang, with its massive ruined chedi, Wat Phra Singh, home to the revered Phra Buddha Sihing statue, and the smaller but exquisite Wat Phan Tao, known for its teakwood viharn (assembly hall). Each temple has a unique story and architectural style, offering a fascinating glimpse into the region's spiritual and artistic heritage. It's a journey back in time right in the heart of the modern city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the center of Chiang Mai. Easily accessible on foot, by bicycle, tuk-tuk, or Grab.

Opening Hours: Most temples are open from dawn until dusk, around 6:00 AM - 5:00 PM.

Cost: Most are free, but some larger ones like Wat Chedi Luang have a small entrance fee (around 40 THB).

Website: N/A (General tourist area)

Doi Inthanon National Park

Known as the 'Roof of Thailand,' Doi Inthanon is home to the country's highest peak, standing at 2,565 meters (8,415 feet) above sea level. The park is a natural paradise of lush rainforests, powerful waterfalls like Wachirathan and Sirithan, and unique cloud forests. The cooler climate at the summit supports flora and fauna not found elsewhere in Thailand. A highlight is visiting the twin Royal Pagodas (Phra Mahathat Naphamethanidon and Naphapholphumisiri) built to honor the late King Bhumibol Adulyadej and Queen Sirikit. Surrounded by beautiful gardens, they offer stunning views of the surrounding landscape. The park also features hiking trails, such as the Ang Ka Nature Trail, and Karen and Hmong hill tribe villages. A day trip here offers a refreshing escape from the city heat and a chance to witness Thailand's spectacular natural beauty.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Best visited via a pre-booked tour or by hiring a private driver for the day. It's a 1.5-2 hour drive from Chiang Mai.

Opening Hours: 5:30 AM - 6:00 PM daily

Cost: Park entrance fee: 300 THB for foreign adults, 150 THB for children. Additional 40 THB for the Royal Pagodas.

Website: https://www.thainationalparks.com/doi-inthanon-national-park

Sunday Walking Street Market (Tha Pae Gate)

Every Sunday evening, Ratchadamnoen Road transforms from a quiet Old City thoroughfare into Chiang Mai's most impressive market. Stretching from Tha Pae Gate all the way to Wat Phra Singh, this sprawling market is a sensory delight. It's less about tourist trinkets and more about high-quality, locally made goods. You'll find intricate silverware, hand-woven textiles, beautiful ceramics, unique clothing, and original art. Beyond shopping, the market is a cultural event. Street food stalls offer a dizzying array of northern Thai delicacies, from sai oua (spicy sausage) to khao soi (curry noodles). Local musicians play traditional Lanna music, and you can even get an open-air foot massage to soothe your tired feet. The atmosphere is vibrant, friendly, and authentically local. It's the perfect place to find unique souvenirs and experience Chiang Mai's creative community spirit.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Starts at Tha Pae Gate in the Old City. Easily reached on foot from anywhere in the Old City, or by songthaew/tuk-tuk.

Opening Hours: Every Sunday, approximately 4:00 PM - 11:00 PM.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: N/A (Weekly event)

Wat Chedi Luang

Dominating the Old City skyline, Wat Chedi Luang is one of Chiang Mai's most historically significant temples. Its centerpiece is a massive, partially ruined Lanna-style chedi that once stood over 80 meters tall before being damaged by an earthquake in the 16th century. Despite the damage, its scale is still awe-inspiring and speaks to the power of the former Lanna Kingdom. The chedi's base is guarded by impressive elephant statues, some of which are replicas of the originals. The temple complex also houses the city pillar shrine (Lak Mueang) and several beautiful viharns. One of these once housed the famous Emerald Buddha, which is now in Bangkok. Today, you can see a jade replica. A popular activity is participating in the daily 'monk chat,' where you can speak with young monks to learn about their lives and Buddhism.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Centrally located within the Old City, easily walkable from most hotels.

Opening Hours: 6:00 AM - 5:00 PM daily

Cost: 40 THB for foreigners.

Website: N/A

Take a Thai Cooking Class

Northern Thai cuisine is distinct and delicious, and there's no better souvenir than learning how to cook it yourself. Chiang Mai is famous for its excellent cooking schools, which offer immersive, hands-on experiences. A typical class involves a trip to a local market to learn about fresh ingredients like galangal, kaffir lime leaves, and Thai basil. Back at the school, which is often set in a beautiful garden or traditional Thai house, you'll be guided step-by-step through making several classic dishes. You'll learn to pound your own curry paste and cook iconic meals like Khao Soi, Pad Thai, Tom Yum soup, and mango sticky rice. It's a fun, educational, and delicious activity suitable for all skill levels. You'll leave with a full stomach and a recipe book to recreate the flavors of Thailand back home.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Most cooking schools include hotel pick-up and drop-off in the price.

Opening Hours: Half-day (morning/evening) and full-day classes are available daily.

Cost: 1,000 - 2,000 THB per person.

Website: https://www.baanthai.com/ (Example: Baan Thai Cookery School)

Bua Tong 'Sticky' Waterfalls

For a truly unique natural adventure, head to the Bua Tong Waterfalls, affectionately known as the 'Sticky Waterfalls.' The magic here lies in the mineral deposits in the water, which create a limestone surface on the rocks that is incredibly grippy, almost like pumice. This allows you to climb directly up the multi-tiered waterfall against the flow of the water, no special equipment needed, feeling a bit like Spider-Man. It's a fantastic and fun experience for all ages. The surrounding park area is peaceful, with picnic spots and shaded areas perfect for a relaxing afternoon. The water is cool and refreshing, making it an ideal escape from the city's heat. It's less crowded than some of the more famous attractions, offering a more serene and playful interaction with nature.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located about 1.5 hours north of Chiang Mai. Best reached by renting a scooter, hiring a private driver, or grabbing a songthaew.

Opening Hours: 8:30 AM - 5:00 PM daily

Cost: Free entry.

Website: N/A

Chiang Mai Night Bazaar

The Chiang Mai Night Bazaar is a legendary shopping experience that has been a city fixture for decades. Every night, Chang Klan Road comes alive with hundreds of stalls selling everything imaginable. While it's more tourist-focused than the Sunday Walking Street, it's still a fantastic place to browse for souvenirs, clothing, accessories, and electronics. Haggling is expected and part of the fun. The bazaar is more than just shopping. It's an entertainment hub with multiple indoor plazas, food courts, and live music venues. You can find everything from cheap and cheerful street food to sit-down seafood restaurants. The atmosphere is bustling and energetic, making it a great place for an evening stroll even if you don't plan to buy anything. It's a classic Chiang Mai experience that's open every single night.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Chang Klan Road, east of the Old City. A short walk or tuk-tuk ride from most central areas.

Opening Hours: Approximately 6:00 PM - 12:00 AM daily.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: N/A (Nightly event)

Wat Umong (The Tunnel Temple)

Escape the city bustle at Wat Umong, a serene forest temple known for its unique network of ancient tunnels. Built in the 14th century, these brick-lined tunnels were constructed for a distinguished but eccentric monk to meditate in peace. Walking through the cool, quiet tunnels, you'll find small shrines and Buddha images in alcoves, creating a contemplative and mysterious atmosphere. The temple grounds are sprawling and peaceful, set in a wooded area with a large pond where you can feed fish and turtles. You can also see a collection of weathered Buddha statues scattered amongst the trees and an impressive, unadorned stupa. Wat Umong is also a functioning meditation center, and you'll find 'talking trees' with proverbs in Thai and English, adding to the tranquil vibe. It's a perfect spot for quiet reflection.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at the base of Doi Suthep, west of the Old City. Best reached by scooter, Grab, or songthaew.

Opening Hours: 6:00 AM - 5:00 PM daily

Cost: Free entry, donations welcome.

Website: http://www.watumong.org/

Nimmanhaemin Road (Nimman)

Nimmanhaemin Road, or 'Nimman,' is Chiang Mai's trendiest and most modern neighborhood. It's a hub for digital nomads, expats, and young Thais, characterized by its upscale boutiques, art galleries, chic cafes, and innovative restaurants. The main road is busy, but the real charm lies in the numbered side streets (sois), where you'll discover hidden gems and beautifully designed spaces. Spend an afternoon cafe-hopping, sampling specialty coffee from local roasters, or browse the unique fashion and design shops. In the evening, the area comes alive with a sophisticated nightlife scene, from rooftop bars offering city views to cool cocktail lounges and live music venues. A visit to Nimman provides a fascinating contrast to the ancient culture of the Old City, showcasing Chiang Mai's contemporary and creative side.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located west of the Old City, near Chiang Mai University. Easily accessible by Grab or songthaew.

Opening Hours: Shops and cafes typically open around 10:00 AM; bars and restaurants stay open late.

Cost: Free to explore; costs vary by establishment.

Website: N/A (Neighborhood)

Wat Phra Singh Woramahawihan

Wat Phra Singh is one of the most revered and visually stunning temples within Chiang Mai's Old City. Second only to Wat Chedi Luang in importance, it dates back to the 14th century and is a prime example of classic Lanna architecture. The temple's name means 'Temple of the Lion Buddha,' and it houses the highly venerated Phra Buddha Sihing image in the beautiful Viharn Lai Kham assembly hall. The entire complex is immaculate, featuring golden chedis, ornate prayer halls with high, red-lacquered ceilings, and intricate murals depicting local life and Buddhist tales. The main chedi, gilded in gold, is particularly impressive. The temple is an active place of worship, and visiting gives you a sense of its living importance to the community. It's an essential stop on any temple tour of the Old City.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at the western end of Ratchadamnoen Road in the Old City. Easily walkable.

Opening Hours: 6:00 AM - 5:00 PM daily

Cost: Free to enter the grounds; 20 THB fee to enter the main viharn.

Website: N/A

Grand Canyon Water Park

For a day of thrilling, water-based fun, head to the Grand Canyon Water Park. This former limestone quarry has been transformed into a massive aquatic playground. The main attraction is a huge inflatable obstacle course floating in the canyon's turquoise waters, featuring slides, climbing walls, and trampolines. It's a fantastic challenge for kids and adults alike. Beyond the inflatable course, the park offers zip-lining across the canyon, wakeboarding, kayaking, and areas for swimming and relaxing. Safety is a priority, with lifeguards on duty and mandatory life jackets. It's a popular spot for both locals and tourists looking to cool off and burn some energy, providing a very different kind of Chiang Mai adventure.

Practical Information

How to Get There: About a 30-minute drive southwest of the Old City. Best reached by Grab, private taxi, or by renting a scooter.

Opening Hours: 10:00 AM - 7:00 PM daily

Cost: Approximately 650 THB for an all-day pass.

Website: https://www.canyoncnxticket.com/

Lanna Folklife Museum

Located in the heart of the Old City, directly opposite the Three Kings Monument, the Lanna Folklife Museum offers a deep dive into the rich history and culture of Northern Thailand. Housed in a beautifully restored colonial-style courthouse, the museum's exhibits are modern, well-curated, and presented in both Thai and English. It's an excellent place to gain context before or after visiting the city's temples. The displays cover all aspects of Lanna life, from religious ceremonies and belief systems to traditional costumes, textiles, and craftsmanship. You can see intricate dioramas of historical events and beautifully preserved artifacts. A visit provides a comprehensive understanding of what makes Lanna culture unique and enriches your appreciation for the art and architecture you see throughout Chiang Mai.

Practical Information

How to Get There: In the center of the Old City, opposite the Three Kings Monument. Easily accessible on foot.

Opening Hours: 8:30 AM - 4:30 PM, Tuesday to Sunday (Closed Mondays).

Cost: 90 THB for foreign adults, 40 THB for children.

Website: N/A

Huay Tung Tao Lake

A favorite weekend escape for locals, Huay Tung Tao Lake is a large reservoir at the foot of Doi Suthep-Pui National Park. The main draw is the collection of bamboo huts built on stilts over the water's edge. You can rent one for a small fee, order delicious and affordable local Thai food and drinks, and spend the afternoon relaxing with your feet in the water. It's a very low-key and authentic experience. You can swim in the lake, rent a paddleboat, or simply enjoy the peaceful scenery and mountain backdrop. There's also a 12-kilometer track around the lake that's popular for cycling and jogging. In recent years, giant animal sculptures made from rice straw have been added, creating quirky photo opportunities. It's the perfect place to unwind like a local.

Practical Information

How to Get There: About a 20-minute drive north of the Old City. Best reached by scooter or Grab.

Opening Hours: 8:00 AM - 6:00 PM daily

Cost: 50 THB per person entry fee.

Website: N/A

Planning Your Visit to Chiang Mai

Getting Around

Chiang Mai is easy to navigate. The 'songthaew' (red shared trucks) are the most common and affordable option; just flag one down and tell the driver your destination. Tuk-tuks are available for shorter, private trips (agree on a price beforehand). The ride-hailing app Grab is very popular, reliable, and offers fixed pricing. For maximum freedom, many visitors rent scooters (around 200-300 THB/day), but only do so if you are an experienced and licensed rider with proper travel insurance.

Where to Stay

For first-time visitors, staying within the Old City offers easy access to temples and markets. For a modern, trendy vibe with chic cafes and bars, choose Nimmanhaemin (Nimman). The Riverside area along the Ping River offers luxury hotels and romantic dining. For budget travelers and backpackers, the areas around Tha Pae Gate and Loi Kroh Road are popular. Recommended hotels include The Inside House (Luxury), U Nimman Chiang Mai (Mid-Range), and The Common Hostel (Budget).

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Chiang Mai is during the cool, dry season from November to February. The weather is pleasant with average temperatures of 25°C (77°F), clear skies, and low humidity, perfect for exploring. The hot season is from March to May, with temperatures soaring above 35°C (95°F); this is also the 'burning season' when air quality can be poor. The rainy season runs from June to October, with frequent afternoon downpours, but the landscape is incredibly lush and green.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: Explore the Old City's temples like Wat Chedi Luang and Wat Phra Singh on foot in the morning. In the late afternoon, take a songthaew up to Wat Phra That Doi Suthep for sunset views. Enjoy dinner in the Old City or visit the nightly Night Bazaar. Day 2: Spend the morning at an ethical elephant sanctuary, feeding and bathing the elephants. In the afternoon, take a Thai cooking class to learn the secrets of Northern Thai cuisine. Day 3: Take a day trip to Doi Inthanon National Park to see Thailand's highest peak and stunning waterfalls, or visit the Bua Tong 'Sticky' Waterfalls for a unique climbing adventure. In the evening, if it's a Sunday, immerse yourself in the Sunday Walking Street Market.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Chiang Mai expensive?
No, Chiang Mai is known for being very affordable, especially compared to Western countries and even Bangkok or the southern islands. You can find delicious street food meals for 40-60 THB ($1-2 USD), a private room in a guesthouse for 500-800 THB ($15-25 USD), and get around via songthaew for 30 THB. While there are luxury hotels and fine dining options, it's very easy to travel comfortably on a budget of $30-50 USD per day.
How many days do you need in Chiang Mai?
A minimum of 3 to 4 days is recommended to cover the main highlights: the Old City temples, Doi Suthep, an elephant sanctuary, and a market. However, to truly appreciate the region's charm and explore further afield (like Doi Inthanon or Pai), a stay of 5 to 7 days is ideal. Many people fall in love with the relaxed pace and end up staying for weeks or even months.
Is Chiang Mai safe?
Chiang Mai is generally a very safe city for travelers, with low rates of violent crime. The main things to be aware of are petty theft (like pickpocketing in crowded markets) and traffic accidents. Always keep your valuables secure. If you rent a scooter, be sure you are licensed, experienced, always wear a helmet, and have comprehensive travel insurance, as road safety is a major concern in Thailand.
What is Chiang Mai famous for?
Chiang Mai is famous for its rich Lanna culture, hundreds of ancient temples, ethical elephant sanctuaries, and stunning natural scenery including mountains and waterfalls. It's also a major hub for digital nomads and is renowned for its incredible food scene, particularly the unique Northern Thai cuisine with dishes like Khao Soi. Additionally, its vibrant night markets, traditional crafts, and relaxed, friendly atmosphere make it a world-famous travel destination.
