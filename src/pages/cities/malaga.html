15 Best Things to Do in Malaga, Spain (2025 Guide)

Basking on Spain's sun-drenched Costa del Sol, Malaga is a city where ancient history and vibrant modernity dance under endless blue skies. As one of the world's oldest cities, its streets are a tapestry of Phoenician, Roman, and Moorish influences, crowned by the majestic Alcazaba fortress. Yet, Malaga is also the proud birthplace of Pablo Picasso, a legacy celebrated in world-class museums that inject a dose of avant-garde cool into its historic heart. From sipping sweet local wine and feasting on fresh seafood at a chiringuito to exploring contemporary art districts and relaxing on golden beaches, there is a captivating energy here. This guide will walk you through the very best things to do in Malaga, ensuring you experience the full spectrum of its Andalusian charm. Get ready to discover a city that effortlessly blends culture, cuisine, and coastal bliss, making it an unforgettable Spanish escape.

Complete Guide to Malaga Attractions

Alcazaba of Malaga

The Alcazaba is Malaga's most iconic landmark, a formidable Moorish fortress-palace dating back to the 11th century. Perched on a hill overlooking the city and sea, its strategic importance is immediately clear. Wandering through its labyrinthine courtyards, you'll discover tranquil patios, fragrant gardens, burbling fountains, and stunning examples of Nasrid architecture. The double-walled fortifications offer a fascinating glimpse into the city's defensive past. Connected to the Castillo de Gibralfaro above, the Alcazaba is a journey back in time. As you explore its ramparts, you are rewarded with breathtaking views over the Roman Theatre below and the modern port beyond. It's a remarkably well-preserved structure and an essential starting point for understanding Malaga's rich history.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Calle Alcazabilla, 2, in the historic center, at the foot of Gibralfaro hill. Easily accessible on foot.

Opening Hours: Summer (Apr-Oct): 9:00 AM - 8:00 PM; Winter (Nov-Mar): 9:00 AM - 6:00 PM. Closed Mondays.

Cost: General: ~€3.50. Combined ticket with Gibralfaro Castle: ~€5.50. Free entry on Sundays after 2:00 PM.

Website: https://www.malagaturismo.com/en/tourist-resources/detail/alcazaba/6

Museo Picasso Málaga

Malaga is the birthplace of Pablo Picasso, and this museum is a magnificent tribute to its most famous son. Housed in the beautiful 16th-century Buenavista Palace, the collection was established thanks to donations from the artist's family. It showcases over 200 works that span Picasso's entire career, offering a unique insight into his artistic evolution, from his early academic studies to his revolutionary cubist creations and late-career sculptures. The museum provides a deeply personal look at the artist's journey, with many pieces revealing his intimate connection to his Andalusian roots. Beyond the permanent collection, the museum hosts excellent temporary exhibitions, making it a dynamic and ever-engaging destination for art lovers.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Palacio de Buenavista, Calle San Agustín, 8, in the heart of the historic center.

Opening Hours: Open daily. Hours vary by season but generally 10:00 AM - 7:00 PM. Check website for specifics.

Cost: General: ~€9.00. Free entry on Sundays for the last two hours of opening.

Website: https://www.museopicassomalaga.org/en

Malaga Cathedral

Known affectionately as 'La Manquita' (The One-Armed Lady), Malaga Cathedral is a stunning masterpiece of Renaissance and Baroque architecture. Construction began in the 16th century on the site of a former mosque, but it was never fully completed due to a lack of funds. The most notable omission is its southern tower, which gives the cathedral its unique, asymmetrical silhouette and its charming nickname. Inside, the grandeur is overwhelming. Soaring vaulted ceilings, an intricately carved choir stall made of mahogany and cedar, and magnificent chapels filled with religious art create a serene and awe-inspiring atmosphere. You can also take a rooftop tour for an unforgettable panoramic view of Malaga's skyline.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Dominating the skyline at Calle Molina Lario, 9, in the old town. Impossible to miss.

Opening Hours: Mon-Sat: 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM; Sun: 2:00 PM - 6:00 PM. Hours can vary for religious services.

Cost: Cathedral entrance: ~€8.00. Rooftop tour is extra.

Website: http://www.malagacatedral.com/

Castillo de Gibralfaro

For the most spectacular panoramic views of Malaga, a trip up to the Castillo de Gibralfaro is non-negotiable. This 14th-century castle sits atop the hill of the same name, built to house troops and protect the Alcazaba below. The name 'Gibralfaro' means 'rock of light', referring to a historic lighthouse or watchtower that once stood here. While the interior is mostly ruins, the main reason to visit is to walk along the entire length of its massive ramparts. From here, you can see the entire city, the port, the bullring, and the expansive Mediterranean Sea. The walk up is steep but rewarding, though a bus or taxi is a popular alternative. Visiting at sunset offers a particularly magical experience.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Camino Gibralfaro, 11. Can be reached via a steep footpath from the Alcazaba or by bus #35 from Alameda Principal.

Opening Hours: Summer (Apr-Oct): 9:00 AM - 8:00 PM; Winter (Nov-Mar): 9:00 AM - 6:00 PM.

Cost: General: ~€3.50. Combined ticket with Alcazaba: ~€5.50.

Website: https://www.malagaturismo.com/en/tourist-resources/detail/castillo-de-gibralfaro/12

Roman Theatre (Teatro Romano)

Discovered by accident in 1951 during construction work, the Roman Theatre is Malaga's oldest monument. Nestled at the foot of the Alcazaba, this 1st-century BC relic is a powerful reminder of the city's Roman past under Emperor Augustus. The theatre was used for several centuries before being repurposed as a quarry by the Moors to build the fortress above, a fascinating layering of history. The site has been beautifully excavated and restored. You can freely walk around the viewing platforms to admire the 'cavea' (seating area), 'orchestra' (stage area), and 'proscaenium' (stage front). The adjacent interpretation center provides context and displays artifacts found during the excavation.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Calle Alcazabilla, 8. Located right next to the Alcazaba entrance in the city center.

Opening Hours: Tue-Sat: 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM; Sun: 10:00 AM - 4:00 PM. Closed Mondays.

Cost: Free admission.

Website: https://www.malagaturismo.com/en/tourist-resources/detail/teatro-romano/647

Centre Pompidou Málaga

A vibrant, multi-colored glass cube known as 'El Cubo' marks the entrance to the Malaga branch of the famous Parisian Centre Pompidou. This striking pop-up museum is the first to be established outside of France and has become a major cultural landmark on the city's redeveloped waterfront, Muelle Uno. It offers a fresh, modern counterpoint to the city's ancient historical sites. The museum houses a superb semi-permanent collection of 20th and 21st-century art drawn from the main Pompidou collection, featuring works by artists like Frida Kahlo, Francis Bacon, and Max Ernst. It also hosts world-class temporary exhibitions, workshops, and performances, making it a hub of contemporary creativity.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Muelle Uno, the port's main promenade. A pleasant walk from the city center.

Opening Hours: Wed-Mon: 9:30 AM - 8:00 PM. Closed Tuesdays.

Cost: General: ~€9.00. Free entry on Sundays after 4:00 PM.

Website: https://centrepompidou-malaga.eu/en/

Mercado Central de Atarazanas

For a true taste of local life, a visit to the Atarazanas Market is essential. This bustling central market is housed in a stunning 19th-century iron building that incorporates a monumental 14th-century Moorish archway, its original main gate. The name 'Atarazanas' means 'shipyard,' a nod to the site's original purpose when the sea reached this far inland. Inside, a huge stained-glass window depicts the city's history. The market is a feast for the senses. Stalls are piled high with fresh produce, glistening seafood, local cheeses, cured meats, and fragrant spices. It's the perfect place to sample local delicacies. Grab a seat at one of the small tapas bars at the entrance for some of the freshest fried fish and a cold beer.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Calle Atarazanas, 10. A short walk from the main shopping street, Calle Larios.

Opening Hours: Mon-Sat: 8:00 AM - 3:00 PM. Closed Sundays.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: http://www.mercadocentralatarazanas.com/

Playa de la Malagueta

Just a short walk from the historic center, Playa de la Malagueta is Malaga's main city beach. This long, wide stretch of dark sand is the perfect urban oasis for sunbathing, swimming, or simply relaxing with a view of the Mediterranean. The beach is man-made and well-maintained, equipped with all the necessary amenities including sunbed rentals, showers, and playgrounds. Lining the promenade are numerous 'chiringuitos' (beachfront restaurants) where you can experience a quintessential Malagueño tradition: 'espetos de sardinas'. These are fresh sardines skewered on a cane and grilled over an open fire in a sand-filled boat. It's a simple, delicious, and authentic taste of the Costa del Sol.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located just east of the port and Muelle Uno. A 10-15 minute walk from the city center.

Opening Hours: 24/7

Cost: Free to access. Sunbed and umbrella rentals cost ~€10-€15 per day.

Website: N/A

Jardín Botánico-Histórico La Concepción

Escape the city's hustle and bustle at La Concepción, one of Spain's most beautiful and important botanical gardens. Created in the 19th century, this lush tropical paradise is located just north of the city center. It features a stunning collection of over 25,000 plants from five continents, including a magnificent palm grove, ancient ficus trees, and a historical mansion. The garden is designed as a series of romantic, winding paths that lead you past waterfalls, ponds, and even a small Doric temple. The 'Historical Route' takes you through the original 19th-century garden, while other routes explore specific plant collections. It’s a tranquil and breathtakingly beautiful spot for a leisurely stroll.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Camino del Jardín Botánico, 3. Best reached by car, taxi, or Bus #2 from the city center.

Opening Hours: Tue-Sun. Summer: 9:30 AM - 8:30 PM; Winter: 9:30 AM - 5:30 PM. Closed Mondays.

Cost: General: ~€5.20.

Website: https://laconcepcion.malaga.eu/

Soho, Malaga's Art District

Once a neglected neighborhood, Soho has been transformed into Malaga's vibrant and edgy arts district. Situated between the Alameda Principal and the port, this area is a canvas for urban creativity. A project called MAUS (Málaga Arte Urbano Soho) invited renowned international street artists like Obey and D*Face to leave their mark, resulting in incredible large-scale murals on buildings throughout the district. Beyond the street art, Soho is home to the Teatro del Soho CaixaBank, independent art galleries, craft beer pubs, quirky cafes, and vintage shops. It's a fantastic area to wander through, camera in hand, to discover hidden artistic gems and experience Malaga's contemporary cultural pulse.

Practical Information

How to Get There: The area is located south of the Alameda Principal. Easily walkable from the historic center.

Opening Hours: 24/7 for street art viewing.

Cost: Free.

Website: https://www.malagaturismo.com/en/resources/neighbourhoods/soho/106

Museo Carmen Thyssen Málaga

Housed in a beautifully restored 16th-century palace, the Carmen Thyssen Museum is a must-see for lovers of Spanish art. The collection focuses primarily on 19th-century Spanish and Andalusian painting, providing a captivating visual narrative of the country's culture and customs during that period. The artworks were once part of the personal collection of Baroness Carmen Thyssen-Bornemisza. The galleries are organized thematically, covering landscapes, portraits, and scenes of daily life in Andalusia, from romantic bullfights and vibrant ferias to serene seascapes. The museum provides a wonderful context for the region's history and aesthetic, perfectly complementing the modern art found at the Picasso and Pompidou museums.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Plaza Carmen Thyssen, Calle Compañía, 10. Located in the historic center near Plaza de la Constitución.

Opening Hours: Tue-Sun: 10:00 AM - 7:00 PM. Closed Mondays.

Cost: General: ~€10.00.

Website: https://www.carmenthyssenmalaga.org/en

Paseo del Muelle Uno

Muelle Uno is the stunning result of Malaga's port regeneration project. This contemporary open-air shopping and leisure complex stretches along the waterfront, offering a beautiful promenade with fantastic views of the Alcazaba, the Gibralfaro Castle, and the luxury yachts in the marina. It's a lively, modern space that perfectly integrates the city with the sea. The promenade is lined with an array of stylish shops, diverse restaurants, and cocktail bars with sunny terraces. It's also where you'll find the Centre Pompidou and a small chapel from the 18th century. On weekends, a bustling craft market ('El Zoco') adds to the vibrant atmosphere. It’s the ideal place for a sunset stroll, a meal with a view, or simply people-watching.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at the port, adjacent to the historic center and La Malagueta beach.

Opening Hours: Retail shops generally 10:00 AM - 10:00 PM. Restaurants and bars have longer hours.

Cost: Free to walk around.

Website: https://www.muelleuno.com/

Hammam Al Ándalus Arab Baths

Step back into the era of Al-Andalus and indulge in a deeply relaxing experience at the Hammam Al Ándalus. These Arab baths are a stunning recreation of a traditional Moorish hammam, located in the heart of the old city. The ambiance is enchanting, with Nazari-style architecture, candle-lit walkways, intricate tilework, and the gentle sound of flowing water. The experience involves rotating between hot, warm, and cold thermal baths, followed by a steam room infused with eucalyptus. You can enhance your visit with a traditional 'kessa' massage and exfoliation on a hot stone bed or a relaxing oil massage. It's a perfect way to unwind after several days of sightseeing and connect with the city's Moorish heritage on a sensory level.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Plaza de los Mártires Ciriaco y Paula, 5. In the historic center.

Opening Hours: Open daily in 90-minute sessions, from 10:00 AM to 10:00 PM. Booking is essential.

Cost: Prices start from ~€50 for the bath and massage experience.

Website: https://malaga.hammamalandalus.com/en/

Automobile and Fashion Museum

For a dose of glamour and unexpected history, visit the Museo Automovilístico y de la Moda. Housed in a former tobacco factory ('La Tabacalera'), this unique museum presents a stunning private collection that intertwines automotive history with 20th-century haute couture. It's an exhibition that appeals to a wide range of interests beyond just car enthusiasts. The museum showcases nearly 100 beautifully restored classic cars from the Belle Époque to the modern era, including Bugattis, Rolls Royces, and even a solar-powered prototype. Each car is paired with period fashion from designers like Chanel, Dior, and Balenciaga, creating a fascinating dialogue between technology and style. It's a visually spectacular and surprisingly captivating journey through design history.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Avenida de Sor Teresa Prat, 15. A bit outside the center; take bus #7 from Alameda Principal.

Opening Hours: Open daily 10:00 AM - 7:00 PM.

Cost: General: ~€9.50.

Website: https://www.museoautomovilmalaga.com/en/

Gibralfaro Viewpoint (Mirador de Gibralfaro)

While often combined with a visit to the castle, the main viewpoint just below the castle entrance deserves its own mention. The Mirador de Gibralfaro is the classic postcard view of Malaga. From this specific vantage point, your gaze sweeps down over the city's bullring (Plaza de Toros), across the Paseo del Parque, and out to the port and Muelle Uno promenade, with the Mediterranean stretching to the horizon. It's a breathtaking spot that perfectly captures the layout of the city. You can drive or take a bus right to the viewpoint, making it more accessible than the full castle walk for those with limited mobility. It is an unmissable photo opportunity, especially during the golden hours of sunrise or sunset when the city is bathed in a warm, magical light.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the road leading up to the Castillo de Gibralfaro. Accessible by car, taxi, or Bus #35.

Opening Hours: 24/7

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Planning Your Visit to Malaga

Getting Around

Malaga's historic center is compact and best explored on foot. For longer distances, the EMT bus network is extensive and efficient. The city also has two metro lines, useful for reaching areas further out. Renting bicycles or electric scooters is a popular and fun way to explore the waterfront and beach promenades. Taxis and ride-sharing services are readily available.

Where to Stay

For first-time visitors, the Centro Histórico (Old Town) is ideal for its atmosphere and proximity to major sights; try the Room Mate Larios or Only YOU Hotel. For beach lovers, La Malagueta offers sea views and easy beach access, with options like the Gran Hotel Miramar. The Soho district provides a trendier, artsy vibe with boutique hotels like the Mariposa Hotel Málaga.

Best Time to Visit

The best times to visit Malaga are during the spring (April-June) and autumn (September-October). During these months, the weather is pleasantly warm and sunny, perfect for sightseeing without the intense summer heat. Summer (July-August) is hot and busy, ideal for beach holidays but can be crowded. Winter is mild and quieter, offering a peaceful escape with fewer tourists.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: Immerse yourself in history. Start at the Roman Theatre, then climb to the Alcazaba. In the afternoon, explore the Malaga Cathedral and wander the charming streets of the old town. Day 2: Dive into art and culture. Spend the morning at the Museo Picasso Málaga, then stroll through the Soho art district to see the street art. In the afternoon, visit the Centre Pompidou on the Muelle Uno waterfront. Day 3: Enjoy views and relaxation. Take a bus up to the Castillo de Gibralfaro for panoramic views, then head down to Playa de la Malagueta for some sun and a traditional 'espeto' lunch at a chiringuito.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Malaga expensive?
Malaga is generally considered one of the more affordable large cities in Spain, especially compared to Madrid or Barcelona. Accommodation offers a wide range of prices, from budget hostels to luxury hotels. Eating out can be very cheap, particularly if you stick to tapas bars where a small beer or wine often comes with a free snack. A meal at a mid-range restaurant is reasonably priced. Most major attractions have entrance fees, but many offer free entry on certain days or times.
How many days do you need in Malaga?
To see the main highlights of the city itself, 3 full days is a good amount of time. This allows you to explore the historical sites, visit the key museums, and relax on the beach without rushing. If you want to use Malaga as a base for day trips to other beautiful places in Andalusia like Ronda, Nerja, or the Caminito del Rey, you should plan for 5 to 7 days.
Is Malaga safe?
Yes, Malaga is a very safe city for tourists. The crime rate is low, and it's generally safe to walk around at night, especially in the well-lit and busy city center and waterfront areas. However, like in any popular tourist destination, you should take standard precautions. Be aware of your surroundings in crowded places like markets and public transport to avoid pickpocketing, and don't leave your valuables unattended on the beach.
What is Malaga famous for?
Malaga is most famous for being the birthplace of artist Pablo Picasso. It's also renowned for its rich history, showcased by its impressive Moorish Alcazaba and Roman Theatre. As the capital of the Costa del Sol, it's famous for its sunny weather and beautiful beaches. Culinarily, Malaga is known for its sweet dessert wine, fresh seafood, and especially 'espetos de sardinas' (grilled sardine skewers).
