15 Best Things to Do in Havana, Cuba (2025 Guide)

Stepping into Havana is like entering a time capsule where vibrant history, revolutionary spirit, and infectious rhythms converge. Faded colonial facades in candy colors line cobblestone streets, while chrome-fendered classic American cars cruise along the iconic Malecón seawall. This is a city that engages all the senses: the scent of Cuban cigars, the taste of aged rum, the sound of salsa spilling from open doorways, and the sight of a community brimming with warmth and resilience. Beyond the postcard images, you'll find a complex and captivating culture waiting to be explored. From grand plazas to hidden art projects, discovering the layers of this Caribbean capital is an unforgettable adventure. This guide will walk you through all the very best things to do in Havana, ensuring your trip is as magical as the city itself.

Top 5 Must-Visit Attractions

  • Stroll Along El Malecón
  • Ride in a Classic American Car
  • Visit Fusterlandia

Complete Guide to Havana Attractions

Wander Through Habana Vieja (Old Havana)

A UNESCO World Heritage site, Old Havana is the city's historic heart and a living museum. Its five large plazas, including the magnificent Plaza de la Catedral and the bustling Plaza Vieja, are connected by a labyrinth of narrow, cobblestone streets. Every corner reveals beautifully restored baroque and neoclassical buildings, charming courtyards, and lively street scenes. It's a place to get lost intentionally, discovering hidden cafes, art galleries, and the pulse of daily Cuban life. Spend hours meandering without a plan, soaking in the atmosphere. You'll encounter street musicians, artists selling their work, and locals going about their day. The architectural grandeur, combined with the visible decay of some buildings, creates a poignant and beautiful contrast that defines Havana.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the eastern part of the city. It's best explored on foot. Taxis can drop you off at any of the main plazas, like Parque Central.

Opening Hours: 24/7, though individual shops, museums, and restaurants have their own hours, typically 10:00 AM to 6:00 PM.

Cost: Free to walk around. Museum and attraction entries vary.

Website: whc.unesco.org/en/list/204

Stroll Along El Malecón

The Malecón is Havana’s legendary 8-kilometer-long esplanade, sea drive, and seawall, stretching from Old Havana to the Vedado neighborhood. It’s often called 'the world’s longest sofa,' as it serves as a massive open-air living room for Habaneros. By day, fishermen cast their lines into the turquoise waters. As the sun sets, the wall comes alive with couples, families, musicians, and friends gathering to chat, drink rum, and enjoy the ocean breeze. A walk along the Malecón offers a cross-section of Cuban life against a backdrop of crumbling, salt-sprayed buildings and the crashing waves of the Straits of Florida. It's an essential experience, especially at sunset when the sky erupts in color and the classic cars cruising by seem to glow.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Easily accessible from Old Havana, Centro Habana, and Vedado. It's a long walk, so you might choose a section or take a taxi.

Opening Hours: 24/7

Cost: Free

Website: No official website.

Ride in a Classic American Car

No trip to Havana is complete without a ride in one of the iconic 1950s American classic cars. These 'almendrones,' as they are locally known, are meticulously maintained relics from a bygone era, gleaming in vibrant shades of pink, turquoise, and red. Taking a city tour in a convertible Cadillac or Chevrolet is one of the most stylish and enjoyable ways to see the sights. Most tours last for one to two hours and typically cover major landmarks like Plaza de la Revolución, El Morro fortress, the Malecón, and the grand mansions of the Vedado and Miramar districts. It's a fantastic photo opportunity and a truly unique way to experience the city's scale and grandeur.

Practical Information

How to Get There: You can find classic car taxis congregating around major tourist spots like Parque Central, the Capitolio, and near large hotels.

Opening Hours: Tours are available all day, typically from 9:00 AM until after sunset.

Cost: Approximately $30-50 USD per hour for the car (not per person). Prices are negotiable.

Website: No official booking website; arrange directly with drivers.

Visit Fusterlandia

In the fishing village of Jaimanitas, on the outskirts of Havana, you'll find the fantastical world of Fusterlandia. This is the home and workshop of artist José Fuster, who has transformed his entire neighborhood into a massive, surrealist art installation inspired by Gaudi's work in Barcelona. Over several decades, Fuster has covered his own home and over 80 neighboring houses with whimsical, colorful mosaic tiles. Walking through Fusterlandia feels like stepping into a dream. The art depicts Cuban life, fantastical creatures, and Santería symbols. It's a joyful, imaginative, and utterly unique public art project that showcases the incredible creativity and community spirit of the Cuban people.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in Jaimanitas, about a 20-30 minute taxi ride west of central Havana. A taxi there and back is the best option.

Opening Hours: Tuesday to Sunday, 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM.

Cost: Free, but donations are welcome.

Website: No official website.

Explore Castillo de los Tres Reyes del Morro

Guarding the entrance to Havana Bay, the Castillo de los Tres Reyes del Morro (El Morro) is a formidable 16th-century fortress with a commanding presence. Its iconic lighthouse has become a symbol of the city. The fortress was built to protect Havana from pirates and foreign invaders, and its stone walls and cannons tell tales of colonial battles. Visitors can explore the ramparts, dungeons, and old barracks while enjoying breathtaking panoramic views of the Malecón and the city skyline. It's particularly impressive at sunset. Across the road is the even larger La Cabaña fortress, where a traditional cannon-firing ceremony (El Cañonazo) takes place nightly at 9:00 PM.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the eastern side of the bay entrance. The best way to get there is by taxi via the tunnel under the bay.

Opening Hours: Daily, 8:00 AM to 8:00 PM.

Cost: Around $6 USD for entry. An additional fee is charged for the 9 PM cannon ceremony.

Website: No official website.

Experience a Live Tropicana Show

The Tropicana Club is a world-famous open-air cabaret that has been dazzling audiences since 1939. Set in a lush tropical garden in the Marianao district, this legendary institution offers a spectacular showcase of Cuban music and dance. The show features hundreds of performers in extravagant, feathered, and sequined costumes, performing intricate choreography under the stars. While it's a splurge, the Tropicana experience is a throwback to Havana's glamorous golden age. The ticket price usually includes a welcome drink and a bottle of rum to share at your table. It's a night of pure, over-the-top spectacle and vibrant energy that you won't soon forget.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Marianao district. A taxi is the best way to get there and back.

Opening Hours: Shows typically start at 10:00 PM, but doors open earlier.

Cost: Tickets range from approximately $80 to $100 USD per person, depending on seating.

Website: www.cabaret-tropicana.com

Sip a Mojito at La Bodeguita del Medio

Famous as one of Ernest Hemingway's favorite watering holes, La Bodeguita del Medio is a tiny, historic bar in the heart of Old Havana. Its walls are covered in graffiti and signatures from decades of visitors, including Hemingway, Pablo Neruda, and Salvador Allende. The bar claims to be the birthplace of the mojito, and while that's debatable, there's no denying its historic charm. Push your way through the crowd to the bar, order a famously strong mojito, and listen to the live Son Cubano band that's almost always playing. It's a crowded, touristy, but undeniably classic Havana experience that connects you to the city's storied past.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Calle Empedrado, just off Plaza de la Catedral in Old Havana. Easily walkable.

Opening Hours: Daily, 10:30 AM to midnight.

Cost: Mojitos are around $5 USD.

Website: No official website.

Tour a Cigar Factory (Real Fábrica de Tabacos Partagás)

Cuban cigars are world-renowned, and a factory tour offers a fascinating look into the meticulous, handcrafted process. While the original Partagás factory behind the Capitolio is now a store, the official tours have been relocated to another working factory. Here, you can watch hundreds of expert 'torcedores' (cigar rollers) sort, roll, and box the famous cigars by hand. The tour takes you through the entire process, from the selection of the leaves to the final packaging. The aroma of rich tobacco fills the air, and the skill on display is incredible. It’s an authentic cultural experience that provides a deep appreciation for Cuba's most famous export.

Practical Information

How to Get There: The current tour location is on Calle San Carlos between Peñalver and Sitios in Centro Habana. Check with a tourist office for the latest location, as it can change. The original building is now a La Casa del Habano store.

Opening Hours: Monday to Friday, with tours usually in the morning (e.g., 9:30 AM, 10:30 AM).

Cost: Approximately $10 USD per person.

Website: No official website; book at a hotel tour desk or tourist office.

Walk Down Paseo del Prado

Paseo del Prado is a beautiful, tree-lined marble promenade that serves as the dividing line between Old Havana and Centro Habana. Flanked by ornate, historic buildings, bronze lion statues, and old-fashioned street lamps, it's one of the most elegant boulevards in the city. It stretches from the Malecón all the way to Parque Central and the magnificent Gran Teatro. This is a popular spot for locals to stroll, skateboard, and relax on the marble benches. On weekends, it often hosts art exhibitions and street performances. Walking its length provides a more tranquil experience than the bustling streets of Old Havana and offers a glimpse into the city's former architectural splendor.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Runs between Parque Central and the Malecón. Easily accessible on foot from Old Havana.

Opening Hours: 24/7

Cost: Free

Website: No official website.

Discover the Museo de la Revolución

Housed in the opulent former Presidential Palace, the Museum of the Revolution chronicles Cuba's history from the pre-Columbian era to the present day, with a heavy focus on the 1959 revolution. The building itself is a sight to behold, with lavish interiors designed by Tiffany's of New York. Bullet holes from a 1957 student attack on the dictator Batista are still visible on the central staircase. Behind the museum, in the Granma Memorial pavilion, you'll find the actual yacht that carried Fidel Castro, Che Guevara, and 80 other revolutionaries from Mexico to Cuba in 1956. The museum's exhibits are primarily in Spanish, but the artifacts and the grandeur of the setting make it a compelling visit for anyone interested in Cuba's tumultuous past.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Calle Refugio No. 1, between Monserrate and Zulueta, near the Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes.

Opening Hours: Daily, 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM.

Cost: Approximately $8 USD for entry.

Website: No official website.

Enjoy the View from the Camera Obscura

For one of the best bird's-eye views of Old Havana, head to the Camera Obscura. Located on the top floor of the Gómez Vila building on the corner of Plaza Vieja, this unique attraction uses a simple periscope and a series of lenses to project a live, 360-degree image of the city onto a concave screen in a dark room. It was the first of its kind in Latin America. A guide narrates the 10-minute presentation, pointing out key landmarks and sharing interesting anecdotes about the city below. After the show, you can step out onto the rooftop for incredible real-life panoramic photos of the plaza and the surrounding cityscape.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the northeast corner of Plaza Vieja in Old Havana.

Opening Hours: Daily, 9:30 AM to 5:30 PM.

Cost: Approximately $2 USD.

Website: No official website.

Explore Callejón de Hamel

Callejón de Hamel is a short but vibrant alleyway in the Cayo Hueso neighborhood that explodes with color and creativity. The project was started in 1990 by artist Salvador Gonzáles Escalona, who transformed the street into a shrine to Afro-Cuban culture. The walls are covered in murals, sculptures made from bathtubs and scrap metal, and phrases from Santería religions. It's a dynamic, ever-evolving art space that feels raw and authentic. The best time to visit is on Sunday around noon, when the alley comes alive with the powerful rhythms of rumba music and dance, drawing in crowds of locals and tourists alike for an unforgettable street party.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located between Calles Espada and Aramburu in Centro Habana. It's a short walk from the Malecón or the University of Havana.

Opening Hours: 24/7, but best on Sunday from 12:00 PM to 3:00 PM for live rumba.

Cost: Free, but be prepared to tip the musicians or buy a cocktail.

Website: No official website.

Visit Plaza de la Revolución

This vast and historically significant square is a must-see for understanding modern Cuba. It has been the stage for numerous political rallies and speeches by Fidel Castro. The plaza is dominated by the José Martí Memorial, a towering 109-meter star-shaped monument. Visitors can take an elevator to the top for sweeping views of the city. Across the square are the iconic steel outlines of two of the revolution's most revered figures on the facades of government buildings: Che Guevara with the phrase 'Hasta la Victoria Siempre' (Until Victory, Always) and Camilo Cienfuegos with 'Vas Bien, Fidel' (You're doing well, Fidel). The sheer scale and stark modernist design of the plaza make it a powerful and humbling sight.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Vedado district. It's too far to walk from Old Havana, so a taxi (especially a classic car) is the best option.

Opening Hours: Plaza is open 24/7. The Martí memorial is open Monday-Saturday, 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM.

Cost: Free to visit the plaza. A fee of around $5 USD is charged to go up the memorial.

Website: No official website.

See a Performance at the Gran Teatro de La Habana

The Gran Teatro de La Habana 'Alicia Alonso' is one of the world's most beautiful and architecturally stunning opera houses. Located next to the Capitolio, its neo-baroque facade is adorned with stone and marble statues. It is home to the prestigious Cuban National Ballet, as well as the Cuban National Opera and other performances. Even if you don't see a show, the building itself is worth admiring from the outside. However, catching a world-class ballet performance for an incredibly low price is a fantastic cultural experience. The lavish, gilded interior and the talent on stage make for a truly memorable evening.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Paseo del Prado, next to Parque Central. Easily walkable from Old Havana.

Opening Hours: Box office hours vary. Performances are usually in the evenings on weekends. Guided tours are sometimes available during the day.

Cost: Performance tickets can be as low as $10-30 USD. Tours are around $5 USD.

Website: No consistent official website, check with the box office or a tourist information center.

Relax on Playas del Este

When you need a break from the city's heat and hustle, head to Playas del Este, a string of beautiful white-sand beaches just 30 minutes east of Havana. The most popular and well-serviced of these is Playa Santa María del Mar, which offers wide stretches of sand, clear turquoise water, and plenty of amenities. You can rent sunbeds and umbrellas, buy cheap coconuts and cocktails from beachside vendors, and enjoy a fresh seafood lunch at one of the simple 'ranchón' restaurants. It's where Habaneros go to escape the city, making it a great place to relax and people-watch.

Practical Information

How to Get There: The T3 tourist bus leaves from Parque Central every 30-40 minutes. A round-trip ticket costs about $5 USD. A taxi will cost around $20-25 USD each way.

Opening Hours: Accessible all day. Bus service runs from approximately 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM.

Cost: Bus fare or taxi, plus around $2-3 USD to rent a sunbed.

Website: No official website.

Planning Your Visit to Havana

Getting Around

Walking is the best way to explore compact areas like Old Havana. For longer distances, you have several options: 'almendrones' (classic American car taxis) for city tours and private trips; yellow state-run taxis (metered); 'colectivos' (shared taxis running fixed routes); and three-wheeled Coco Taxis for short, fun hops. For travel between cities, the Viazul bus service is a reliable option for tourists.

Where to Stay

The best way to experience local culture is by staying in a 'Casa Particular' (a private room or apartment rented from a Cuban family). For location, Habana Vieja (Old Havana) offers historic charm and proximity to major sights. Vedado is a more modern neighborhood with grand avenues, theaters, and a lively nightlife. Miramar is an upscale residential district with large hotels and embassies. Top hotel picks include the historic Hotel Nacional de Cuba and the modern Gran Hotel Manzana Kempinski La Habana.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Havana is during the dry season, which runs from November to April. During these months, you can expect pleasant, sunny weather with lower humidity and comfortable temperatures, perfect for exploring the city on foot. The wet season is from May to October, characterized by higher heat, humidity, and afternoon thunderstorms. Hurricane season peaks in August and September.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: Immerse yourself in Habana Vieja. Wander through Plaza Vieja, Plaza de Armas, and Plaza de la Catedral. Visit the Museo de la Revolución and have a mojito at La Bodeguita del Medio. In the evening, walk the Malecón at sunset. Day 2: Take a classic car tour in the morning, visiting Plaza de la Revolución and Vedado. Explore Callejón de Hamel for Afro-Cuban culture. In the evening, see a show at the Tropicana or the Gran Teatro de La Habana. Day 3: Take a morning trip to Fusterlandia. Return to the city for a cigar factory tour. Spend the afternoon relaxing at Playas del Este or shopping for souvenirs at the Almacenes San José Artisans' Market. Enjoy a final farewell dinner at a 'paladar' (private restaurant).

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Havana expensive?
Havana can be both affordable and expensive, depending on your travel style. Staying in 'Casas Particulares' (around $25-50 USD/night) and eating at local 'paladares' (private restaurants) is very budget-friendly. State-run luxury hotels and high-end restaurants will be significantly more expensive, with prices comparable to major US or European cities. Activities like tours and museum entries are generally affordable. Note that Cuba uses the Cuban Peso (CUP), and it's best to bring cash (Euros or Canadian Dollars are preferred for exchange) as US credit/debit cards do not work.
How many days do you need in Havana?
To fully experience the highlights of Havana without feeling rushed, 3 to 4 days is ideal. This allows you enough time to explore Old Havana thoroughly, visit key sites in Vedado and Centro Habana, take a classic car tour, and enjoy the nightlife. If you plan to take day trips to nearby places like Viñales (for tobacco plantations) or Varadero (for beaches), you should add at least one full day for each excursion.
Is Havana safe?
Havana is generally considered a very safe city for tourists, with a low rate of violent crime. The biggest concerns are petty crimes like pickpocketing or bag-snatching in crowded areas. Be aware of 'jineteros' (hustlers) who may try to sell you overpriced cigars or offer unsolicited guide services. A firm 'no, gracias' usually suffices. Use common sense, don't flash expensive items, and be cautious at night, just as you would in any major city. Cubans are overwhelmingly friendly and welcoming to visitors.
What is Havana famous for?
Havana is famous for a unique combination of cultural and historical elements. It's renowned for its fleet of 1950s classic American cars, world-class cigars (like Cohiba and Montecristo), and high-quality rum (like Havana Club). The city is a hub for music and dance, particularly salsa and Son Cubano. Architecturally, it's famous for the beautifully preserved colonial buildings of Old Havana, a UNESCO World Heritage site. Finally, it's known for its revolutionary history, embodied by figures like Che Guevara and Fidel Castro, and landmarks like Plaza de la Revolución.
