15 Best Things to Do in Seville, Spain (2025 Guide)

Drenched in sunshine and history, Seville is the heart of Andalusia, a city where fiery flamenco rhythms echo through ancient cobblestone streets. The air here is fragrant with orange blossoms, and every corner reveals a story, from magnificent Moorish palaces to vibrant tapas bars buzzing with life. As the capital of southern Spain, it effortlessly blends its rich past—seen in its grand cathedral and royal alcázars—with a forward-looking, passionate spirit. Whether you're getting lost in the labyrinthine Jewish Quarter, cruising the Guadalquivir River, or marveling at a modern architectural wonder, you'll quickly fall under its spell. Prepare to be enchanted as we explore the very best things to do in Seville, a city that truly captures the soul of Spain.

Top 5 Must-Visit Attractions

  • Real Alcázar de Sevilla
  • Seville Cathedral & La Giralda
  • Plaza de España
  • Barrio Santa Cruz
  • Metropol Parasol (Las Setas de Sevilla)

Complete Guide to Seville Attractions

Real Alcázar de Sevilla

A breathtaking testament to centuries of history, the Royal Alcázar of Seville is a stunning palace complex that remains the official residence of the Spanish royal family when they are in the city. Its origins trace back to a Moorish fort, but it was largely rebuilt in the 14th century in the Mudéjar style, a unique fusion of Islamic and Christian artistic traditions. The result is a jaw-dropping maze of ornate courtyards, intricate tilework (azulejos), gilded ceilings, and lush gardens with bubbling fountains. Fans of the TV series *Game of Thrones* will recognize its halls and gardens as the filming location for the Water Gardens of Dorne. The Patio de las Doncellas (Courtyard of the Maidens) and the Salón de Embajadores (Hall of Ambassadors) are particular highlights. Booking tickets in advance is essential to avoid exceptionally long queues, especially during peak season.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the city center, next to the Cathedral. Accessible on foot from most central locations. Patio de Banderas, s/n, 41004 Sevilla.

Opening Hours: October to March: 9:30 AM - 5:00 PM. April to September: 9:30 AM - 7:00 PM. Check website for holiday closures.

Cost: General admission approx. €14.50. Concessions available. Free entry on Mondays during the last hour.

Website: https://www.alcazarsevilla.org/

Seville Cathedral & La Giralda

The Cathedral of Saint Mary of the See, better known as Seville Cathedral, is a UNESCO World Heritage site and the largest Gothic cathedral in the world. Built on the site of a former grand mosque, its scale is meant to inspire awe, a declaration of the city's wealth and power after the Reconquista. Inside, you'll find the monumental tomb of Christopher Columbus, carried by four figures representing the kingdoms of Spain. The cavernous nave and stunning stained-glass windows are a spectacle of light and shadow. Adjacent to the cathedral stands its iconic bell tower, La Giralda. Originally the minaret of the mosque, it was so beautiful that it was preserved and repurposed. Instead of stairs, a series of 35 ramps lead to the top—wide enough for the muezzin to ride a horse up for the call to prayer. The climb is well worth it for the panoramic views over Seville's rooftops.

Practical Information

How to Get There: In the heart of the city, next to the Alcázar. Av. de la Constitución, s/n, 41004 Sevilla.

Opening Hours: Monday-Saturday: 10:45 AM - 5:00 PM; Sunday: 2:30 PM - 6:00 PM. Hours can vary for religious services.

Cost: General admission (includes Giralda) approx. €12. Free audio guide available with online ticket purchase.

Website: https://www.catedraldesevilla.es/

Plaza de España

Built for the Ibero-American Exposition of 1929, Plaza de España is one of Seville's most magnificent and beloved landmarks. This grand, semi-circular plaza is a stunning showcase of Spanish architectural styles, primarily Renaissance Revival and Moorish Revival (Neo-Mudéjar). A canal runs along its perimeter, crossed by four bridges representing the ancient kingdoms of Spain (Castile, Aragon, Navarre, and León), where you can rent small rowboats for a romantic paddle. The plaza's most famous features are the 48 tiled alcoves, or 'pabellones', lining the walls. Each alcove represents a different province of Spain, adorned with a beautifully crafted ceramic (azulejo) scene and a map. It's a popular spot for both locals and tourists to stroll, take photos, and enjoy the grandeur of the space.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located within María Luisa Park. A pleasant 15-20 minute walk from the Cathedral.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7, though park gates may close late at night.

Cost: Free to visit.

Website: N/A (Public space)

Barrio Santa Cruz

The Barrio Santa Cruz is the historic heart of Seville and its former Jewish Quarter. Getting lost in its labyrinth of narrow, winding streets is a quintessential Seville experience. The whitewashed houses, flower-filled balconies, and hidden plazas create a postcard-perfect scene at every turn. The air is often filled with the sounds of a distant Spanish guitar or the scent of jasmine and orange trees. This charming neighborhood is packed with history, from the tranquil Plaza de Santa Cruz to the legendary Callejón del Agua (Water Alley), which runs alongside the Alcázar's walls. It's the perfect place for a leisurely wander, discovering tiny tapas bars, artisan shops, and picturesque courtyards like the Patio de Banderas, which offers a classic view of the Giralda.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Centrally located, surrounding the Cathedral and Alcázar. Best explored on foot.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7.

Cost: Free to explore.

Website: N/A (Neighborhood)

Metropol Parasol (Las Setas de Sevilla)

A striking contrast to Seville's historic architecture, the Metropol Parasol, locally known as 'Las Setas' (The Mushrooms), is a massive wooden structure that claims to be the largest in the world. Designed by German architect Jürgen Mayer, this futuristic, waffle-like canopy revitalized the Plaza de la Encarnación. It's a multifunctional space, housing a local market and restaurants on the ground level and an archaeological museum in the basement. The main attraction is the rooftop walkway. A lift takes you to the top, where you can stroll along meandering paths offering spectacular 360-degree views of the city. Visiting at sunset is particularly magical, as you watch the sky change color and the city lights begin to twinkle, providing a modern perspective on historic Seville.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in Plaza de la Encarnación, about a 10-minute walk north of the Cathedral.

Opening Hours: Walkway: Daily from 9:30 AM - 12:00 AM (midnight).

Cost: Daytime visit approx. €10, Sunset/night visit approx. €15. Includes a small drink at the rooftop bar.

Website: https://setasdesevilla.com/

Triana Neighborhood

Cross the Isabel II Bridge (commonly known as Triana Bridge) from the city center, and you'll find yourself in the spirited and authentic neighborhood of Triana. Historically the city's Romani quarter and home to sailors, potters, and bullfighters, Triana has a character all its own. It is considered one of the birthplaces of flamenco, and its passion for the art form is palpable in its many 'tablaos' and ceramics workshops. Stroll along Calle Betis for beautiful views of Seville's skyline across the Guadalquivir River, especially at sunset. Explore the Mercado de Triana, a bustling market built on the ruins of an ancient castle, where you can sample local delicacies. Don't miss the Centro Cerámica Triana, a museum dedicated to the neighborhood's rich pottery tradition.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Walk across the Puente de Isabel II from the El Arenal neighborhood.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7. Shops and market have their own hours.

Cost: Free to explore.

Website: N/A (Neighborhood)

Parque de María Luisa

Seville's principal green space, the Parque de María Luisa, is a vast and enchanting public park stretching along the Guadalquivir River. Donated to the city in 1893 by the Infanta María Luisa Fernanda de Borbón, it was redesigned for the 1929 Ibero-American Exposition. It's a botanical wonderland, filled with palm and orange groves, elms, and Mediterranean pines, offering a shady respite from the Andalusian sun. The park is a romantic landscape of tiled fountains, ornamental ponds, and hidden pavilions. It's the perfect place for a peaceful walk, a picnic, or a ride in a horse-drawn carriage. The park is also home to the Plaza de España and the Plaza de América, earning it the nickname 'the park of the plazas'.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Adjacent to the Plaza de España, south of the historic center.

Opening Hours: Daily from 8:00 AM to 10:00 PM (or later in summer).

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: N/A (Public Park)

Torre del Oro

Standing guard on the banks of the Guadalquivir River, the Torre del Oro (Tower of Gold) is a 13th-century military watchtower. It was built by the Almohad Caliphate to control access to Seville via the river. A heavy chain was once stretched from its base across the water to another tower on the opposite bank to block enemy ships. Its name is rumored to come from a golden gleam projected by its original tile covering. Today, the tower houses a small but fascinating naval museum detailing the history of the Spanish navy and Seville's importance as a port city during the Age of Discovery. After exploring the exhibits, climb to the top for lovely views of the river, the Triana bridge, and the city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: On Paseo de Cristóbal Colón, a short walk from the Cathedral and Plaza de Toros.

Opening Hours: Monday-Friday: 9:30 AM - 6:45 PM; Saturday-Sunday: 10:30 AM - 6:45 PM.

Cost: General admission approx. €3. Free on Mondays.

Website: https://armada.defensa.gob.es/ArmadaPortal/page/Portal/ArmadaEspannola/ciencia_museo/prefLang-es/01_MuseoNavalSevilla

Plaza de Toros de la Real Maestranza

Whether you agree with the practice of bullfighting or not, the Plaza de Toros in Seville is an undeniable part of the city's cultural history and a magnificent piece of architecture. It is one of the oldest and most important bullrings in Spain, with construction beginning in 1761. The baroque-style ring, with its striking white and yellow arches, is a beautiful sight. A guided tour offers insight into the controversial tradition and the world of the 'matador'. You'll visit the chapel where bullfighters pray, the stables, and the main ring itself. The on-site museum displays paintings, costumes ('trajes de luces'), and memorabilia related to famous bullfights and fighters.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the El Arenal neighborhood, near the river and the Torre del Oro.

Opening Hours: Daily from 9:30 AM - 7:30 PM (hours vary on bullfight days).

Cost: Tour costs approx. €10.

Website: https://realmaestranza.com/

Casa de Pilatos

The Casa de Pilatos is arguably Seville's finest private palace and a stunning example of Andalusian civil architecture. Nicknamed 'Pilate's House' due to a belief that it was a replica of Pontius Pilate's residence in Jerusalem, the palace is a harmonious blend of Italian Renaissance and Spanish Mudéjar styles. It was built in the late 15th century by the Enríquez de Ribera family and is still the residence of the Dukes of Medinaceli. The palace is centered around a classic Andalusian patio, featuring a Genoese marble fountain and busts of Roman emperors. The walls are covered in over 150 different designs of azulejo tiles, considered one of the finest collections in the world. A visit offers a glimpse into the opulent lifestyle of Sevillian aristocracy.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the eastern part of the historic center. Plaza de Pilatos, 1, 41003 Sevilla.

Opening Hours: November to March: 9:00 AM - 6:00 PM. April to October: 9:00 AM - 7:00 PM.

Cost: Ground floor only approx. €10. Full house tour approx. €12.

Website: https://fundacionmedinaceli.org/monumentos/casa-de-pilatos/

Experience a Live Flamenco Show

You cannot visit the cradle of flamenco without experiencing its raw power and passion firsthand. This dramatic art form, combining song (cante), dance (baile), and guitar (toque), is an essential part of Andalusian culture. While you might stumble upon impromptu performances in the streets, seeing a professional show in an intimate 'tablao' is an unforgettable experience. There are many options, from large theaters to small, family-run venues. For an authentic experience, head to the Triana neighborhood or consider venues like the Museo del Baile Flamenco (Flamenco Dance Museum), which offers a show in its beautiful courtyard, or Tablao El Arenal for a classic, high-energy performance. The intensity, skill, and emotion on display will leave you breathless.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Venues are concentrated in Santa Cruz, El Arenal, and Triana neighborhoods.

Opening Hours: Shows are typically in the evening, with multiple performances (e.g., 7:00 PM, 9:00 PM).

Cost: Prices range from €20 to €50, sometimes including a drink or dinner.

Website: https://museodelbaileflamenco.com/ or https://tablaoelarenal.com/

Museo de Bellas Artes

Often called the 'second most important art gallery in Spain' after the Prado in Madrid, the Museo de Bellas Artes (Museum of Fine Arts) is a must-visit for art lovers. The museum is housed in the former Convento de la Merced, a beautiful 17th-century building with serene courtyards that are a work of art in themselves. Its collection focuses primarily on Spanish art from the medieval period to the 20th century, with a special emphasis on the masters of Seville's Golden Age. The galleries are filled with religious-themed works by masters like Zurbarán, Valdés Leal, and, most notably, Bartolomé Esteban Murillo. The room dedicated to Murillo's soft, ethereal paintings is a major highlight. It provides a peaceful and culturally enriching break from the city's bustling streets.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Plaza del Museo, 9, a 15-minute walk west of the Cathedral.

Opening Hours: Tuesday-Saturday: 9:00 AM - 9:00 PM; Sunday: 9:00 AM - 3:00 PM. Closed on Mondays.

Cost: €1.50 for non-EU citizens. Free for EU citizens.

Website: https://www.museodebellasartesdesevilla.gob.es/

Palacio de las Dueñas

Belonging to the esteemed House of Alba, one of Spain's most prominent aristocratic families, the Palacio de las Dueñas opened its doors to the public in 2016. This 15th-century palace is a treasure trove of art, history, and stunning architecture, mixing Gothic-Mudéjar styles with Renaissance touches. It was the favorite Seville residence of the late Duchess of Alba, a famously flamboyant and beloved figure in Spain. The palace is renowned for its magnificent patios and gardens, bursting with bougainvillea, citrus trees, and fountains. Inside, the rooms are filled with an eclectic collection of paintings, sculptures, tapestries, and personal belongings of the Alba family. It offers a more intimate and personal feel than many other historic sites.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Calle Dueñas, 5, near Las Setas.

Opening Hours: Daily. April to September: 10:00 AM - 8:00 PM; October to March: 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM.

Cost: General admission approx. €12. Free on non-holiday Mondays from 4:00 PM.

Website: https://www.lasduenas.es/

Mercado de Triana

For a true taste of local life, visit the Mercado de Triana. This vibrant indoor market is built on the historic site of the Castle of San Jorge, the former headquarters of the Spanish Inquisition. The ruins of the castle are visible through glass panels in the floor and in a small museum below the market. This historical weight adds a unique layer to the bustling, cheerful atmosphere of the market itself. Inside, you'll find stalls piled high with fresh produce, glistening seafood, cured meats (jamón!), and local cheeses. It's not just a place to shop for groceries; it's a social hub. Several small bars and eateries are tucked among the stalls, where you can enjoy incredibly fresh tapas and a cold beer or sherry. It's the perfect spot for a casual and delicious lunch.

Practical Information

How to Get There: At the end of the Triana Bridge (Puente de Isabel II) on the Triana side. Plaza del Altozano, s/n.

Opening Hours: Market stalls: Monday-Saturday, roughly 8:00 AM - 3:00 PM. Bars/restaurants stay open later.

Cost: Free to enter. Cost of food and drink is very reasonable.

Website: N/A (Public Market)

Archivo General de Indias

Completing the trio of UNESCO World Heritage sites alongside the Cathedral and Alcázar, the Archivo General de Indias (General Archive of the Indies) is a treasure for history buffs. Housed in a serene, stately Renaissance building, this archive holds an immensely valuable collection of documents illustrating the history of the Spanish Empire in the Americas and the Philippines. While you can't leaf through the ancient documents yourself—which include Christopher Columbus's personal journal and Ferdinand Magellan's maps—the archive features rotating exhibitions that showcase digitized and original copies of these priceless papers. The sheer volume of history contained within these walls, over 80 million pages, is staggering. It's a quiet, contemplative stop that provides profound context to Seville's golden age as the gateway to the New World.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located right between the Cathedral and the Alcázar.

Opening Hours: Tuesday-Saturday: 9:30 AM - 4:45 PM; Sunday: 10:00 AM - 1:45 PM. Closed Mondays.

Cost: Free admission.

Website: http://www.culturaydeporte.gob.es/cultura/archivos/sedes/archivo-general-de-indias.html

Planning Your Visit to Seville

Getting Around

Seville is a very walkable city, with most major attractions located in the flat, compact historic center. For longer distances, the TUSSAM public transport system is efficient, with an extensive bus network and a tram line (MetroCentro) serving the center. You can buy single tickets or a multi-trip card. Taxis and ride-sharing services are readily available. Sevici is the city's bike-sharing program, perfect for exploring the parks and riverside paths.

Where to Stay

For first-time visitors, Barrio Santa Cruz offers unbeatable charm and proximity to major sights, though it can be busy. El Arenal, between Santa Cruz and the river, is central and slightly less crowded. For a more authentic, local vibe, stay across the river in Triana, known for its flamenco and ceramics. Macarena, north of the center, offers more budget-friendly options. Recommended hotels include Hotel Alfonso XIII (Luxury), Hotel Casa 1800 Sevilla (Boutique), and The Corner House (Budget).

Best Time to Visit

The best times to visit Seville are in the spring (March to May) and fall (September to November). The weather is pleasantly warm, and the city is beautiful with blooming flowers. Spring hosts two major events: Semana Santa (Holy Week) and the Feria de Abril, offering unique cultural experiences but also higher prices and crowds. Summers (June to August) are extremely hot, with temperatures often exceeding 40°C (104°F). Winter is mild but can be rainy.

Suggested Itinerary

A simple 3-day itinerary: Day 1: Morning at the Seville Cathedral & La Giralda. Afternoon exploring the Real Alcázar. Evening getting lost in the streets of Barrio Santa Cruz, enjoying tapas and a flamenco show. Day 2: Morning at the magnificent Plaza de España and a stroll through Parque de María Luisa. Afternoon crossing the bridge to explore the Triana neighborhood, visiting its market and ceramics shops. Sunset drinks on Calle Betis. Day 3: Morning marveling at the views from Metropol Parasol (Las Setas). Mid-day visit to Casa de Pilatos or Museo de Bellas Artes. Afternoon for shopping on Calle Sierpes and a final stroll by the Torre del Oro and the river.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Seville expensive?
Compared to other major European destinations and Spanish cities like Madrid or Barcelona, Seville is quite affordable. Accommodation offers a wide range of prices. You can eat very well for a reasonable price by enjoying tapas; a tapa typically costs €3-€5, and 3-4 can make a meal. Major attractions have entry fees, but many offer free entry times. Overall, it's a great value-for-money destination.
How many days do you need in Seville?
To comfortably see the main sights without feeling rushed, 3 days is the ideal minimum. This allows you to dedicate ample time to the big three (Alcázar, Cathedral, Plaza de España) and still have time to explore neighborhoods like Santa Cruz and Triana. With 4 or 5 days, you can explore at a more leisurely pace, visit more museums and palaces, and perhaps take a day trip to nearby cities like Córdoba or Jerez.
Is Seville safe?
Seville is a very safe city for tourists. Violent crime is rare. The main concern, as in any major tourist city, is pickpocketing. Be extra vigilant in crowded areas like the Cathedral entrance, busy streets, and on public transport. Keep your valuables secure and be aware of your surroundings, especially at night. Otherwise, you can feel very comfortable walking around the city.
What is Seville famous for?
Seville is famous for being the heart of the Andalusia region and is renowned for its rich cultural heritage. Its most famous exports are flamenco music and dance, which originated here. Architecturally, it's known for its stunning Moorish and Mudéjar landmarks, especially the Royal Alcázar palace. It is also home to the world's largest Gothic cathedral, the tomb of Christopher Columbus, and the vibrant, historic neighborhoods of Santa Cruz and Triana. The city's legendary tapas culture and its massive spring festivals, Semana Santa and Feria de Abril, are also central to its fame.
