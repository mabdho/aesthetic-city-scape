15 Best Things to Do in Casablanca, Morocco (2025 Guide)

Welcome to Casablanca, Morocco's vibrant economic heart and largest city. Often seen as a mere entry point, 'Casa' offers a fascinating blend of modern ambition and deep-rooted tradition. Here, French colonial legacy is etched into grand boulevards and Mauresque architecture, while the pulse of contemporary Moroccan life thrums in its bustling markets and chic coastal districts. Beyond the cinematic romance, you'll find a city of striking contrasts, from the architectural marvel of the Hassan II Mosque standing defiant against the Atlantic to the labyrinthine alleys of the ancient medina. It's a place of commerce, art, and coastal charm, waiting to be explored. Whether you have one day or several, this guide will walk you through all the very best things to do in Casablanca, revealing a destination rich with authentic experiences and hidden gems.

Top 5 Must-Visit Attractions

  • Hassan II Mosque
  • Quartier Habous (New Medina)
  • Old Medina (Ancienne Medina)
  • La Corniche
  • Place Mohammed V

Complete Guide to Casablanca Attractions

Hassan II Mosque

Dominating the city's coastline, the Hassan II Mosque is an architectural masterpiece and one of the largest mosques in the world. Commissioned by King Hassan II and completed in 1993, its 210-meter-tall minaret is the second tallest in the world, acting as a beacon for the city. Part of the mosque is built on land reclaimed from the sea, and its roof is retractable, allowing worshippers to pray under the stars on special occasions. The intricate details, from hand-carved stone and wood to stunning marble floors and zellij tilework, represent the finest Moroccan craftsmanship. As one of the few mosques in Morocco open to non-Muslims, a guided tour is essential. The tours are conducted in multiple languages and provide fascinating insight into the building's construction, religious significance, and architectural symbolism. The sheer scale and beauty of the prayer hall are breathtaking, making this an unmissable highlight of any visit to Casablanca.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the Bd de la Corniche. Easily accessible by petit taxi from anywhere in the city.

Opening Hours: Guided tours for non-Muslims are scheduled outside of prayer times. Typically 9am, 10am, 11am, 12pm, 3pm, and 4pm (hours may vary, especially during Ramadan).

Cost: Approx. 130 MAD for adults, 65 MAD for students.

Website: https://www.fmh2.ma/en

Quartier Habous (New Medina)

The Quartier Habous, or New Medina, was built by the French in the 1930s as a solution to housing shortages. It was designed to blend traditional Moroccan architecture with French urban planning, resulting in a charming, clean, and well-organized medina. Its neat alleyways are lined with arcades, souks, and beautiful buildings that house everything from artisan workshops to modern cafes. It's a more relaxed shopping experience than the Old Medina, ideal for finding quality Moroccan goods like leather babouches, djellabas, spices, and pastries. Don't miss the olive souk, the brass and copperware artisans, and Pâtisserie Bennis, a legendary spot for authentic Moroccan sweets. The area's blend of Moorish and European styles makes for a delightful and photogenic wander.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located south of the city center. Best reached by petit taxi.

Opening Hours: Shops are generally open from 10am to 7pm. Some close for a midday break.

Cost: Free to wander.

Website: N/A

Old Medina (Ancienne Medina)

Step back in time in Casablanca's Old Medina, the original walled city predating the French protectorate. This labyrinth of narrow, winding streets is a world away from the modern boulevards surrounding it. While not as vast or polished as the medinas of Fes or Marrakech, it offers an authentic, gritty glimpse into traditional city life. Here, you'll find locals haggling for produce, artisans in tiny workshops, and children playing in hidden squares. The medina's northern bastion, the Sqala, offers great views of the port and the Hassan II Mosque. It's a place to get lost and observe daily life, but it's wise to be mindful of your belongings and visit during daylight hours for the best experience.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located just north of Place des Nations Unies. Easily walkable from the city center.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7, but shops and stalls operate roughly from 9am to 8pm.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: N/A

La Corniche

Stretching along the Atlantic coast, La Corniche is Casablanca's glamorous seaside promenade. Located in the affluent Anfa district, this is where locals and tourists come to see and be seen. The boulevard is lined with beach clubs, swimming pools, luxury hotels, restaurants, and trendy nightclubs. During the day, it’s a popular spot for jogging, strolling, and enjoying the ocean breeze. You can relax at a public beach or pay for entry to a private beach club for a more exclusive experience with pools and amenities. As evening approaches, the area comes alive with people flocking to its many bars and restaurants to enjoy dinner with a view of the sunset. It offers a modern, Westernized side of Casablanca, contrasting sharply with the historic medina.

Practical Information

How to Get There: West of the Hassan II Mosque. A petit taxi is the easiest way to get there.

Opening Hours: Public access 24/7. Businesses have their own hours.

Cost: Free to walk. Beach clubs charge entry fees (approx. 100-200 MAD).

Website: N/A

Place Mohammed V

Place Mohammed V is the administrative and symbolic heart of modern Casablanca. This grand central plaza is surrounded by impressive public buildings constructed during the French colonial era, showcasing a unique architectural style known as Mauresque. This style beautifully melds traditional Moroccan design elements with European Art Deco. Key buildings include the Wilaya (government offices), the courthouse (Palais de Justice), the Bank Al-Maghrib, and the main post office. The square features a large central fountain and is a hub of activity, especially in the evening when locals gather. It’s a fantastic place to admire the city's architectural identity and take photos, particularly of the clock tower and the intricate facades of the surrounding structures.

Practical Information

How to Get There: In the city center, easily accessible by the Casa Tramway (Mohammed V station) or by foot from most central hotels.

Opening Hours: Public square, open 24/7.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Rick's Café

While the classic film 'Casablanca' was shot entirely in Hollywood studios, Rick's Café brings the movie's iconic gin joint to life. Opened in 2004 by a former American diplomat, this beautifully restored riad is a meticulous recreation of the bar made famous by Humphrey Bogart and Ingrid Bergman. The atmosphere is thick with 1940s nostalgia, featuring a grand piano, curved archways, intricate tilework, and moody lighting. More than just a tourist gimmick, Rick's offers a high-quality dining experience with a menu of Moroccan and international cuisine. A jazz pianist plays classic tunes, including 'As Time Goes By,' nightly. Reservations are highly recommended, especially for dinner. It's a chance to indulge in a bit of cinematic fantasy in the city that inspired it.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at 248 Boulevard Sour Jdid, on the edge of the Old Medina.

Opening Hours: Open daily for lunch (12pm-3pm) and dinner (6:30pm-1am).

Cost: Expensive. Main courses range from 180-300 MAD. Drinks are also pricey.

Website: http://www.rickscafe.ma/

Cathédrale Sacré-Cœur

A surprising and beautiful landmark in a predominantly Muslim city, the Cathédrale Sacré-Cœur (Sacred Heart Cathedral) is a former Roman Catholic cathedral built in the 1930s. Its stunning design is a masterpiece of Neo-Gothic architecture blended with Moroccan decorative elements and Art Deco influences. Though it was deconsecrated and ceased its religious function after Morocco gained independence in 1956, its soaring white facade and twin towers remain an iconic part of Casablanca's skyline. Today, the cathedral is used as a cultural center, hosting art exhibitions and events. Visitors can admire its grand exterior and, when open, explore its vast, empty interior, which highlights the unique architectural fusion. The play of light through its stained-glass windows is particularly beautiful.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located near the Parc de la Ligue Arabe in the city center.

Opening Hours: Interior access is sporadic and depends on current exhibitions. The exterior can be viewed anytime.

Cost: Free to view from the outside. A small fee may apply for exhibitions.

Website: N/A

Musée Abderrahman Slaoui

Housed in a charming Art Deco villa, the Abderrahman Slaoui Museum is a hidden gem dedicated to Moroccan arts and culture. The museum showcases the private collection of the late Moroccan businessman Abderrahman Slaoui, featuring an eclectic and fascinating array of objects. The collection includes magnificent antique Moroccan jewelry, intricate kaftans, powerful Orientalist travel posters, and delicate perfume flasks. Each room is thoughtfully curated, offering an intimate and personal look into Moroccan heritage and craftsmanship. It provides a quiet and cultured escape from the city's bustle and is a must-visit for anyone interested in Moroccan decorative arts and history.

Practical Information

How to Get There: 12 Rue Du Parc, a short walk from Parc de la Ligue Arabe.

Opening Hours: Tuesday - Saturday: 10am - 6pm; Sunday: 10am - 1pm; Closed Mondays.

Cost: Approx. 40 MAD for adults.

Website: https://www.musee-as.ma/

Villa des Arts de Casablanca

The Villa des Arts is one of Casablanca's premier contemporary art museums, set in a magnificent 1934 Art Deco building. Operated by the ONA Foundation, the museum is dedicated to promoting modern Moroccan and international art. It hosts a dynamic program of rotating temporary exhibitions, showcasing paintings, sculptures, photography, and multimedia installations from both established and emerging artists. The villa itself is a work of art, with elegant lines and a beautiful garden that provides a tranquil setting for the exhibitions. It's an essential stop for art lovers looking to understand the contemporary creative scene in Morocco.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at 30 Boulevard Brahim Roudani, near the Parc de la Ligue Arabe.

Opening Hours: Tuesday - Sunday: 9:30am - 7:30pm; Closed Mondays.

Cost: Free admission.

Website: http://www.fondationona.ma/

Marché Central (Central Market)

For a vibrant and authentic slice of Casablanca life, head to the Marché Central. This bustling open-air market is where locals shop for their daily needs, and it's a feast for the senses. The stalls are piled high with fresh produce, fragrant spices, colorful flowers, and a staggering array of fresh seafood straight from the Atlantic. The seafood section is particularly famous, where you can buy fish, shrimp, oysters, and sea urchins. One of the market's best features is the cluster of small, no-frills restaurants. You can purchase your seafood directly from a vendor and have it grilled or fried on the spot for a delicious and incredibly fresh lunch. It's a chaotic, lively, and unmissable culinary experience.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Boulevard Mohammed V, near the city center.

Opening Hours: Daily from early morning until around 2pm-3pm.

Cost: Free to browse. Cost of food is very reasonable.

Website: N/A

Morocco Mall

Situated at the far end of the Corniche, the Morocco Mall is one of the largest shopping centers in Africa. This massive, modern complex offers a stark contrast to the city's traditional souks. It houses hundreds of international brands, from high-street fashion to luxury labels, as well as a large Galeries Lafayette department store. Beyond shopping, the mall is a major entertainment destination. Its centerpiece is a huge central aquarium (Aquadream) with a glass elevator that takes you through the middle of the tank. It also features an indoor souk, an IMAX cinema, a children's adventure park, and a large food court with an outdoor musical fountain. It's a glimpse into the modern, consumer-driven side of Casablanca.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at the end of the Corniche. Accessible by taxi or the Casa Tramway (terminus station).

Opening Hours: Daily from 10am to 9pm (shops); restaurants and cinema stay open later.

Cost: Free to enter. Attractions inside have separate fees.

Website: https://www.moroccomall.ma/en

Parc de la Ligue Arabe

Casablanca's largest public park, the Parc de la Ligue Arabe, is a welcome green oasis in the heart of the bustling city. Originally designed in 1918, it features long avenues of towering palm trees, manicured lawns, and formal flowerbeds. It's a popular spot for locals to escape the urban grind, go for a walk, or relax on a bench with a book. While some parts of the park have seen better days, recent renovation efforts are restoring its former glory. It's a pleasant place for a quiet stroll and is conveniently located next to other key sights like the Cathédrale Sacré-Cœur and the Villa des Arts, making it easy to incorporate into a day of sightseeing.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the city center, easily walkable from Place Mohammed V.

Opening Hours: Open daily during daylight hours.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Mahkama du Pacha (Pasha's Courthouse)

An architectural jewel hidden within the Quartier Habous, the Mahkama du Pacha is a stunning example of Hispano-Moorish design. Built in the 1940s, it served as a courthouse and reception hall for the Pasha of Casablanca. Its interior is a breathtaking display of traditional Moroccan craftsmanship, featuring intricate zellij tilework, carved cedarwood ceilings, white marble columns, and elegant stucco work. Although it is still an active government building (prefecture offices), it is sometimes possible for tourists to gain entry. You may need to ask a guard for permission, and a small tip is often expected. If you can get inside, the magnificent courtyards and grand halls make it well worth the effort. It's considered one of the most beautiful administrative buildings in the country.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Quartier Habous, near the Royal Palace.

Opening Hours: Standard government office hours (approx. 8:30am - 4:30pm, Mon-Fri). Access is not guaranteed and depends on the guards' discretion.

Cost: Officially free, but a tip (20-50 MAD) for the guard is customary.

Website: N/A

Shrine of Sidi Abderrahman

For a truly unique and off-the-beaten-path experience, visit the Shrine of Sidi Abderrahman. This small village and marabout (shrine of a holy man) is built on a rocky outcrop just off the Corniche coast, accessible only by a small bridge during low tide. The shrine itself is reserved for Muslims, but visitors are welcome to explore the tiny, whitewashed village that has sprung up around it. The village is a photogenic jumble of houses, and it is home to a community of soothsayers and fortune-tellers who cater to pilgrims seeking blessings or cures. It's a fascinating look at local beliefs and superstitions, set against the dramatic backdrop of the crashing Atlantic waves.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located off the Corniche, past the Anfa Place shopping center. Best reached by taxi.

Opening Hours: Accessible during low tide. Best visited during daylight hours.

Cost: Free to walk around the village. Donations are welcome.

Website: N/A

Royal Palace of Casablanca

While you cannot enter the Royal Palace, viewing its magnificent exterior is an experience in itself. This is one of several palaces owned by the King of Morocco across the country and serves as his main residence when in Casablanca. The palace is a vast complex surrounded by high walls and guarded by the smartly dressed Royal Guard. The main gates are an impressive sight, showcasing elaborate Islamic architectural details and intricate tilework. Located near the Quartier Habous, it's easy to stop by for a photo while exploring the area. Remember to be respectful, as it is a secure royal property; do not take photos of the guards without permission.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Adjacent to the Quartier Habous. A petit taxi can take you to the main gate area.

Opening Hours: Not open to the public. Exterior view only.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Planning Your Visit to Casablanca

Getting Around

Casablanca is a large, sprawling city. For short distances in the center, walking is feasible. 'Petit Taxis' (small, red taxis) are metered and ideal for trips within the city limits; always insist on the meter. 'Grand Taxis' (larger, white cars) operate on fixed routes and are shared. The Casa Tramway is modern, efficient, and great for connecting key areas like the city center, the Corniche, and the train stations.

Where to Stay

For first-time visitors, the city center near Place Mohammed V and the Art Deco district offers convenient access to many sights and the tram. For a luxury experience with ocean views, the Corniche in the Anfa district is packed with high-end hotels and beach clubs. The area around Quartier Habous provides a more local, charming atmosphere, though it is further from the main attractions.

Best Time to Visit

The best times to visit Casablanca are during the spring (April to May) and autumn (September to October). During these months, the weather is pleasantly warm and sunny, perfect for exploring the city. Summers (June to August) can be very hot and humid, while winters (November to February) are mild but can be rainy and gray.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: Start at the magnificent Hassan II Mosque (take a tour). Stroll along La Corniche, enjoy lunch with an ocean view, and perhaps relax at a beach club. In the evening, visit the Old Medina and have a nostalgic dinner at Rick's Café. Day 2: Explore the charming Quartier Habous (New Medina). Try to visit the Mahkama du Pacha and see the exterior of the Royal Palace. Head to the Marché Central for a fresh seafood lunch. In the afternoon, admire the architecture at Place Mohammed V and the Cathédrale Sacré-Cœur. Day 3: Dive into art and culture at the Villa des Arts and the Musée Abderrahman Slaoui. Spend the afternoon shopping and exploring the entertainment options at the Morocco Mall. Enjoy a final Moroccan dinner in the city center.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Casablanca expensive?
Casablanca is moderately expensive compared to other Moroccan cities like Fes or Chefchaouen, but generally more affordable than major European or North American cities. Accommodation ranges from budget-friendly hostels to luxury hotels. Eating at local cafes and markets is very cheap, while dining at upscale restaurants, especially along the Corniche or at places like Rick's Café, can be pricey. Taxis are inexpensive if you ensure the meter is used.
How many days do you need in Casablanca?
Two to three days are generally sufficient to experience the main highlights of Casablanca. This allows enough time to visit the Hassan II Mosque, explore both the Old and New Medinas, stroll the Corniche, and see the central Art Deco architecture without feeling rushed. Many travelers use it as a 1 or 2-day stop before heading to more tourist-focused destinations like Marrakech or Rabat.
Is Casablanca safe?
Casablanca is generally safe for tourists, but like any large city, it requires common sense and awareness. Petty crime, such as pickpocketing and bag snatching, can occur in crowded areas like the Old Medina and markets. It's advisable to dress modestly, avoid wearing flashy jewelry, and keep valuables secure. Stick to well-lit main streets at night and be cautious of unsolicited guides. Using official taxis is recommended over unmarked cars.
What is Casablanca famous for?
Casablanca is most famous for several key things: the spectacular Hassan II Mosque, one of the world's largest; its status as Morocco's economic and business capital; its impressive collection of French colonial and Art Deco architecture; and, of course, the enduring romantic legacy of the 1942 classic film 'Casablanca,' even though it was filmed elsewhere.
