15 Best Things to Do in Lyon, France (2025 Guide)

Nestled at the confluence of the Rhône and Saône rivers, Lyon is France's vibrant third-largest city and its undisputed gastronomic capital. A city of captivating contrasts, it boasts a 2,000-year history, from the Roman ruins on Fourvière Hill to the Renaissance elegance of Vieux Lyon, a UNESCO World Heritage site. Beyond its culinary prowess and historic charm, Lyon is a hub of innovation, art, and a thriving cultural scene. Whether you're navigating the secret 'traboules' passageways, indulging in a traditional bouchon, or relaxing in one of its sprawling parks, you'll find a city that effortlessly blends its rich heritage with a forward-thinking spirit. This guide will walk you through all the very best things to do in Lyon, ensuring an unforgettable journey into the heart of French culture.

Top 5 Must-Visit Attractions

  • Vieux Lyon (Old Lyon)
  • Basilique Notre-Dame de Fourvière
  • Les Halles Paul Bocuse
  • Parc de la Tête d'Or
  • Musée des Confluences

Complete Guide to Lyon Attractions

Vieux Lyon (Old Lyon)

Step back in time in Vieux Lyon, one of Europe's most extensive Renaissance neighborhoods and a UNESCO World Heritage site. This enchanting district is a labyrinth of cobblestone streets, pastel-colored buildings, and hidden courtyards (cours). It's divided into three sections: Saint-Jean, the medieval heart with its famous cathedral; Saint-Paul, home to historic puppet theaters; and Saint-Georges, once the silk weavers' district. Exploring Vieux Lyon is about more than just admiring the architecture; it's about getting lost in its atmosphere. Duck into a traditional 'bouchon' for an authentic Lyonnaise meal, discover artisan shops, and hunt for the secret 'traboules' – hidden passageways that weave through buildings, connecting one street to another. It's a truly magical experience that captures the essence of historic Lyon.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the west bank of the Saône river. Accessible via Metro Line D (Vieux Lyon – Cathédrale Saint-Jean station).

Opening Hours: The district is open 24/7. Shops and restaurants have their own hours, typically 10:00 AM - 7:00 PM.

Cost: Free to explore.

Website: https://en.lyon-france.com/discover-lyon/heritage-unesco/vieux-lyon-old-lyon-district

Basilique Notre-Dame de Fourvière

Dominating the city skyline from Fourvière Hill, the Basilique Notre-Dame de Fourvière is Lyon's most iconic landmark. This opulent 19th-century basilica, often affectionately called the 'upside-down elephant' by locals due to its four main towers, showcases a stunning mix of Byzantine and Romanesque architectural styles. The interior is breathtaking, adorned with intricate mosaics, brilliant stained-glass windows, and gilded surfaces that tell the story of the Virgin Mary and the history of France. Aside from its architectural splendor, the basilica offers the most spectacular panoramic views of Lyon, the two rivers, and on a clear day, the distant Alps. You can take a rooftop tour for an even more dramatic perspective. The basilica sits on the site of the original Roman forum, making Fourvière Hill the historical heart of the city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take the Funicular (F2 line) from Vieux Lyon station up to Fourvière.

Opening Hours: Daily 7:00 AM - 7:00 PM. Rooftop tours have specific times and fees.

Cost: Free to enter the basilica. A fee applies for guided tours and rooftop access.

Website: https://www.fourviere.org/en/

Les Halles Paul Bocuse

For food lovers, Les Halles Paul Bocuse is a non-negotiable pilgrimage. This legendary indoor market is a temple of French gastronomy, named after the city's most famous chef. It brings together over 50 of the region's finest producers, cheese-makers (fromagers), butchers (bouchers), pastry chefs (pâtissiers), and chocolatiers under one roof. The quality and variety are astounding, from Bresse chicken and Charolais beef to St-Marcellin cheese and pink praline tarts. The atmosphere is electric, buzzing with locals doing their weekly shopping and tourists marveling at the displays. You can simply wander and sample delicacies, or pull up a stool at one of the several on-site restaurants and oyster bars for an unforgettable meal, surrounded by the very best ingredients.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Part-Dieu district. Accessible via Tram T1 (Halles Paul Bocuse station) or Metro B (Place Guichard station).

Opening Hours: Tuesday to Saturday: 7:00 AM - 7:00 PM; Sunday: 7:00 AM - 4:30 PM. Closed Mondays.

Cost: Free to enter. Cost of food and drink varies widely.

Website: https://www.halles-paulbocuse.com/

Parc de la Tête d'Or

The 'Park of the Golden Head' is Lyon's magnificent urban green space, spanning 117 hectares. It's the perfect escape from the city bustle, offering something for everyone. Designed in the style of an English garden, it features a large lake where you can rent paddle boats, vast rolling lawns for picnics, and shady paths for strolling or jogging. The park is also home to a free zoo, one of the largest botanical gardens in France, and a beautiful rose garden. Whether you want to admire exotic plants, watch giraffes and red pandas, or simply relax by the water, the Parc de la Tête d'Or provides a full day of leisure and natural beauty.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Accessible via Metro A (Masséna station) or multiple bus lines (C1, C2, C6).

Opening Hours: Daily 6:30 AM - 10:30 PM (summer), 6:30 AM - 8:30 PM (winter). Zoo and botanical gardens have shorter hours.

Cost: Free entry to the park, zoo, and botanical gardens.

Website: https://www.loisirs-parcdelatetedor.com/

Musée des Confluences

Situated at the very tip of the Presqu'île where the Rhône and Saône rivers meet, the Musée des Confluences is as remarkable for its architecture as its collections. This futuristic structure of glass, concrete, and steel resembles a crystal cloud and aims to explore the story of humankind and the history of life. It's a museum of science and anthropology, but presented in a wonderfully engaging way. The permanent exhibition is organized around four key questions: Where do we come from? Who are we? What do we do? and What's next? The exhibits blend natural history specimens like dinosaur skeletons with anthropological artifacts from across the globe, creating a fascinating dialogue between different cultures and disciplines. It's a thought-provoking experience for all ages.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take Tram T1 to the terminus station, Musée des Confluences.

Opening Hours: Tuesday to Sunday: 10:30 AM - 6:30 PM. Closed Mondays.

Cost: Around €9 for adults. Free with the Lyon City Card.

Website: https://www.museedesconfluences.fr/en

Traboules of Vieux Lyon & Croix-Rousse

The traboules are one of Lyon's most unique and fascinating features. These hidden passageways, primarily found in the Vieux Lyon and Croix-Rousse districts, cut through buildings and courtyards, creating a secret network of shortcuts. Historically, they were used by silk workers (canuts) in Croix-Rousse to transport their delicate fabrics sheltered from the rain, and by residents of Vieux Lyon to quickly get to the river. Finding and exploring these traboules feels like a treasure hunt. Look for small, unassuming doors and push them gently – many are open to the public during the day. Following the signs for the most famous traboules, like the 'Cour des Voraces' in Croix-Rousse, is a must. Remember to be quiet and respectful, as these are residential buildings.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Best explored on foot in Vieux Lyon (Metro D) and Croix-Rousse (Metro C).

Opening Hours: Generally open to the public from early morning until around 7:00 PM. Access can be intermittent.

Cost: Free.

Website: https://en.lyon-france.com/discover-lyon/heritage-unesco/the-traboules-secret-passageways

Presqu'île District

The Presqu'île, meaning 'almost island', is the vibrant peninsula of land between the Rhône and Saône rivers that forms Lyon's modern city center. This UNESCO-listed area is the hub of shopping, dining, and culture. It's anchored by the vast Place Bellecour at its southern end and the magnificent Place des Terreaux to the north, home to the City Hall and the Fine Arts Museum. Stroll down the pedestrianized Rue de la République, Lyon's main shopping street, lined with both international brands and classic French department stores. Explore the opulent architecture of the 19th-century buildings, discover charming squares, and enjoy the lively atmosphere of its countless cafes and restaurants. It's the beating heart of contemporary Lyon.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Central and highly accessible. Key metro stations include Bellecour (Lines A, D), Cordeliers (Line A), and Hôtel de Ville (Lines A, C).

Opening Hours: Area is open 24/7. Shops generally open 10:00 AM - 7:30 PM.

Cost: Free to explore.

Website: https://en.lyon-france.com/discover-lyon/shopping/presqu-ile-the-shopping-peninsula

Musée des Beaux-Arts de Lyon

Housed in a former 17th-century Benedictine convent, the Musée des Beaux-Arts (Museum of Fine Arts) is one of the largest art museums in France. Its impressive collections span from ancient Egypt to modern art, offering a comprehensive journey through art history. The serene central courtyard, with its garden and sculptures, is an oasis of calm in the bustling Place des Terreaux. The museum's galleries feature masterpieces by European painters such as Rembrandt, Rubens, Veronese, and Poussin, as well as a significant collection of Impressionist works by Monet, Degas, and Renoir. The sculpture wing is equally impressive, with works by Rodin and Bourdelle. It's a must-visit for any art enthusiast.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Place des Terreaux. Metro stop: Hôtel de Ville - Louis Pradel (Lines A, C).

Opening Hours: Wednesday to Monday: 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM. Closed Tuesdays.

Cost: Around €8 for permanent collections. Free with the Lyon City Card.

Website: https://www.mba-lyon.fr/en

Roman Theatres of Fourvière

Adjacent to the famous basilica on Fourvière Hill lies a remarkable testament to Lyon's Roman past. The city, then known as Lugdunum, was the capital of the Gauls, and this archaeological site contains two beautifully preserved Roman structures. The Grand Theatre, built around 15 BC, is the oldest in France and once held up to 10,000 spectators. Next to it is the smaller Odeon, which was used for musical performances and poetry readings. Visitors can freely walk among the ancient stones, sit on the tiered seating, and imagine the spectacles that took place here two millennia ago. The site is still used today for the 'Nuits de Fourvière' summer festival, bringing music, dance, and theater back to this historic stage. Don't miss the adjacent Gallo-Roman Museum, built cleverly into the hillside.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take the Funicular (F1 or F2) from Vieux Lyon and get off at Minimes - Théâtres Romains or Fourvière.

Opening Hours: Tuesday to Sunday: 7:00 AM - 7:00 PM (until 9 PM in summer). Closed Mondays.

Cost: Free to access the outdoor theatre site. The adjacent museum has an entrance fee.

Website: https://lugdunum.grandlyon.com/en/

La Croix-Rousse Neighborhood

Known as 'the hill that works' in contrast to Fourvière's 'hill that prays', La Croix-Rousse was the heart of Lyon's booming silk industry in the 19th century. Today, this district retains a unique village-like atmosphere with a bohemian, artistic spirit. Its steep, winding streets are lined with tall buildings specifically designed with high ceilings and large windows to accommodate the massive looms of the silk weavers (canuts). Explore the daily market on the Boulevard de la Croix-Rousse, discover independent designer boutiques, and hunt for more of the city's famous traboules. The area offers a different, more local perspective on Lyon, with a strong sense of community and a creative energy that's palpable in its cafes, artist workshops, and street art.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take Metro Line C to the Croix-Rousse or Hénon stations.

Opening Hours: Area is always accessible. Shops and market have their own hours.

Cost: Free to explore.

Website: https://en.lyon-france.com/discover-lyon/heritage-unesco/the-croix-rousse-a-village-within-the-city

Institut Lumière

Lyon is the birthplace of cinema, and the Institut Lumière pays homage to its inventors, Auguste and Louis Lumière. The museum is located in the magnificent Villa Lumière, the former family home of the brothers. It chronicles the invention of the Cinematograph and the dawn of filmmaking through a fascinating collection of early cameras, projectors, and photographs. Next to the museum is the Hangar du Premier-Film, the actual site of the Lumière brothers' factory and where they shot their first film, 'Workers Leaving the Lumière Factory,' in 1895. The institute also includes a cinema that screens a fantastic program of classic and contemporary films. It's a pilgrimage site for any cinephile.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Monplaisir district. Take Metro Line D to Monplaisir-Lumière station.

Opening Hours: Tuesday to Sunday: 10:00 AM - 6:30 PM. Closed Mondays.

Cost: Around €8 for the museum. Separate tickets for film screenings.

Website: https://www.institut-lumiere.org/

Mur des Canuts

One of Lyon's most impressive pieces of public art, the Mur des Canuts (Wall of the Silk Weavers) is a massive and incredibly detailed trompe-l'œil mural in the Croix-Rousse neighborhood. Covering 1,200 square meters, it's one of the largest murals in Europe. It depicts a vivid scene of daily life in the Croix-Rousse district, complete with buildings, staircases, shops, and local residents. The mural is a living artwork; it has been updated several times since its creation in 1987 to reflect the changing neighborhood and its people. The level of detail is astonishing, and it's fun to spot the different characters and scenes within the painting. It perfectly captures the unique spirit of the Croix-Rousse hill.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at the corner of Boulevard des Canuts and Rue Denfert Rochereau. Metro stop: Hénon (Line C).

Opening Hours: 24/7.

Cost: Free.

Website: https://www.lyon-france.com/je-decouvre-lyon/culture-et-musees/les-murs-peints/le-mur-des-canuts

Place Bellecour

As one of the largest open squares in Europe, Place Bellecour is an unmissable landmark on the Presqu'île. This vast, uncluttered square, with its distinctive red gravel, is the city's central point (Kilometer 0). In the center stands an equestrian statue of King Louis XIV. The square offers fantastic views up to the Fourvière Basilica on the hill. Aside from being a major transport hub and meeting point, Place Bellecour hosts various events throughout the year, from concerts and markets to the iconic Ferris wheel that often appears during the winter holidays. The Lyon Tourist Office is also conveniently located here, making it a great starting point for your exploration of the city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Metro station Bellecour, served by lines A and D.

Opening Hours: 24/7.

Cost: Free.

Website: https://en.lyon-france.com/discover-lyon/must-see-sites-and-districts/places-squares/place-bellecour

Cathédrale Saint-Jean-Baptiste

The main cathedral of Lyon, located in the heart of the Vieux Lyon district, is a beautiful example of architecture that transitions from Romanesque to Gothic style. Construction spanned from the 12th to the 15th century, and its facade is adorned with intricate carvings and statues. Inside, the soaring nave and stunning stained-glass windows from the 13th century create a serene and awe-inspiring atmosphere. One of the cathedral's most famous features is its 14th-century astronomical clock. A marvel of medieval engineering, it still chimes four times a day (12 PM, 2 PM, 3 PM, and 4 PM), putting on a brief show with its moving figures. The cathedral is the historical religious heart of the city and a cornerstone of the Vieux Lyon experience.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Metro station Vieux Lyon – Cathédrale Saint-Jean (Line D).

Opening Hours: Generally open daily, but hours can vary. Check the website for specific times and mass schedules. Clock chimes at 12, 2, 3, 4 PM.

Cost: Free.

Website: https://cathedraledelyon.fr/

Les Berges du Rhône (Rhône Riverbanks)

The redeveloped banks of the Rhône River offer a fantastic recreational space stretching for several kilometers. This modern, pedestrian-friendly promenade is a favorite spot for locals to walk, jog, cycle, and socialize. The area is dotted with lawns, gardens, playgrounds, and even houseboats (péniches) that have been converted into lively bars and restaurants. On a sunny day, the Berges du Rhône are buzzing with activity. It's the perfect place for a leisurely stroll with great views of the Hôtel-Dieu hospital and the distant Fourvière hill. You can rent a bike from the city's Velo'v service and cycle the entire length, stopping for a drink or an ice cream along the way. It represents the modern, relaxed side of Lyonnaise life.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Accessible from multiple points along the river's left bank. Key access points near bridges like Pont de la Guillotière and Pont Wilson.

Opening Hours: 24/7.

Cost: Free.

Website: https://en.lyon-france.com/discover-lyon/must-see-sites-and-districts/rivers-and-river-banks/the-rhone-river-and-its-banks

Planning Your Visit to Lyon

Getting Around

Lyon has an excellent public transportation system (TCL) including four metro lines, trams, buses, and two funiculars up Fourvière hill. A single ticket works across the network. The city is also very walkable, especially the Presqu'île and Vieux Lyon districts. For cycling, the Velo'v bike-sharing system is widespread and easy to use.

Where to Stay

For historic charm, stay in Vieux Lyon. For a central location with shopping and dining, choose the Presqu'île (around Place Bellecour or Cordeliers). La Croix-Rousse offers a bohemian, village-like atmosphere with great views. The Part-Dieu area is convenient for train travelers and business, with many modern hotels.

Best Time to Visit

The best times to visit Lyon are spring (April-June) and autumn (September-October) when the weather is pleasant and the city is vibrant. Summer (July-August) can be hot and crowded. Winter is cold but magical, especially in early December during the world-famous Fête des Lumières (Festival of Lights).

Suggested Itinerary

A simple 3-day itinerary: Day 1: Explore Fourvière Hill (Basilica, Roman Theatres), then descend into Vieux Lyon for the afternoon, exploring traboules and the Cathedral. Enjoy dinner at a traditional bouchon. Day 2: Discover the Presqu'île, starting at Place Bellecour, walking up Rue de la République to Place des Terreaux and the Musée des Beaux-Arts. Have an amazing lunch at Les Halles Paul Bocuse. Day 3: Head up to the Croix-Rousse district to see the Mur des Canuts and its market. In the afternoon, relax at the Parc de la Tête d'Or. End the day with a stroll or drink along the Berges du Rhône.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Lyon expensive?
Lyon is generally more affordable than Paris. While you can find high-end dining and luxury hotels, there are many budget-friendly options. Eating out can range from inexpensive boulangeries and market stalls to mid-range bouchons and fine dining. Public transport is efficient and reasonably priced, and many key attractions are free to explore on foot.
How many days do you need in Lyon?
A stay of 3 to 4 days is ideal. This allows you enough time to explore the main districts like Vieux Lyon, Presqu'île, and Croix-Rousse at a relaxed pace, visit several key museums, and fully immerse yourself in the city's renowned culinary scene without feeling rushed.
Is Lyon safe?
Lyon is considered a very safe city for tourists. As with any major European city, you should take standard precautions against pickpocketing, especially in crowded areas like transport hubs, markets, and the main tourist districts. Use common sense, be aware of your surroundings, and your visit should be trouble-free.
What is Lyon famous for?
Lyon is most famous for being the gastronomic capital of France, home to legendary chef Paul Bocuse and traditional 'bouchon' restaurants. It's also known for its rich history, particularly its well-preserved Renaissance old town (Vieux Lyon), its role in the silk industry (the 'canuts' and 'traboules'), being the birthplace of cinema (the Lumière brothers), and its spectacular annual Festival of Lights (Fête des Lumières).
