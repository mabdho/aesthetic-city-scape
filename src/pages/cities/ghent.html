15 Best Things to Do in Ghent, Belgium (2025 Guide)

Often described as Europe's best-kept secret, Ghent is a captivating city where medieval history and vibrant, youthful energy collide. Less crowded than nearby Bruges, it offers a more authentic Flemish experience. Picture-perfect canals are lined with ornate guildhalls, a formidable castle looms over the city center, and cobblestone streets lead to cozy pubs and world-class museums. This is a city that lives and breathes its history without being trapped by it, thanks to a large student population that fuels a dynamic cultural and culinary scene. Whether you're climbing a medieval belfry for panoramic views or exploring a street dedicated to urban art, you'll find an incredible array of the best things to do in Ghent. It’s a city that invites you to wander, discover, and fall in love with its unique charm.

Top 5 Must-Visit Attractions

  • Gravensteen (Castle of the Counts)
  • St. Bavo's Cathedral & The Mystic Lamb
  • Graslei and Korenlei
  • The Belfry of Ghent
  • St. Michael's Bridge

Complete Guide to Ghent Attractions

Gravensteen (Castle of the Counts)

Dominating the Ghent skyline, the Gravensteen is a formidable 12th-century stone castle that looks straight out of a fairytale. Originally built by Philip of Alsace, the Count of Flanders, it served as a seat of power, a courthouse, and even a cotton mill before its restoration. A visit here is a journey back in time. The self-guided audio tour is highly recommended; it's narrated with a healthy dose of Flemish humor and brings the castle's grim and glorious history to life. You can explore the ramparts, the keep, and the count's residence. The castle also houses a unique collection of torture devices, offering a chilling glimpse into medieval justice. The panoramic views of Ghent from the top are simply breathtaking.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Sint-Veerleplein 11, easily walkable from the city center.

Opening Hours: Daily 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM (last entry at 4:40 PM).

Cost: €13 for adults, concessions available.

Website: https://gravensteen.stad.gent/en

St. Bavo's Cathedral & The Mystic Lamb

A masterpiece of Gothic architecture, St. Bavo's Cathedral is a spiritual and artistic sanctuary. While its soaring nave, ornate pulpit, and Rubens' 'Saint Bavo enters the Convent at Ghent' are impressive, the main draw is a single, world-renowned artwork: 'The Adoration of the Mystic Lamb' by the Van Eyck brothers. Completed in 1432, this polyptych altarpiece is considered a pinnacle of Western art and has a dramatic history of being stolen, hidden, and rescued multiple times. The recently opened visitor's center uses augmented reality to help you explore the masterpiece's details and symbolism before you see the restored panels in person. It's an unforgettable experience for art and history lovers alike.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Sint-Baafsplein, in the heart of the city.

Opening Hours: Cathedral: Mon-Sat 8:30 AM - 5:30 PM, Sun 1:00 PM - 5:30 PM. Mystic Lamb: Mon-Sat 10:00 AM - 5:00 PM, Sun 1:00 PM - 5:00 PM. Hours vary by season.

Cost: Cathedral entry is free. Mystic Lamb visit costs €16.

Website: https://www.sintbaafskathedraal.be/en/

Graslei and Korenlei

The Graslei and Korenlei are the two quays that line the Leie river, forming the historic heart of Ghent. This is arguably the most beautiful view in the city, where a stunning row of historic guildhalls reflects in the water. By day, it's a bustling hub where locals and tourists alike gather on the banks to chat, drink, and watch the boats go by. The atmosphere is vibrant and cheerful. As evening descends, the buildings are beautifully illuminated, creating a magical, romantic setting. Many canal boat tours start here, offering a different perspective of the facades. Grab a Belgian beer or a coffee from a nearby café and simply soak in the view – it’s a quintessential Ghent experience.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Central location, just a short walk from St. Michael's Bridge and Gravensteen.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7.

Cost: Free to visit.

Website: https://visit.gent.be/en/see-do/graslei-korenlei

The Belfry of Ghent

As the tallest belfry in Belgium and a UNESCO World Heritage site, the 91-meter-high Ghent Belfry is a powerful symbol of the city's independence and wealth. Construction started in 1313, and it served as a watchtower and housed the city's charters of privileges. Atop the spire sits a gilded copper dragon, the city's guardian. You can climb the stairs (or take the elevator for most of the way) to the top for an unparalleled 360-degree view of Ghent. Along the way, you'll pass the impressive carillon with its 54 bells. Hearing the bells chime while looking out over the medieval rooftops is a truly memorable moment.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Sint-Baafsplein, between St. Bavo's Cathedral and St. Nicholas' Church.

Opening Hours: Daily 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM (last entry at 5:00 PM).

Cost: €11 for adults.

Website: https://belfortgent.be/en

St. Michael's Bridge

For the perfect postcard picture of Ghent, head to St. Michael's Bridge (Sint-Michielsbrug). This is the only spot in the city where you can capture Ghent's three famous medieval towers in a single frame: St. Nicholas' Church, the Belfry, and St. Bavo's Cathedral. It’s a photographer’s dream, especially during the golden hour or at night when the city is illuminated. Beyond the towers, the bridge offers stunning views of the Graslei and Korenlei, with their historic facades reflected in the Leie river. The bridge itself, with its ornate lanterns and statue of the Archangel Michael, is a beautiful piece of architecture. It’s the central point from which to orient yourself and appreciate the architectural splendor of the city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Centrally located, connecting the Korenmarkt to St. Michael's Church.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7.

Cost: Free to visit.

Website: https://visit.gent.be/en/see-do/st-michaels-bridge

Canal Boat Tour

Seeing Ghent from the water offers a completely different perspective on its history and architecture. A guided canal boat tour is a relaxing and informative way to spend an hour. As you glide along the Leie and Lieve canals, you'll pass under historic bridges and get up-close views of the Graslei, the Gravensteen, and hidden corners of the city you might otherwise miss. Most tours provide commentary in multiple languages, sharing fascinating anecdotes about the city's past, from its prosperous trading days to the lives of the people who lived in the waterside houses. It's a great activity to do early in your trip to get a feel for the city's layout and history.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Multiple operators depart from the Graslei and Korenlei.

Opening Hours: Typically from 10:00 AM to 6:00 PM, with more frequent departures in summer. Check specific operators for timetables.

Cost: Around €9.50 - €12 per person for a 40-50 minute tour.

Website: https://visit.gent.be/en/see-do/boat-trips-ghent

Design Museum Gent

Housed in a stunning 18th-century rococo mansion with a strikingly modern extension, the Design Museum Gent explores design in all its forms, from the Art Nouveau creations of Henry van de Velde to contemporary, thought-provoking pieces. The collection is eclectic and engaging, showcasing everything from whimsical furniture and artistic objects to everyday industrial design. The museum is known for its well-curated temporary exhibitions that often tackle current social themes through the lens of design. It’s a refreshing contrast to the city's medieval attractions and provides a fascinating insight into Belgium's influential role in the world of design.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Jan Breydelstraat, a short walk from the Gravensteen.

Opening Hours: Tues-Sun 9:30 AM - 5:30 PM. Closed on Mondays.

Cost: €8 for adults, often free on the first Sunday of the month.

Website: https://www.designmuseumgent.be/en

Patershol Neighborhood

Step into a labyrinth of narrow cobblestone streets and discover Patershol, Ghent's oldest and most charming neighborhood. This area, nestled beside the Gravensteen, feels like a village within the city. Its history dates back to the 10th century, and the beautifully preserved houses once belonged to leather tanners and weavers. Today, Patershol is a culinary hotspot, packed with an incredible density of high-quality restaurants offering everything from traditional Flemish cuisine to exotic international flavors. It's the perfect place for a romantic evening stroll, followed by a memorable dinner in one of its cozy, atmospheric establishments.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located directly west of the Gravensteen castle.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7. Restaurants typically open for dinner service around 6:00 PM.

Cost: Free to explore.

Website: https://visit.gent.be/en/see-do/patershol

Vrijdagmarkt (Friday Market Square)

The Vrijdagmarkt is one of Ghent's most important historic squares, a place that has been the center of public life for centuries. It was here that monarchs were officially received, but also where disputes were settled and executions took place. Dominating the square is the statue of Jacob van Artevelde, a local hero who sided with England during the Hundred Years' War. True to its name, a lively market takes over the square every Friday morning, a tradition that has continued for hundreds of years. On Saturdays, it hosts a smaller food and flea market. Surrounded by historic guildhalls and bustling pubs, it's a great place to people-watch and soak in the local atmosphere.

Practical Information

How to Get There: A large public square in the city center, north of St. Jacob's Church.

Opening Hours: Square is always open. Main market is Friday 7:30 AM - 1:00 PM.

Cost: Free to visit.

Website: https://visit.gent.be/en/see-do/vrijdagmarkt-friday-market

St. Nicholas' Church

The oldest and most prominent of Ghent's 'three towers', St. Nicholas' Church (Sint-Niklaaskerk) is a magnificent example of the local Scheldt Gothic style. Built from blue-gray stone from the Tournai area, its most striking feature is the central lantern tower, which allowed light to flood into the transept. This design was influential on later cathedrals, including St. Bavo's. Funded by Ghent's wealthy merchants and guilds, the church was a reflection of the city's prosperity. Step inside to appreciate the impressive organ and the quiet, contemplative atmosphere. Its location right next to the Korenmarkt makes it an unmissable landmark on any walking tour of the city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the Korenmarkt, next to St. Michael's Bridge.

Opening Hours: Tues-Sun 10:00 AM - 5:00 PM. Mon 2:00 PM - 5:00 PM.

Cost: Free entry.

Website: https://www.sintniklaaskerk.be/

Graffiti Street (Werregarenstraat)

Discover Ghent's edgy, creative side in Werregarenstraat, better known as Graffiti Street. This narrow alleyway serves as a legal canvas for local and international street artists. It's a living exhibition, constantly changing as new layers of paint cover old ones. One week you might see a detailed portrait, and the next it could be replaced by bold lettering or an abstract design. It's a vibrant explosion of color and creativity that offers a stark contrast to the medieval architecture just steps away. Take a slow walk through, admire the talent on display, and don't forget your camera. It's a dynamic snapshot of Ghent's contemporary urban culture.

Practical Information

How to Get There: A small alley connecting Hoogpoort with Onderstraat, near the Vrijdagmarkt.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7.

Cost: Free.

Website: https://visit.gent.be/en/see-do/graffiti-street

STAM - Ghent City Museum

To truly understand Ghent, a visit to STAM is essential. Housed in a beautifully restored 14th-century abbey with a sleek, modern entrance, this museum tells the story of Ghent's evolution from a medieval metropolis to the vibrant city it is today. It’s far from a stuffy history museum; the exhibits are interactive, engaging, and cleverly designed. The highlight for many is a giant, walk-on aerial photograph of the city printed on the floor, allowing you to 'walk' through Ghent. The 'View of Ghent' multimedia presentation offers a fascinating, fast-paced overview of its history. It's the perfect place to start your visit to get context for everything else you'll see.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Godshuizenlaan 2, a bit outside the main tourist core but easily reachable by tram.

Opening Hours: Tues-Sun 9:30 AM - 5:30 PM. Closed on Mondays.

Cost: €8 for adults.

Website: https://stamgent.be/en

Museum of Fine Arts (MSK)

The Museum voor Schone Kunsten (MSK) is Belgium's oldest museum and houses a spectacular collection of art spanning from the Middle Ages to the first half of the 20th century. The focus is primarily on Belgian and Flemish art, making it a must-see for anyone interested in the region's artistic heritage. Here you can admire masterpieces by Hieronymus Bosch, Peter Paul Rubens, and Anthony van Dyck. The museum's collection of modern art from the 19th and early 20th centuries, including works by Belgian surrealists like René Magritte and symbolists like Fernand Khnopff, is also outstanding. The building itself is a beautiful, light-filled space, making for a pleasant and inspiring visit.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Fernand Scribedreef 1, in Citadelpark, opposite the S.M.A.K.

Opening Hours: Tues-Sun 9:30 AM - 5:30 PM. Closed on Mondays.

Cost: €12 for adults.

Website: https://www.mskgent.be/en

S.M.A.K. (Municipal Museum of Contemporary Art)

Directly opposite the classical MSK is its rebellious younger sibling, the S.M.A.K. This is Ghent's hub for contemporary and avant-garde art, known for its provocative and often challenging exhibitions. The permanent collection features major international artists like Andy Warhol and Francis Bacon, alongside influential Belgian artists such as Luc Tuymans. The museum is celebrated for its dynamic temporary shows that push the boundaries of art. If you're looking for something that will make you think and question, S.M.A.K. delivers. Visiting both the MSK and S.M.A.K. in one trip provides a fantastic journey through centuries of art history and innovation.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Jan Hoetplein 1, in Citadelpark, opposite the MSK.

Opening Hours: Tues-Sun 9:30 AM - 5:30 PM. Closed on Mondays.

Cost: €12 for adults.

Website: https://smak.be/en

Great Butchers' Hall (Groot Vleeshuis)

This impressive 15th-century covered market is a feast for the senses and a testament to Ghent's culinary heritage. The Groot Vleeshuis was originally a central place for selling and inspecting meat. Today, its magnificent open timber roof is still its most striking feature, but now, large Ganda Hams hang from the rafters, curing in the open air. The hall has been repurposed as a promotional center for East Flemish regional products. You can browse and buy local specialties, from Tierenteyn mustard and cuberdon candies to local beers and cheeses. There's also a restaurant inside where you can sample these products firsthand. It's a unique and delicious stop for any foodie.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at Groentenmarkt 7, close to the Gravensteen.

Opening Hours: Wed-Sun 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM. Check website for seasonal changes.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: https://www.grootvleeshuis.be/

Planning Your Visit to Ghent

Getting Around

Ghent's city center is remarkably compact and pedestrian-friendly, making walking the best way to explore the main sights. For longer distances, the city has an excellent public transport network of trams and buses run by De Lijn. A day pass or multi-day pass offers good value. Ghent is also a famous cycling city, with numerous bike rental shops available. A 'water tram' (hop-on-hop-off boat) is another scenic way to get between key locations.

Where to Stay

For first-time visitors, staying in the Historic Center is ideal. The Patershol neighborhood is charming and full of restaurants, while the area around the Graslei and Korenmarkt puts you right in the middle of the action. For those on a budget, the area around the main train station, Gent-Sint-Pieters, offers more affordable options and easy connections. - Luxury: 1898 The Post, a stunning hotel in the old post office building. - Mid-Range: Hotel Harmony, offering modern comfort and a rooftop pool with city views. - Budget: Hostel Uppelink, located in a historic building with unbeatable views of the Graslei.

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Ghent is during the shoulder seasons of spring (April to June) and autumn (September to October). The weather is pleasant for walking, and the city is less crowded than in peak summer. Spring brings blooming flowers, while autumn offers beautiful foliage. Summer (July and August) is warm and lively, highlighted by the massive ten-day 'Gentse Feesten' music and theatre festival in late July, which is an incredible experience but books up far in advance. Winter is cold but magical, with Christmas markets and fewer tourists.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: Historic Heart. Start at Gravensteen (Castle of the Counts). Wander through the charming Patershol neighborhood. Explore the Graslei and Korenlei, then cross St. Michael's Bridge for the iconic photo. Finish with a relaxing canal boat tour. Day 2: Towers & Art. Climb the Belfry for panoramic views. Visit St. Bavo's Cathedral to marvel at the 'Adoration of the Mystic Lamb'. In the afternoon, choose between the Museum of Fine Arts (MSK) or the S.M.A.K. for your art fix. Day 3: Local Life & Flavors. Visit the Vrijdagmarkt (especially on Friday morning). Discover urban art in Graffiti Street. Stop at the Great Butchers' Hall (Groot Vleeshuis) to sample local Ganda ham. Spend the afternoon exploring local shops and enjoying a Belgian beer at a 'brown cafe'.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Ghent expensive?
Ghent is moderately expensive, comparable to other major Western European cities, but often considered slightly more affordable than Brussels or Bruges. Accommodation can be the biggest expense, so book in advance. Eating out ranges from budget-friendly 'frituur' (fry shops) and sandwich places to high-end restaurants. Many museums offer free admission on the first Sunday of the month, and simply walking around the beautiful city center is free.
How many days do you need in Ghent?
Two to three days are ideal for a visit to Ghent. Two days allow you to see all the main highlights, including the castle, cathedrals, and a museum. A third day lets you explore at a more relaxed pace, visit another museum, wander through different neighborhoods like Patershol, and truly soak in the city's vibrant atmosphere without feeling rushed.
Is Ghent safe?
Ghent is a very safe city for travelers. The crime rate is low, and the city center is well-lit and welcoming. As in any popular tourist destination, it's wise to take standard precautions. Be aware of your surroundings in crowded areas like markets and public transport to prevent pickpocketing. Overall, visitors can feel very comfortable and secure while exploring Ghent, day or night.
What is Ghent famous for?
Ghent is famous for its stunningly preserved medieval architecture, particularly the trio of towers (St. Bavo's, the Belfry, and St. Nicholas') and the formidable Gravensteen castle. It is world-renowned for housing the 'Adoration of the Mystic Lamb' altarpiece. The city is also known for its vibrant, youthful atmosphere thanks to its large university, the massive Gentse Feesten festival, and local culinary specialties like 'Waterzooi' (a creamy stew), 'cuberdons' (cone-shaped candies also known as 'neuzekes' or 'noses'), and Tierenteyn mustard.
