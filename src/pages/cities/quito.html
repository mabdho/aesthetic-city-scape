15 Best Things to Do in Quito, Ecuador (2025 Guide)

Perched high in the Andean foothills at an altitude of 2,850m (9,350ft), Quito is a city of stunning contrasts. As the first-ever UNESCO World Heritage site, its historic center is a beautifully preserved labyrinth of colonial architecture, bustling plazas, and magnificent churches. Beyond the cobblestone streets, Ecuador's capital pulsates with a vibrant, modern energy, surrounded by breathtaking volcanic peaks. From standing on the equator to soaring above the city on a cable car, the diversity of experiences makes it a captivating destination for any traveler. Whether you're an art lover, a history buff, or an outdoor enthusiast, exploring the city reveals a rich tapestry of culture and natural beauty. This guide will walk you through all the very best things to do in Quito, ensuring your adventure in the 'City in the Middle of the World' is unforgettable.

Complete Guide to Quito Attractions

Centro Histórico de Quito (Old Town)

The heart and soul of the city, Quito's Old Town was declared the very first UNESCO World Heritage site in 1978, and for good reason. It's one of the largest and best-preserved historic centers in the Americas. Spend a day wandering its narrow cobblestone streets, discovering hidden courtyards, and admiring the ornate facades of colonial-era mansions, churches, and convents. The area is a living museum, with bustling plazas like Plaza Grande and Plaza de San Francisco serving as the social hubs for locals and visitors alike. Every corner tells a story, offering a profound glimpse into centuries of Ecuadorian history, art, and culture.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the center of the city. Easily accessible on foot if staying nearby, or by taxi/ride-sharing from other neighborhoods for a few dollars. The Trolebús 'Plaza Grande' stop is also convenient.

Opening Hours: The area is always open, but individual attractions have their own hours. Best explored during daylight hours (9:00 AM - 5:00 PM).

Cost: Free to walk around. Entrance fees apply to most churches and museums within the area.

Website: https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/2/

TelefériQo

For the most spectacular panoramic views of Quito and the surrounding volcanoes, a ride on the TelefériQo is non-negotiable. This high-altitude cable car ascends the flanks of the Pichincha Volcano, taking you from the city's edge at 3,117m (10,226ft) to the top of Cruz Loma at 3,945m (12,943ft) in about 18 minutes. At the summit, the air is crisp and the views are breathtaking. On a clear day, you can see a chain of volcanoes, including Cotopaxi and Cayambe. There are hiking trails, including one that leads to the summit of Rucu Pichincha, as well as a small chapel and a few cafes. It's an essential experience to grasp the sheer scale and stunning geography of the city's Andean setting.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Av. Occidental. It's best reached by taxi or ride-sharing app, as it's not well-served by public transport. A taxi from the Old Town costs around $5-8.

Opening Hours: Tuesday-Thursday: 10:00 AM - 7:00 PM, Friday-Monday: 9:00 AM - 8:00 PM (Hours can vary, check website).

Cost: $9.00 USD for foreign adults, $7.00 for children.

Website: https://www.teleferico.com.ec/

Ciudad Mitad del Mundo

Stand in two hemispheres at once at the 'Middle of the World'. This monument and park complex is located about 26 km (16 miles) north of Quito, marking the equatorial line. The main attraction is the towering 30-meter-tall monument, which houses an ethnographic museum. You can take an elevator to the top for a great view and, of course, snap the classic photo with one foot on each side of the yellow line representing the equator. While modern GPS shows the true equator is a couple of hundred meters away (at the Intiñan Museum), this monument is an iconic and fun pilgrimage. The complex also features small museums, craft shops, and restaurants, making it a great half-day trip.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take the blue 'Mitad del Mundo' bus from the Ofelia bus terminal in the north of Quito, or hire a taxi/Uber for around $15-20 one way.

Opening Hours: Daily, 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM.

Cost: Entrance to the complex is $5.00 USD per person.

Website: https://www.mitaddelmundo.gob.ec/

Basílica del Voto Nacional

Dominating the skyline of the Old Town, this massive neo-Gothic basilica is the largest of its kind in the Americas. Construction began in 1892 but, technically, it has never been finished – legend has it that the world will end when it is. What makes this church truly unique are its gargoyles. Instead of traditional mythical creatures, they are sculpted representations of Ecuadorian animals like iguanas, armadillos, tortoises, and condors. For a small fee, you can climb the main towers. The ascent involves crossing a rickety wooden plank high above the nave and climbing steep ladders, but the reward is an unparalleled, close-up view of the Old Town and El Panecillo.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on the edge of the Old Town on Calle Carchi and Venezuela. A short uphill walk from Plaza Grande or a quick taxi ride.

Opening Hours: Monday-Friday: 9:00 AM - 5:00 PM, Weekends: 6:00 AM - 6:30 PM.

Cost: Around $2.00 to enter the church, and another $2.00 to climb the towers.

Website: N/A

El Panecillo

This prominent hill in the center of Quito, whose name means 'the little bread loaf', is one of the city's most recognizable landmarks. It is crowned by a colossal 41-meter-tall aluminum statue of the Virgin of Quito, depicted with wings, which is a unique representation known as the Virgin of the Apocalypse. From the base of the statue, you get a stunning 360-degree view, separating the historic center to the north from the sprawling southern part of the city. It's a fantastic spot for photos, especially in the late afternoon as the city lights begin to twinkle. You can also enter the monument and climb up to a small viewing platform for an even higher vantage point.

Practical Information

How to Get There: It is strongly recommended to take a taxi or Uber to the top, as the walk up can be unsafe. Most drivers will wait for you for a small extra fee.

Opening Hours: Daily, 9:00 AM - 6:00 PM (viewpoint). The monument itself has slightly more restricted hours.

Cost: Free to visit the viewpoint. A $2.00 entrance fee applies to climb the statue.

Website: N/A

Museo Templo del Sol Pintor Ortega Maila

Located near the Mitad del Mundo, this is not just a museum but a vibrant cultural experience. Built and curated by the indigenous artist Ortega Maila, the Temple of the Sun is a recreation of an Incan temple constructed using traditional methods. Inside, you'll find a stunning collection of Maila's powerful artwork, which blends Andean cosmology with impressive technique. The guided tour is what makes this place special; you'll participate in an aromatherapy session, learn about ancient beliefs, drink a traditional coca leaf tea, and even get to feed the resident llamas. It offers a fascinating, hands-on insight into the region's ancestral culture.

Practical Information

How to Get There: It's a short 5-minute taxi ride or a 20-minute walk from the Mitad del Mundo complex.

Opening Hours: Daily, 9:00 AM - 5:00 PM.

Cost: $4.00 USD per person, which includes the guided tour.

Website: https://museotemplodelsol.com/

Calle La Ronda

By day, La Ronda is a charming, quiet cobblestone street in the Old Town lined with colorful colonial houses. By night, especially on weekends, it transforms into one of the city's liveliest social hubs. The street comes alive with music, from traditional Andean bands to salsa rhythms pouring out of bars. It's the perfect place to experience Quito's bohemian side. You can browse artisan workshops selling handmade crafts, sip on canelazo (a traditional hot spiced alcoholic drink), and sample local foods like empanadas de viento. It's a wonderfully atmospheric place for an evening stroll, offering a safe and festive environment to enjoy Quito's nightlife.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the southern part of the Old Town, a short walk from Plaza Santo Domingo.

Opening Hours: Shops are open during the day. The street becomes most active from Thursday to Saturday evening (7:00 PM onwards).

Cost: Free to walk. Prices for food, drinks, and crafts are very reasonable.

Website: N/A

Fundación Guayasamín & Capilla del Hombre

This is a must-visit for art lovers. The complex is dedicated to Oswaldo Guayasamín, Ecuador's most celebrated modern artist. His former home, now a museum, showcases his personal collection of pre-Columbian and colonial art alongside his own powerful paintings that depict the struggles and identity of Latin American people. Adjacent to the home is the Capilla del Hombre (Chapel of Man), a breathtaking, purpose-built art gallery designed by Guayasamín himself. The monumental and moving murals inside are a tribute to human suffering and resilience. The entire complex is a profound artistic and emotional journey, set on a hill with beautiful views over the city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Bellavista neighborhood. A taxi or Uber from La Mariscal or the Old Town is the easiest way to get there ($3-5).

Opening Hours: Daily, 10:00 AM - 5:00 PM.

Cost: $10.00 USD for adults, which includes access to both the museum and the chapel.

Website: https://www.guayasamin.org/

Plaza Grande (Plaza de la Independencia)

This is Quito's main square and the political and social heart of the nation. Surrounded by four key buildings representing the powers of Ecuadorian society—the Presidential Palace (Carondelet Palace), the Cathedral of Quito, the Archbishop's Palace, and the Municipal Palace—the plaza is a hive of activity. Locals relax on benches, vendors sell snacks, and shoeshiners ply their trade. At the center stands the Independence Monument, commemorating Ecuador's heroes. On Mondays at 11:00 AM, you can witness the pomp and circumstance of the changing of the presidential guard ceremony. It's the perfect starting point for exploring the Old Town.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Centrally located in the Historic Center. The 'Plaza Grande' Trolebús stop is right there.

Opening Hours: Open 24/7, but best enjoyed during the day.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Iglesia de la Compañía de Jesús

Often cited as Ecuador's most beautiful church, La Compañía de Jesús is the absolute pinnacle of Spanish Baroque architecture in South America. While its volcanic stone facade is impressive, the interior is what will leave you speechless. Nearly every square inch is covered in shimmering gold leaf—an estimated seven tons of it. The intricate carvings, detailed Moorish patterns on the ceiling, and stunning paintings create an overwhelming sense of opulence and devotion. It took 160 years to complete, and the result is a jaw-dropping masterpiece that is simply unmissable, regardless of your religious beliefs.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Old Town, just a block from Plaza Grande on Calle García Moreno.

Opening Hours: Mon-Thurs: 9:30 AM - 6:30 PM, Fri: 9:30 AM - 5:30 PM, Sat: 9:30 AM - 4:00 PM, Sun: 12:30 PM - 4:00 PM.

Cost: $5.00 USD for foreigners.

Website: https://www.fundacioniglesiadelacompania.org.ec/

Mercado Artesanal La Mariscal

If you're looking for souvenirs, this is the place to go. The Mercado Artesanal La Mariscal is Quito's largest artisan market, a sprawling collection of stalls packed with colorful textiles, alpaca wool sweaters, leather goods, hand-carved wooden items, Panama hats (which actually originate from Ecuador), and intricate jewelry. It's a one-stop shop for gifts to take home. The atmosphere is vibrant and friendly, but don't forget to practice your bargaining skills – it's expected and part of the fun. Even if you're not buying, it's a fantastic place to browse and soak in the colors and craftsmanship of Ecuador.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the La Mariscal neighborhood on Calle Jorge Washington, between Reina Victoria and Juan León Mera.

Opening Hours: Daily, approximately 9:00 AM to 7:00 PM.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: N/A

Parque Metropolitano Guangüiltagua

As one of South America's largest urban parks, this vast green space offers a wonderful escape from the city's hustle and bustle. Covering over 550 hectares, the park is a forest of eucalyptus trees crisscrossed with miles of trails for walking, jogging, and mountain biking. It's a favorite spot for locals, especially on weekends, for picnics and family outings. The park's viewpoints offer fantastic perspectives of Quito and the Cumbayá valley below. If you're looking for an active morning or a peaceful afternoon in nature without leaving the city, Parque Metropolitano is the perfect destination.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the north of Quito, east of Estadio Olímpico Atahualpa. Best reached by taxi or bus lines that run along Av. Eloy Alfaro.

Opening Hours: Daily, 6:00 AM to 5:30 PM.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Jardín Botánico de Quito

Nestled inside Parque La Carolina, Quito's Botanical Garden is a serene oasis showcasing the incredible biodiversity of Ecuador. The well-designed pathways lead you through different ecosystems, from high-altitude Andean wetlands to humid cloud forests. The highlight for many is the magnificent orchid house, which displays over 1,200 species of orchids in a stunning, colorful collection. You'll also find sections dedicated to medicinal plants, carnivorous plants, and native trees. It's a peaceful and educational experience that highlights the country's status as one of the most biodiverse places on Earth.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located within Parque La Carolina in the modern financial district. Accessible via the Ecovía bus ('La Carolina' stop) or taxi.

Opening Hours: Daily, 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM.

Cost: $4.00 USD for adults.

Website: https://jardinbotanicoquito.com/

Yaku Water Museum

Situated on the slopes of Pichincha overlooking the Old Town, Yaku offers a unique and interactive perspective on the importance of water. Housed in the city's first public water treatment plant, the museum combines science, history, and art to create engaging exhibits for all ages. It explores water's role in nature, society, and the cosmos according to Andean beliefs. The outdoor 'Bubble Room' is a huge hit with kids and adults alike. Beyond the fun exhibits, the museum's terrace provides one of the best and least-crowded photo opportunities of the historic center below.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located above the Old Town in the El Placer neighborhood. It's a steep uphill walk, so a taxi is recommended ($2-3 from the center).

Opening Hours: Tuesday to Sunday, 9:00 AM to 5:30 PM.

Cost: $4.00 USD for adults.

Website: https://www.yakumuseoagua.gob.ec/

Iglesia y Convento de San Francisco

Facing its namesake plaza, the Church and Convent of San Francisco is a monumental religious complex and Quito's oldest church. Construction began just weeks after the city's founding in 1534. The sprawling complex covers nearly two city blocks and includes the main church, several chapels, and a convent with a beautiful inner courtyard. While the facade is imposing and Mannerist in style, the interior boasts a stunning Mudejar-style coffered ceiling and a magnificent baroque altar. The adjoining museum houses a vast collection of colonial religious art, including the famous 'Virgen de Quito' by Bernardo de Legarda, the sculpture that inspired the statue on El Panecillo.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Dominates the Plaza de San Francisco in the Old Town, an easy walk from Plaza Grande.

Opening Hours: Mon-Sat: 9:00 AM - 5:30 PM, Sun: 9:00 AM - 1:00 PM. Museum has slightly different hours.

Cost: Free to enter the main church. A $3.00 fee applies for the convent, museum, and choir.

Website: https://museofraypedrogocial.com/

Planning Your Visit to Quito

Getting Around

Walking is the best way to explore the compact Historic Center. For longer distances, official yellow taxis are plentiful and cheap; always ensure the driver uses the meter ('taxímetro'). Ride-sharing apps like Uber and Cabify are also widely available and reliable. The city's public transit system includes the Trolebús (trolleybus), Ecovía, and various bus lines, which are very inexpensive but can be crowded.

Where to Stay

For history and charm, stay in the Centro Histórico (Old Town), with beautiful boutique hotels like Casa Gangotena and Hotel Patio Andaluz. For nightlife, restaurants, and a central location, La Mariscal (nicknamed 'Gringolandia') is a popular choice, with options ranging from hostels like Selina to mid-range hotels. For a trendy, bohemian vibe with great cafes and art galleries, the La Floresta neighborhood is an excellent option.

Best Time to Visit

Quito's high altitude gives it a spring-like climate year-round, with average temperatures of 10-20°C (50-68°F). The main distinction is between the dry season (verano - summer) from June to September, and the wet season (invierno - winter) from October to May. The dry season is ideal, with sunnier days and less rain. The wet season typically features sunny mornings followed by afternoon showers, so plan activities for earlier in the day.

Suggested Itinerary

A simple 3-day itinerary plan: Day 1: Immerse yourself in the Centro Histórico. Visit Plaza Grande, La Compañía de Jesús, and the San Francisco Church. In the afternoon, take a taxi up El Panecillo for sunset views. Enjoy dinner and a walk on Calle La Ronda. Day 2: Go for altitude and latitude. Take the TelefériQo up Pichincha in the morning for stunning views (go early for clear skies). In the afternoon, take a trip north to the Mitad del Mundo and the Museo Templo del Sol. Day 3: Explore Quito's art and markets. Visit the Fundación Guayasamín & Capilla del Hombre in the morning. Head to the Mercado Artesanal La Mariscal for souvenir shopping, then relax in the nearby Parque La Carolina or visit the Botanical Garden.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Quito expensive?
Quito is one of the most affordable capital cities in South America. Accommodation can be found for all budgets, from $15 hostels to $400 luxury hotels. A full meal ('almuerzo') can be found for $3-5, while a dinner at a mid-range restaurant might cost $15-25 per person. Taxi rides within the city rarely exceed $5-8. Overall, it's a very budget-friendly destination.
How many days do you need in Quito?
To comfortably see the main highlights of the city and its immediate surroundings, 3 to 4 days is recommended. This allows enough time to explore the Old Town, visit the Mitad del Mundo, ride the TelefériQo, and see a museum or two. It also gives your body a chance to acclimatize to the high altitude before heading to other destinations in Ecuador.
Is Quito safe?
Quito is generally as safe as any other large city in Latin America, but it requires awareness and common-sense precautions. Petty crime, such as pickpocketing and bag snatching, is the most common concern, especially in crowded areas like buses and markets. It's advisable to avoid walking alone at night in certain areas, including parts of the Old Town and La Mariscal. Always use official taxis (yellow with orange plates) or ride-sharing apps, and keep your valuables secure and out of sight.
What is Quito famous for?
Quito is most famous for having one of the most extensive and best-preserved colonial centers in the Americas, which led to it being named the first-ever UNESCO World Heritage City. It's also renowned for its stunning location high in the Andes mountains, surrounded by volcanoes, and its proximity to the equatorial line, which gives it the nickname 'The Middle of the World'.
