15 Best Things to Do in Tunis, Tunisia (2025 Guide)

A captivating fusion of ancient history and vibrant modern life, Tunis is a city that enchants every visitor. Its heart beats within the labyrinthine alleys of the UNESCO-listed Medina, a world of bustling souks, hidden courtyards, and architectural marvels. Beyond the old walls, grand French colonial boulevards lead to world-class museums showcasing millennia of history, from Punic Carthage to Roman mosaics. The city serves as a perfect gateway to the iconic blue-and-white village of Sidi Bou Said and the legendary ruins of Carthage. Whether you're a history buff, a culture enthusiast, or simply seeking an authentic North African adventure, this guide will walk you through the very best things to do in Tunis, ensuring a trip filled with discovery and wonder. Prepare to immerse yourself in a city where every corner tells a story.

Complete Guide to Tunis Attractions

Medina of Tunis

Step back in time in the Medina of Tunis, a UNESCO World Heritage site since 1979. This sprawling labyrinth of narrow, winding streets is one of North Africa's most impressive medieval cities. Inside its ancient walls, you'll discover a vibrant tapestry of life with bustling souks (markets), historic mosques, serene madrassas (schools), and ornate private homes with hidden courtyards. Getting lost here is part of the experience; every turn reveals a new scent, from spices and leather to fresh mint tea. The Medina is divided into various souks, each specializing in a different craft, such as Souk el-Attarine for perfumes, Souk des Chechias for traditional red felt hats, and Souk el-Berka for jewelers. It's a sensory overload in the best way possible, offering an authentic glimpse into Tunisian life that has remained unchanged for centuries. Be sure to look up and admire the intricate tilework and studded wooden doors that adorn the buildings.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the heart of Tunis. Accessible on foot from the city center via the Bab el Bhar gate. Taxis can drop you at the edge.

Opening Hours: Shops and souks are generally open from 10:00 AM to 6:00 PM. Some areas are accessible 24/7, but it's best to visit during the day.

Cost: Free to enter and wander. Prices for goods are negotiable.

Website: whc.unesco.org/en/list/36

The Bardo National Museum

Housed in a magnificent 19th-century Hafsid palace, the Bardo National Museum is home to one of the world's most important collections of Roman mosaics. These incredibly preserved artworks depict scenes of daily life, mythology, and nature, offering a vivid window into the Roman era in North Africa. The sheer scale and intricate detail of the mosaics are breathtaking, making the Bardo an unmissable stop for anyone interested in history and art. Beyond the mosaics, the museum's collection spans Tunisia's rich history, from prehistoric artifacts to Punic treasures and Islamic ceramics. The building itself is an attraction, with its beautiful domed ceilings, decorated tiles, and grand halls. Highlights include the Virgil Room and the stunning Sousse Room, which features the famous mosaic 'Triumph of Neptune'.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Le Bardo suburb. Take Metro line 4 to the 'Bardo' station or a short taxi ride from the city center.

Opening Hours: Tuesday-Sunday: 9:00 AM - 5:00 PM (Summer), 9:30 AM - 4:30 PM (Winter). Closed on Mondays.

Cost: Approximately 13 TND.

Website: www.bardomuseum.tn

Ruins of Carthage

Explore the legendary city of Carthage, a sprawling archaeological site that was once the seat of a powerful Punic empire and later a major city of the Roman Republic. The ruins are spread across a modern suburb of Tunis and offer a fascinating journey through layers of history. Key sites include the Antonine Baths, once the largest in the Roman Empire, offering stunning views over the Gulf of Tunis. Imagine the grandeur of this seaside complex as you wander among its colossal remains. Other must-see spots within the Carthage complex include the Punic Port, the Tophet where child sacrifices were allegedly made, the Roman amphitheater, and the beautifully situated Roman villas on Byrsa Hill, which also houses the Carthage National Museum. A single ticket grants access to multiple sites, so plan for at least half a day to do them justice.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Easily accessible via the TGM light rail line from Tunis Marine station. Get off at stops like 'Carthage Hannibal' or 'Carthage Dermech'.

Opening Hours: Daily, 8:00 AM - 7:00 PM (Summer), 8:30 AM - 5:30 PM (Winter). Hours can vary slightly between sites.

Cost: A global ticket for all Carthage sites costs approximately 12 TND.

Website: www.patrimoinedetunisie.com.tn

Sidi Bou Said

Perched on a cliff overlooking the Mediterranean, the village of Sidi Bou Said is a picture-perfect escape. Famous for its whitewashed buildings with vibrant blue doors, shutters, and decorative latticework (mashrabiya), it has been an inspiration for artists like Paul Klee and August Macke for over a century. The village is a joy to explore on foot; wander its cobblestone streets, browse the art galleries and souvenir shops, and soak in the bohemian atmosphere. The main draw is the breathtaking view. Find a spot at a clifftop café, most famously the Café des Nattes, and sip a sweet mint tea with pine nuts while gazing out at the azure sea and the marina below. The tranquil ambiance and stunning aesthetics make Sidi Bou Said one of the most beloved destinations in Tunisia.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take the TGM light rail line from Tunis Marine to the 'Sidi Bou Said' station. It's the stop just before the terminus at La Marsa.

Opening Hours: The village is always open. Shops and cafes generally open from 10:00 AM until late evening.

Cost: Free to enter. Costs apply for cafes and shops.

Website: www.com-sidibousaid.gov.tn

Zitouna Mosque

The Zitouna Mosque, or the 'Mosque of the Olive Tree,' is the spiritual heart of the Tunis Medina. Founded in 698, it is the oldest mosque in the capital and one of the country's most important. For centuries, it was also a major center of learning, functioning as a university long before those in Europe. Its architecture is a beautiful blend of styles, with a majestic prayer hall featuring columns repurposed from the ruins of Carthage. While the main prayer hall is reserved for Muslims, non-Muslim visitors are welcome to enter the serene central courtyard outside of prayer times. From here, you can admire the elegant arcades, the 19th-century minaret, and absorb the profound sense of peace and history that permeates the site. Modest dress is required to enter.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the center of the Medina of Tunis. It is easily found by following the main thoroughfares of the souks.

Opening Hours: Open to non-Muslims from approximately 8:00 AM - 12:00 PM, Saturday-Thursday. Closed on Fridays for visitors.

Cost: Free, though a small donation is sometimes requested.

Website: N/A - Information available from local tourist offices.

Avenue Habib Bourguiba

Often called the 'Champs-Élysées of Tunis,' Avenue Habib Bourguiba is the main artery of the modern city. Lined with ficus trees, grand colonial-era buildings, cafes, shops, and theaters, this wide boulevard represents the intersection of Tunisian history and its post-independence identity. It's a place for strolling, people-watching from a Parisian-style terrace café, and feeling the pulse of contemporary Tunis. Key landmarks along the avenue include the iconic Clock Tower, the imposing Cathedral of St. Vincent de Paul, and the Municipal Theatre, a beautiful example of Art Nouveau architecture. The avenue begins at the Bab el Bhar gate, symbolically linking the old Medina with the new city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Centrally located, it's the main street of downtown Tunis. Most transport lines converge here.

Opening Hours: Accessible 24/7.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Cathedral of St. Vincent de Paul

Standing proudly on the Place de l'Indépendance at the end of Avenue Habib Bourguiba, the Cathedral of St. Vincent de Paul is a striking landmark of downtown Tunis. This Roman Catholic cathedral was completed in 1897 and features a unique mix of architectural styles, including Moorish revival, Gothic, and Neo-Byzantine. Its two large square towers make it an unmissable feature of the city's skyline. The cathedral is still an active place of worship and offers a peaceful respite from the bustling city outside. Visitors are welcome to step inside and admire its interior, which is more subdued than its grand exterior but equally impressive. It stands as a testament to the city's diverse cultural and religious history.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Avenue Habib Bourguiba in the city center. Easily accessible on foot from downtown hotels.

Opening Hours: Generally open to visitors during daylight hours, outside of service times.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Bab el Bhar (Porte de France)

Bab el Bhar, meaning 'The Sea Gate,' is the formal entrance to the Tunis Medina from the modern city. Also known as Porte de France, this stone archway was built in 1848 to replace an older gate. It marks the dramatic transition from the wide, European-style Avenue Habib Bourguiba to the narrow, chaotic alleys of the ancient Medina. Standing before it, you can literally see two different worlds meet. Passing through the gate is a symbolic act for any visitor beginning their exploration of the old city. The square in front of the gate is a bustling hub of activity, with street vendors, cafes, and a constant flow of people, making it an excellent spot for photography and observing daily life.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at the eastern end of the Medina, where it meets Avenue de France and Avenue Habib Bourguiba.

Opening Hours: Accessible 24/7.

Cost: Free.

Website: N/A

Central Market (Marché Central)

For a truly immersive and authentic local experience, head to the Marché Central. This sprawling, covered market is a feast for the senses, where Tunisians come to do their daily shopping. The main hall is dominated by a huge, circular fish market, an incredible spectacle of sights and sounds. Surrounding it are vendors selling everything from fresh produce, dates, and olives to fragrant spices, cheeses, and meats. It's a fantastic place to observe local life, practice your bargaining skills, and sample some Tunisian street food. The vibrant atmosphere, the calls of the vendors, and the sheer variety of goods on display make it a photographer's dream and a foodie's paradise.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located just off Avenue de France, a short walk from Bab el Bhar.

Opening Hours: Daily, from early morning (around 6:00 AM) to early afternoon (around 2:00 PM). Best to go in the morning for the most activity.

Cost: Free to enter.

Website: N/A

Tourbet el Bey

Located in the southern part of the Medina, Tourbet el Bey is the largest royal mausoleum in Tunis. This final resting place for the Husainid dynasty, which ruled Tunisia from 1705 to 1957, is an architectural gem. The exterior is notable for its green, fish-scale tiled domes, while the interior is a lavish display of marble carvings, stucco work, and ceramic tiles. The complex contains the tombs of over 160 princes, princesses, and their trusted ministers and officials. Each room is richly decorated, reflecting the status of those interred within. Visiting Tourbet el Bey offers a unique insight into the opulent funereal art and traditions of Tunisia's ruling class.

Practical Information

How to Get There: In the southern part of the Medina on Rue Tourbet el Bey. It's a bit of a walk from the central souks but well worth it.

Opening Hours: Typically open Tuesday-Sunday, 9:30 AM - 4:30 PM. Hours can be irregular, so check locally.

Cost: Approximately 5 TND.

Website: N/A

Dar Ben Abdallah Museum

Housed in a beautiful 18th-century palace within the Medina, the Dar Ben Abdallah Museum is dedicated to the popular arts and traditions of Tunis. This museum provides a fascinating look into the life of a wealthy merchant family in the 19th century. The palace itself, with its stunning courtyard, marble floors, and Andalusian-style tilework, is worth the visit alone. The exhibits showcase traditional costumes, jewelry, household items, and wedding artifacts, offering a rich ethnographical perspective on Tunisian culture. It's a much quieter and more intimate experience than the Bardo, providing deep cultural context for what you see in the surrounding souks.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the southern part of the Medina, close to Tourbet el Bey.

Opening Hours: Tuesday-Sunday, 9:30 AM - 4:30 PM. Closed on Mondays.

Cost: Approximately 5 TND.

Website: www.patrimoinedetunisie.com.tn

Belvédère Park

Belvédère Park is the largest urban park in Tunis, offering a welcome green oasis away from the city's hustle and bustle. Designed in the late 19th century, this beautifully landscaped park is spread over a hilltop, providing excellent views of the city and the lake of Tunis. It's a popular spot for locals to relax, picnic, and exercise. The park features walking paths, centenary trees, a small lake, and the Tunis Zoo. It also houses the Museum of Modern and Contemporary Art. It's the perfect place to spend a leisurely afternoon, enjoying a bit of nature and tranquility without leaving the city.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located north of the city center. Accessible by taxi or a long walk from downtown.

Opening Hours: The park is open 24/7. The zoo has its own hours (typically 9:00 AM - 5:00 PM).

Cost: Free to enter the park. An entrance fee applies for the zoo.

Website: N/A

Dar Hussein

Another magnificent palace in the Medina, Dar Hussein is a grand example of 18th-century Tunisian architecture. Once a ministerial residence and later the city's first municipal headquarters under the French, it now houses the National Heritage Institute. While not a formal museum, visitors are often permitted to enter its spectacular main courtyard when it's not in use for official functions. The courtyard is a masterpiece of design, featuring beautiful arches, columns, and intricate tilework. It offers a glimpse into the luxurious lifestyle of Tunis's elite. Even if you can only peek inside, its grandeur is undeniable and well worth seeking out near the Zitouna Mosque.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Medina, near the Kasbah square and Zitouna Mosque.

Opening Hours: Access can be sporadic as it's a working government building. Best to try during standard office hours (morning).

Cost: Free, if access is permitted.

Website: N/A

Byrsa Hill & Carthage National Museum

Byrsa Hill is the historical and spiritual heart of ancient Carthage. This was the site of the citadel of Punic Carthage and later the Roman forum. Today, it offers some of the most commanding views over the entire archaeological site and the Gulf of Tunis. At the summit stands the former Carthage National Museum (currently undergoing renovation) and the 19th-century Acropolium, also known as Saint Louis Cathedral. Wandering the grounds, you can see the layout of the ancient Punic quarter and remnants of Roman structures. It is the best starting point for a tour of Carthage, as the panoramic view helps to orient you and understand the scale of the ancient city. The combination of deep history and spectacular scenery is powerful.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take the TGM train to 'Carthage Hannibal'. The hill is a short, uphill walk from the station.

Opening Hours: Daily, 8:00 AM - 7:00 PM (Summer), 8:30 AM - 5:30 PM (Winter). Museum is closed for renovation as of early 2024, but the grounds are accessible.

Cost: Included in the global ticket for Carthage sites (approx. 12 TND).

Website: www.patrimoinedetunisie.com.tn

La Goulette Port

La Goulette is the historic port of Tunis, a vibrant and bustling district with a character all its own. Known for its fantastic seafood restaurants lining the waterfront, this is the place to come for a fresh fish lunch or dinner. The area has a rich multicultural history, with a strong Italian influence still visible in its architecture and atmosphere. 'Little Sicily' was its nickname for a reason. Stroll along the boulevard, watch the fishing boats, and see the 16th-century Spanish fortress (Karaka). La Goulette offers a different, more laid-back vibe than central Tunis and is a great place to experience the city's maritime soul. It's particularly lively in the evenings and on weekends.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Accessible via the TGM light rail line from Tunis Marine. The 'La Goulette' stop is just a few stops from Tunis.

Opening Hours: Accessible 24/7. Restaurants are typically open for lunch and dinner.

Cost: Free to explore. Restaurant prices vary.

Website: N/A

Planning Your Visit to Tunis

Getting Around

Tunis has several transportation options. Yellow taxis are plentiful and affordable for short distances; always ensure the meter is used. The TGM light rail is excellent for reaching Carthage, Sidi Bou Said, and La Marsa. For travel within the city, the 'Métro Léger de Tunis' tramway system covers many districts. For a local experience, shared taxis or 'louages' are a fast and cheap way to travel between neighborhoods and nearby towns.

Where to Stay

For an atmospheric stay, choose a 'Dar' (traditional guesthouse) in the Medina. For modern comforts and convenience, the downtown area around Avenue Habib Bourguiba offers numerous international hotels like the Hotel Carlton or the Sheraton Tunis Hotel. For a seaside retreat with beautiful views, stay in Sidi Bou Said or the neighboring suburb of La Marsa, where you'll find boutique hotels like Dar Said and luxury options.

Best Time to Visit

The best times to visit Tunis are during the spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November). During these months, the weather is pleasantly warm and sunny, perfect for exploring the ancient ruins and wandering through the Medina. Summers (June to August) can be very hot, while winters (December to February) are mild but can be rainy.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: Immerse yourself in the old city. Explore the Medina of Tunis, visit the Zitouna Mosque, get lost in the souks, and see Bab el Bhar. Enjoy lunch at a traditional restaurant within the Medina. In the afternoon, visit the Central Market. Day 2: Focus on ancient history. In the morning, take a taxi or the metro to the Bardo National Museum to marvel at its mosaic collection. In the afternoon, take the TGM train to Carthage. Explore the Antonine Baths, Byrsa Hill, and the Punic Port. Day 3: Discover coastal charm. Take the TGM train to Sidi Bou Said. Spend the morning wandering its blue-and-white streets and enjoying mint tea at a cliffside cafe. Continue on the TGM to La Marsa for a seaside lunch and a stroll along the beach before returning to Tunis.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Tunis expensive?
Tunis is generally considered a very affordable travel destination, especially when compared to European cities. Accommodation, food, and transportation are all reasonably priced. A meal at a local restaurant can cost just a few dollars, and taxis are inexpensive for getting around the city. You can enjoy a rich travel experience in Tunis without a large budget.
How many days do you need in Tunis?
A stay of 3 to 4 days is ideal to experience Tunis and its immediate surroundings comfortably. This allows one full day for the Medina and downtown Tunis, one day for the Bardo Museum and the Ruins of Carthage, and another day to explore the coastal towns of Sidi Bou Said and La Marsa without feeling rushed.
Is Tunis safe?
Tunis is generally a safe city for tourists. However, like any large city, it's important to take standard precautions. Be aware of your surroundings, especially in crowded areas like the Medina and markets, to avoid pickpocketing. It's advisable to dress modestly, particularly when visiting religious sites, to show respect for local customs. Stick to well-lit main streets at night. Overall, Tunisians are known for their hospitality and welcoming nature.
What is Tunis famous for?
Tunis is famous for its incredible historical and cultural sites. Its key highlights include the ancient Ruins of Carthage, the former capital of a powerful Mediterranean empire; the vast and vibrant Medina of Tunis, a UNESCO World Heritage site; the Bardo National Museum, which holds the world's largest collection of Roman mosaics; and the stunningly beautiful blue-and-white clifftop village of Sidi Bou Said.
