15 Best Things to Do in Nice, France (2025 Guide)

Nestled on the sun-drenched shores of the French Riviera, Nice is a dazzling blend of old-world opulence, vibrant street life, and stunning Mediterranean beauty. The capital of the Côte d'Azur captivates visitors with its azure waters, palm-fringed promenades, and a charming old town (Vieux Nice) bursting with pastel-hued buildings and bustling markets. Beyond the beach, the city is a haven for art lovers, home to world-class museums dedicated to masters like Matisse and Chagall. Whether you're strolling along the iconic Baie des Anges (Bay of Angels), savoring local culinary delights like socca, or exploring lush hilltop parks with panoramic views, you'll find no shortage of enchanting experiences. This guide will walk you through all the very best things to do in Nice, ensuring an unforgettable journey into the heart of Riviera glamour and culture.

Top 5 Must-Visit Attractions

  • Promenade des Anglais
  • Vieux Nice (Old Town)
  • Castle Hill (Colline du Château)
  • Marc Chagall National Museum
  • Cours Saleya Market

Complete Guide to Nice Attractions

Promenade des Anglais

Stretching for 7 kilometers along the breathtaking Baie des Anges, the Promenade des Anglais is the quintessential Nice experience. This iconic seafront boulevard was established in the 19th century by English aristocrats who wintered on the Riviera, and its grandeur remains today. It's a hub of activity from dawn until dusk, perfect for a leisurely stroll, a bike ride on its dedicated path, or simply relaxing on one of its famous blue chairs while gazing at the turquoise Mediterranean Sea. The promenade is lined with elegant Belle Époque hotels, including the legendary Le Negresco, and separates the city from its famous pebble beaches. It's the social and scenic heart of Nice, offering spectacular sunrises and sunsets, and a constant parade of locals and tourists soaking up the Riviera atmosphere. It's an absolute must-see and the perfect starting point for any visit.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Runs along the entire seafront of Nice. Easily accessible on foot from most of the city center.

Opening Hours: 24/7

Cost: Free

Website: www.nicetourisme.com

Vieux Nice (Old Town)

Step back in time as you wander into the labyrinthine streets of Vieux Nice. This historic quarter is a delightful maze of narrow, winding alleyways, shaded squares, and pastel-colored buildings with charming wooden shutters. The area exudes an Italianate feel, a nod to its past, and is packed with artisan shops, quaint boutiques, authentic ice cream parlors (fenocchio is a must-try!), and traditional Niçois restaurants. Key sights within the Old Town include the Cathédrale Sainte-Réparate and the Palais Lascaris. But the real joy is getting lost, discovering hidden courtyards, and soaking up the lively, authentic atmosphere. It’s a sensory experience, filled with the scents of fresh flowers from the Cours Saleya, the sound of church bells, and the vibrant colors of daily life.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located at the foot of Castle Hill, just east of Place Masséna. Best explored on foot.

Opening Hours: Shops and restaurants have individual hours, but the area is always accessible.

Cost: Free to wander

Website: www.nicetourisme.com/nice/1-23-vieux-nice.html

Castle Hill (Colline du Château)

For the most breathtaking panoramic views of Nice, the Baie des Anges, the old port, and the surrounding Alps, a trip up Castle Hill is essential. Although the original citadel was dismantled by Louis XIV's troops in 1706, the hilltop has been transformed into a magnificent park. It's a peaceful oasis with lush greenery, playgrounds for children, ancient ruins, and a stunning man-made waterfall. You can reach the summit via a free elevator (ascenseur) from the seaside, or by climbing the scenic staircases from the Old Town or the port side. It's the perfect spot for a picnic, a relaxing afternoon, and capturing postcard-perfect photos of the city below. The views at sunset are particularly magical.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Access via stairs from Vieux Nice or Port Lympia, or take the free Art-Deco lift (ascenseur) at the end of the Quai des États-Unis.

Opening Hours: Park: Approx. 8:30 AM to 8:00 PM in summer, shorter hours in winter. Lift has slightly more limited hours.

Cost: Free

Website: www.nice.fr/fr/parcs-et-jardins/le-parc-de-la-colline-du-chateau

Cours Saleya Market

Cours Saleya is the vibrant heart of the Old Town, a bustling pedestrianized square that hosts one of France's most famous open-air markets. From Tuesday to Sunday, it's a riot of color and fragrance as the Marché aux Fleurs (Flower Market) takes over, alongside stalls selling fresh produce, local cheeses, olives, and spices. It's the perfect place to sample local delicacies like socca, a chickpea pancake cooked in a wood-fired oven. On Mondays, the square transforms into a sprawling Marché à la Brocante, an antiques and flea market where you can hunt for unique treasures and vintage finds. The market is surrounded by lively cafés and restaurants with terraces, making it an ideal spot for people-watching over a morning coffee or a leisurely lunch.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in Vieux Nice, running parallel to the Quai des États-Unis.

Opening Hours: Market: Approx. 6:00 AM to 1:30 PM (Tues-Sun). Antiques market on Mondays.

Cost: Free to browse

Website: www.marches-de-nice.fr

Marc Chagall National Museum

Designed in collaboration with the artist himself, the Musée National Marc Chagall is a truly special place. It was created to house Chagall's series of 17 large paintings illustrating the biblical message, from the books of Genesis, Exodus, and the Song of Songs. The main gallery is bathed in natural light, allowing the artist's famously vibrant and spiritual works to shine. Beyond the main collection, the museum also features a wealth of other works including sculptures, stained glass, mosaics, and numerous sketches and lithographs. The building itself is a modernist masterpiece, surrounded by a serene Mediterranean garden. Even if you're not an art historian, the sheer beauty and emotional power of Chagall's work in this purpose-built space is deeply moving.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Cimiez neighborhood. Take bus number 5 from the city center.

Opening Hours: 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM (May-Oct), 10:00 AM - 5:00 PM (Nov-Apr). Closed Tuesdays.

Cost: Around €10, varies with exhibitions. Free for EU citizens under 26.

Website: www.musees-nationaux-alpesmaritimes.fr/chagall

Musée Matisse

Housed in a striking 17th-century Genoese villa in the peaceful Cimiez neighborhood, the Musée Matisse is a celebration of the artist who lived in Nice from 1917 until his death in 1954. The museum's collection spans Matisse's entire career, showcasing his evolution as an artist through paintings, drawings, sculptures, and his famous paper cut-outs. Visitors can see some of his most significant works, including 'Nature morte aux grenades' and the iconic 'Nu bleu IV'. The collection provides a fascinating insight into his creative process and his deep connection to the light and colors of the French Riviera. The surrounding olive grove and the nearby Roman ruins add to the charm of the visit.

Practical Information

How to Get There: In the Cimiez neighborhood, near the Chagall Museum. Take bus number 5.

Opening Hours: 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM. Closed Tuesdays.

Cost: Around €10 (often a combined ticket is available for municipal museums).

Website: www.musee-matisse-nice.org

Russian Orthodox Cathedral of Saint-Nicolas

A stunning and unexpected sight in the heart of the French Riviera, the Saint-Nicolas Cathedral is the largest Russian Orthodox cathedral in Western Europe. Built in the early 20th century and funded by Tsar Nicholas II, its ornate, onion-domed architecture seems transported directly from Moscow. The vibrant colors of its ceramic tiles and the glint of its six golden domes are a spectacular contrast to the surrounding Niçois architecture. Step inside to discover a richly decorated interior filled with intricate icons, murals, and woodwork, all crafted in Russia and brought to Nice. It's a national monument of France and a testament to the long-standing history of the Russian community on the Côte d'Azur. A visit offers a unique cultural and architectural experience.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located on Avenue Nicolas II, a short walk from the main train station (Gare de Nice-Ville).

Opening Hours: Daily, typically 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM, with a break for lunch. Check for service times.

Cost: Free entry, but donations are welcome. Modest dress code required.

Website: www.cathedrale-russe-nice.fr

Place Masséna

Place Masséna is the main square of Nice and a magnificent example of neo-classical urban design. Its distinctive features include the checkerboard black-and-white tiling, striking red ochre buildings, and the Fontaine du Soleil, a grand fountain featuring a large statue of Apollo. The square is a major hub, connecting the Old Town, the new town, and the Promenade des Anglais. Lining the square are high-end shops and galleries, and it's a frequent venue for public events, concerts, and festivals. At night, the square takes on a magical quality when the seven statues of the 'Conversation à Nice' art installation, representing the seven continents, are illuminated in changing colors.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Centrally located, at the intersection of Avenue Jean Médecin and Vieux Nice. Served by Tram Line 1 (Masséna stop).

Opening Hours: 24/7

Cost: Free

Website: www.nicetourisme.com

Musée d'Art Moderne et d'Art Contemporain (MAMAC)

For lovers of modern art, MAMAC is a must-visit. The museum's bold, tetrarch-shaped architecture is a landmark in itself, with four grey marble towers linked by glass walkways. The collection focuses on European New Realism and American Pop Art from the 1960s onwards, with significant works by artists like Yves Klein (a Nice native), Niki de Saint Phalle, and Andy Warhol. The real highlight is the panoramic roof terrace, which offers some of the best 360-degree views of the city, encompassing the Old Town, the sea, and the surrounding hills. The terrace itself is an art installation, making it a spectacular place to conclude your visit.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located near Place Garibaldi. Served by Tram Lines 1 and 2 (Garibaldi Le Château stop).

Opening Hours: 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM. Closed Mondays.

Cost: Around €10.

Website: www.mamac-nice.org

Villa Ephrussi de Rothschild

Perched atop the exclusive peninsula of Saint-Jean-Cap-Ferrat, this magnificent villa is a short trip from Nice but an unmissable experience. Built by Baroness Béatrice de Rothschild, the rose-colored palace is a masterpiece of Belle Époque architecture, filled with an eclectic collection of art, tapestries, and antique furniture. The true star, however, is the garden—or rather, gardens. The estate is surrounded by nine distinct themed gardens: French, Spanish, Japanese, Florentine, and more. The main French garden is designed to resemble the deck of a ship, with musical fountains that come to life every 20 minutes. It's a place of immense beauty and tranquility, offering stunning views over the bays of Villefranche and Beaulieu.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take bus number 15 from Nice. The journey takes about 30 minutes.

Opening Hours: Daily, 10:00 AM - 6:00 PM (until 7:00 PM in July/August).

Cost: Around €16

Website: www.villa-ephrussi.com

Port Lympia (Nice Port)

Just east of the Old Town and Castle Hill lies Port Lympia, Nice's historic port. Flanked by beautiful 18th-century arcaded buildings in shades of red and yellow ochre, it has a more local and relaxed feel than the glitzy marinas further down the coast. Here you'll find a mix of traditional fishing boats ('pointus'), sleek yachts, and ferries departing for Corsica. The port area is surrounded by excellent seafood restaurants and chic bars, making it a wonderful place for a meal or an evening drink while watching the boats. For a great view, walk along the quays or climb the steps towards Mont Boron. It's a picturesque and authentic slice of Niçois life.

Practical Information

How to Get There: A 15-minute walk from the Old Town, around the base of Castle Hill. Served by Tram Line 2 (Port Lympia stop).

Opening Hours: 24/7

Cost: Free to wander

Website: www.riviera-ports.com/port-de-nice

Mont Boron

For those who enjoy hiking and nature, the forested hill of Mont Boron offers a refreshing escape and unparalleled views. Located just east of the port, this 140-acre park is crisscrossed with walking trails that meander through Aleppo pines and olive groves. The main attraction is the panoramic viewpoint at the summit, which provides a stunning vista over the entire city of Nice on one side and the breathtaking peninsula of Cap-Ferrat on the other. At the top, you'll also find the historic Fort du Mont Alban, a 16th-century military fortress. While the fort itself has limited opening hours, the park is always accessible and is a favorite spot for locals to walk, jog, and picnic away from the city bustle.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Take bus number 33 from the city center, or prepare for a steep but rewarding walk from the port area.

Opening Hours: Park is always open.

Cost: Free

Website: www.nice.fr/fr/parcs-et-jardins/le-parc-du-mont-boron

Avenue Jean Médecin

Avenue Jean Médecin is the primary commercial artery of Nice, a bustling thoroughfare that runs from Place Masséna north towards the train station. If you're in the mood for shopping, this is the place to be. The wide avenue is lined with major international brands, French department stores like Galeries Lafayette, and the Nicetoile shopping center, which houses dozens of retailers and a food court. The street is mostly pedestrianized, with the tram line running down its center, making it easy and pleasant to navigate. It's more than just a shopping street; it's a vibrant showcase of modern Nice, buzzing with energy and showcasing beautiful Haussmannian architecture alongside contemporary buildings.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Centrally located, running north from Place Masséna. Tram Line 1 runs its entire length.

Opening Hours: Standard retail hours, typically 10:00 AM - 7:00 PM.

Cost: Free to browse

Website: www.nicetourisme.com

Cimiez Monastery and Gardens (Monastère de Cimiez)

A haven of peace and spirituality, the Cimiez Monastery is still occupied by Franciscan monks and has been a place of worship since the 16th century. The small church contains several important works of art, including three masterpieces by the Niçois painter Louis Bréa. The adjacent museum details the life and work of the Franciscans in Nice through frescoes, documents, and artifacts. However, the main draw for many visitors is the monastery's spectacular garden. Formerly the monks' vegetable patch, it has been transformed into a beautifully manicured garden offering one of the most lovely views in Nice, looking out over the Old Town and the sea. With its tidy flowerbeds, pergolas covered in climbing roses, and serene atmosphere, it's a perfect spot for quiet contemplation.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located in the Cimiez neighborhood. Take bus number 5.

Opening Hours: Church and Gardens: Daily, until around 6 PM. Museum: 10 AM-1 PM / 2 PM-6 PM, closed Sundays.

Cost: Free for the church and gardens. Small fee for the museum.

Website: www.nice.fr

Parc Phoenix

Located near the airport, Parc Phoenix is a sprawling 7-hectare botanical garden and zoo. It features one of Europe's largest greenhouses, 'Le Diamant Vert' (The Green Diamond), which recreates seven different tropical climates and is home to thousands of rare plant species, flamingos, iguanas, and a butterfly garden. The park is a fantastic destination for families or anyone with an interest in botany and wildlife. Outside the greenhouse, the park is beautifully landscaped with themed gardens, ponds filled with ducks and pelicans, and enclosures for animals like otters, wallabies, and porcupines. It offers a fun and educational day out, providing a different perspective on the natural beauty of the Riviera.

Practical Information

How to Get There: Located opposite the Nice Côte d'Azur Airport (Terminal 1). Take Tram Line 2 to the Parc Phoenix stop.

Opening Hours: 9:30 AM - 7:30 PM (Summer), 9:30 AM - 6:00 PM (Winter).

Cost: Around €5

Website: www.parc-phoenix.org

Planning Your Visit to Nice

Getting Around

Nice is a very walkable city, especially the central areas of Vieux Nice, Place Masséna, and the Promenade. For longer distances, the Lignes d'Azur public transport network is excellent, featuring modern trams and an extensive bus system. A single ticket allows transfers for 74 minutes. The Vélo Bleu bike-sharing system is another great option for exploring the flat coastal areas.

Where to Stay

For historic charm and atmosphere, stay in Vieux Nice (Old Town). For central convenience and shopping, choose the Jean-Médecin area near the main avenue. The Port Lympia neighborhood offers a trendy, more local vibe with great restaurants. For a quieter, upscale experience, consider the hilltop neighborhood of Cimiez. Hotels range from luxury icons like Le Negresco to charming boutique hotels and budget-friendly apartments.

Best Time to Visit

The best times to visit Nice are late spring (May-June) and early autumn (September-October). During these months, the weather is pleasantly warm, the sea is comfortable for swimming, and the summer crowds have thinned. July and August are the hottest and most crowded months, with peak season prices. Winter (November-February) is mild and quiet, offering a peaceful getaway, though some attractions may have shorter hours.

Suggested Itinerary

Day 1: Morning exploration of Vieux Nice and the Cours Saleya market. Afternoon ascent of Castle Hill for panoramic views, followed by a sunset stroll along the Promenade des Anglais. Day 2: Immerse yourself in art. Visit the Marc Chagall National Museum and the Musée Matisse in the Cimiez neighborhood, enjoying the peaceful monastery gardens in between. Day 3: Discover the Port Lympia, enjoy a seafood lunch, and then take a short bus ride to Villa Ephrussi de Rothschild in Saint-Jean-Cap-Ferrat for an afternoon in its magnificent gardens.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Nice expensive?
Nice can be expensive, but it caters to all budgets. Accommodation and seafront dining can be pricey, especially in high season. However, you can save money by eating at local spots in the Old Town (try socca and pissaladière for cheap eats), using public transport, and enjoying free activities like strolling the Promenade, relaxing on the public beaches (though renting a chair is an expense), and exploring parks like Castle Hill. Grocery shopping at local markets can also reduce costs if you're in self-catering accommodation.
How many days do you need in Nice?
A stay of 3 to 4 days is ideal to experience the main highlights of Nice itself without feeling rushed. This gives you enough time to explore the Old Town, visit key museums, relax on the beach, and soak up the atmosphere. If you plan to use Nice as a base to explore the wider Côte d'Azur (e.g., Monaco, Cannes, Èze, Saint-Paul de Vence), you should plan for at least a week.
Is Nice safe?
Nice is generally a safe city for tourists. However, like any major tourist destination, it's wise to take precautions. Be aware of your surroundings and watch out for pickpockets, especially in crowded areas like the tram, train station, and busy markets. Avoid leaving valuables unattended on the beach. At night, stick to well-lit main streets. Overall, by using common sense, you are very likely to have a safe and trouble-free visit.
What is Nice famous for?
Nice is famous for several iconic things. Firstly, its stunning location on the French Riviera and the breathtaking turquoise water of the Baie des Anges. It's renowned for the Promenade des Anglais, its long pebble beach, and its charming, Italian-influenced Old Town (Vieux Nice). The city is also a major center for art, celebrated for its museums dedicated to Matisse and Chagall. Finally, it's famous for its unique Niçoise cuisine, including dishes like Salade Niçoise, socca (chickpea pancake), pissaladière (onion tart), and ratatouille.
